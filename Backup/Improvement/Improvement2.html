Self-Work

I'm describing the process of self-work.

  • It is essential for Christians to take care of our bodies. I'm calling this external work. It includes sleep, diet, and fitness.
  • It is essential for Christians to improve our minds. I'm calling this internal work. It includes character, communication, and learning.
  • It is essential for Christians to work on relationships. I'm calling this outside work. It includes friendship, romance, and family.

External Work

Sleep

Good sleep is a skill that can be developed. What we do during the day will determine what time we fall asleep, how fast we fall asleep, how well we stay asleep, and how restful that sleep is.

Quality sleep is essential for physical recovery, cognitive function, and emotional regulation, supporting muscle growth, memory consolidation, and mood stability. It boosts the immune system by promoting cytokine production, which helps fight infections and inflammation, while sleep deprivation weakens immunity. Sleeping also regulates appetite hormones, affecting metabolic health, with poor sleep linked to weight gain and an increased risk of metabolic disorders like diabetes.

Levels of cortisol rise in the morning to help promote wakefulness and decrease throughout the day. They are typically highest around 30-45 minutes after waking, a phenomenon known as the "cortisol awakening response" (CAR). This peak helps promote wakefulness and prepare the body for the day.

Prolonged stress can lead to consistently elevated cortisol levels, which may disrupt sleep by making it harder to fall or stay asleep. Elevated cortisol levels, particularly at night, are often found in individuals with insomnia. High nighttime cortisol can interfere with the deep sleep stages, leading to poor sleep quality and increased fatigue.

Stress management techniques such as such as mindfulness meditation and regular exercise can help lower cortisol levels and improve sleep quality. Going to bed and waking up at the same time each day, consuming a balanced diet with adequate nutrients, and reducing caffeine and nicotine intake in the early afternoon and evening also supports adrenal health and cortisol regulation, improving sleep quality.

Adenosine promotes feelings of drowsiness and the need for sleep. After a long and restful sleeping period, adenosine levels will be very low. However, adenosine levels gradually increase in the brain during wakefulness as a byproduct of cellular metabolism, particularly ATP (adenosine triphosphate) breakdown.

Caffeine works against adenosine by binding to adenosine receptors and taking their place, blocking the sleeping signal. A safe guideline is to not drink caffeine after 4 pm to avoid trouble falling asleep.

Light is the most powerful external cue (zeitgeber) that synchronizes the circadian rhythm with the external environment. The bodies central biological clock is located in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). The SCN coordinates the timing of the circadian rhythm by receiving direct input from the eyes about light levels. Special photoreceptor cells in the retina contain melanopsin, which detects changes in ambient light and sends signals to the SCN to adjust the timing of the circadian rhythm.

Meal timing, work schedules, environmental temperature fluctuations, and social interactions can also act as zeitgebers, influencing the body's internal clock, albeit less significantly than light.

Exposure to light suppresses the production of melatonin and promotes wakefulness through increased alertness and cognitive function. Getting anywhere between 10 to 30 minutes of sunlight (depending on how cloudy the day is) relatively soon after waking positively informs your circadian rhythm and enhances wakefulness.

In the absence of light, melatonin production increases, promoting sleepiness and preparing the body for rest. Being outside as the sun is setting is another way to inform your body that it is time to go to sleep. Limit screen usage and bright lights in the evening and night times to support this process. Your bodies natural melatonin can wear off if you stay awake through the tiredness, which can make it hard to fall asleep afterwards.

Body temperature follows a circadian pattern, peaking during the day and reaching its lowest point in the early morning hours, coinciding with the propensity for sleep. A cold bedroom for sleeping can positively impact ones ability to fall asleep.

Traveling across time zones can desynchronize the internal clock from the external environment, leading to symptoms like fatigue, insomnia, and digestive issues. It typically takes several days for the circadian rhythm to realign with the new time zone. Use the external factors mentioned above to help reset your bodies internal clock.
