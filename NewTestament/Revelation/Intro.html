Revelation Commentary

Author

Traditionally, the author is identified as John the Apostle, the son of Zebedee. This attribution is supported by early Christian testimonies and internal textual clues.

Date

The Book of Revelation, also known as the Apocalypse, is widely believed to have been written in the late first century, around 95 AD. This dating is based on historical and textual evidence suggesting it was composed during the reign of Emperor Domitian, a period marked by significant persecution of Christians. Irenaeous records that "him who beheld the apocalyptic vision" saw it "almost in our day, towards the end of Domitian's reign."1 Sweet provides a balanced conclusion: "To sum up, the earlier date may be right, but the internal evidence is not sufficient to outweigh the firm tradition stemming from Irenaeus."2

Purpose

"This is the Revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show to his servants the things which must happen soon, which he sent and made known by his angel to his servant, John, who testified to God's word of the testimony of Jesus Christ, about everything that he saw. Blessed is he who reads and those who hear the words of the prophecy, and keep the things that are written in it, for the time is near." (Rev. 1:1-3). The primary purpose of Revelation is to provide hope and encouragement to Christians facing persecution, by affirming God's ultimate sovereignty and the eventual triumph of good over evil. Revelation calls followers of Jesus to resist idolatry, to worship God alone, and to patiently endure suffering knowing that God will ultimately judge evil and redeem creation.

Interpretation

There are four main problems to work through when it comes to interpreting Revelation aright: What do the symbols represent? What is the structure of the book? How does John make use of the OT? Which school of thought, historicist, preterist, idealist, or futurist, should we follow?

Symbolism

Revelation is rich in symbolic and allegorical imagery, drawing extensively on Old Testament motifs. The symbols represent various spiritual and historical realities, often conveying messages about the struggle between God and evil, the perseverance of the faithful, and the final judgment and redemption of creation. The use of symbols was also a way to communicate messages covertly in a time of persecution.

Structure

The structure of Revelation is both linear and cyclical, containing a series of seven visions that John records. The structure of Revelation incorporates imagery and themes from Old Testament prophetic books like Daniel, Ezekiel, and Zechariah. John creatively weaves these allusions to Israel’s Scriptures to craft a rich tapestry of poetry, prophecy, and apocalyptic literature that points to Jesus as the fulfillment of all God’s promises. The structure is designed to convey a sense of unfolding divine revelation.

Old Testament

John heavily references and alludes to the Old Testament, using its imagery, language, and themes. This not only roots Revelation in the Jewish prophetic tradition but also provides a rich interpretive context for understanding its symbolism and message. The Old Testament should be the primary means for interpreting Revelation.

School's of Thought

Revelation is a timeless depiction of the cosmic struggle between good and evil, rather than a linear prediction of future events. This idealist perspective focuses on the theological and spiritual truths of the book, applicable to all Christians across time, rather than tying its fulfillment to specific historical events or future predictions. This contrasts with the historicist, preterist, and futurist schools of thought, which interpret Revelation as primarily relating to specific historical events, first-century occurrences, or future prophetic fulfillment, respectively.
