Introduction

Author

It is important to recognize the fact that the author of the Gospel of John was an eyewitness to the events he records. The author's identity as John the apostle, the son of Zebedee, is virtually unainmous in the early tradition.1 The title, “The Gospel According to John,” was likely used from the time it began being circulated because there are no extant manuscripts with other names.2 If instead the gospel was originally published anonymously as some claim, this is in distinction from the apocryphal gospels which were originally published with false names.3 And as Criag Keener notes, "there appears to be unanimity regarding the authorship of the four Gospels, suggesting the traditions are early."4

Internal evidence: The “disciple whom Jesus loves” (Jn 13:23; 19:26; 20:2; 21:20) is “the disciple who testifies about these things, and wrote these things.” (Jn 21:24). This disciple “whom Jesus loved, was at the table, leaning against Jesus’ breast” at the Last Supper (Jn 13:23). The Synoptics only mention the disciples and Jesus at this celebration (Mk 14:12-26; Mat 26:17-30; Lk 22:7-23). He is standing by the cross when Jesus says, “Woman, behold your son!” thus commissioning him to care of Mary (Jn 19:27). He is also the one that raced Peter to look inside the empty tomb (Jn 20:2-8). It should be clear from these verses that the author was not only an eyewitness but one of the Twelve. The most notable objection against the internal evidence is that John couldn’t have written such fluent Greek, but D. A. Carson writes that “the argument no longer stands” pointing out that John had years to learn how to write, John's Greek “is not elegant,” and he mostly uses the language of the Septuagint (the Greek translation of the OT).5

External evidence: There is a strong tradition that the apostle John was the author of this Gospel that has been accepted by nearly all throughout church history. Irenaeus, in about 180 AD, writes, “Afterwards, John, the disciple of the Lord, who also had leaned upon His breast, did himself publish a Gospel during his residence at Ephesus in Asia” (Against Heresies, 3.1.1). Irenaeus was a student of Polycarp who was a disciple of John the apostle, so his testimony is particularly strong. Clement of Alexandria (150-215 AD), who's testimony is saved by Eusebius, says, "But, last of all, John, perceiving that the external facts had been made plain in the Gospel...composed a spiritual Gospel" (Church History, 6.14.7). The strongest argument against that claim comes from the dating of the book which will be examined in the next section.

Comparison to the Synoptics

The Gospel according to John stands out from the Synoptics (Matthew, Mark, and Luke) in many ways, providing a unique perspective on the life, ministry, and teachings of Jesus. John’s style is highly poetic and symbolic. John's symbolism includes images such as light and darkness, bread and wine, vine and branches, and shepherd and sheep which provide memorable pictures that convey deep spiritual truths about Jesus and his mission. John’s Gospel is structured thematically around seven signs or miracles that continue to emphasize theological truths, especially of Jesus’ divinity.
