God is Simple

Divine simplicity posits that God is without parts, indivisible, and unified. It contrasts with the idea of God being composite rather than complex. God's simplicity means He has no composition or parts, including physical parts, form and matter, essence, and attributes. This doctrine affirms that God cannot be divided, and His attributes are not separable entities or properties like physical characteristics.

While simplicity implies no parts or divisions, it does not exclude the possibility of distinguishable aspects in God's nature, such as the attributes or persons of the Trinity. Biblical passages reveal God’s essence through various attributes (e.g., God is spirit, light, and love). Despite the plurality of attributes, there is a profound unity in God’s nature, suggesting a complex but unified essence. Everything in God is God, and there is nothing in God that does not include all His attributes. For example, God’s love is inherently holy, His knowledge unchanging.

Scriptural references, such as Deuteronomy 4:15-16, John 4:24, Luke 24:39, and Exodus 3:14, along with traditional theological interpretations, support the notion of God’s simplicity. This doctrine is linked to divine independence, infinity, and creation. The biblical approach to God's simplicity emphasizes His personal relationship with us, contrasting with the abstract nature of scholastic philosophy. God relates to us as a whole person, not just as a collection of attributes.

Implications

If God were composed of parts, He would depend on those parts, contradicting His nature as the most fundamental being. Divine simplicity implies that all divine attributes are essentially one and identical with His essence.

It implies God’s immutability (unchangeability), omnipotence (all-powerful), and omniscience (all-knowing). This doctrine also suggests that God, being simple, does not change over time or space, is self-existent (aseity), and is always in a state of pure action (pure act). This steers us away from envisioning God as a being possessing qualities in the way creatures do, and instead invites us to contemplate a God who, in His very nature, is the embodiment of all His attributes.

It is especially relevant in understanding the nature of the Trinity, suggesting that each Person of the Trinity encompasses the whole divine essence. It emphasizes that each person of the Trinity (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) is fully and completely God, not a fractional part of God. The incarnation of Christ does not contradict divine simplicity. While Jesus Christ, in His human nature, experienced change, His divine nature did not change.
