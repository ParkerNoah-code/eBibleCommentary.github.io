Guarding Against Doctrinal Error Without Overstepping

A well-functioning immune system is essential for physical health, but it must maintain balance—neither underactive nor overactive. Similarly, in Christian communities, addressing doctrinal errors requires a balanced approach. Failing to guard against false teachings leaves believers vulnerable to spiritual harm, as false teachers and unchecked errors can mislead many (Matthew 24:11). Church leaders bear a solemn responsibility to protect their congregations from these dangers (Revelation 2:20). However, an overzealous approach that labels minor disagreements as heresy can cause significant harm, likened to an overactive immune response that damages healthy tissue. Such false accusations disrupt unity, harm individuals, and weaken the body of Christ.

Accusing someone of heresy is a serious matter and should be reserved for views that contradict essential Christian doctrines, such as the deity of Christ or the truth of the resurrection. Many secondary issues, like millennial views or debates on Calvinism versus Arminianism, though important, do not constitute heresy. Engaging in respectful dialogue over these differences can strengthen understanding and unity. However, when baseless accusations of heresy are made, they not only harm the accused but also fracture relationships and create unnecessary divisions within the church. Slander, including false accusations, is a grave sin condemned in Scripture (Ephesians 4:31, Colossians 3:8).

Christians are called to discernment and humility. While we must courageously guard against true heresy, we must also approach disagreements with care and avoid careless words that lead to division (Matthew 12:36). Accusations must be weighed carefully, ensuring they are rooted in truth and aimed at building up, not tearing down, the body of Christ. Striking this balance ensures that we protect the church from both harmful teaching and the damage caused by overzealous gatekeeping.

The True Meaning of Death in Scripture

A pervasive teaching within Christendom suggests that "death" as described in the Bible doesn’t mean literal cessation of life but rather "separation from God." This interpretation is at odds with both the plain meaning of the biblical text and the linguistic evidence. In Romans 6:23, Paul plainly states, “The wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.” The Greek word for death, thanatos, unequivocally refers to the absence of life or the natural end of existence. Yet, proponents of eternal torment redefine it to mean a perpetual state of conscious separation. This inversion of meaning turns death into its opposite—never-ending life in torment—distorting the straightforward message of Scripture.

The Bible consistently contrasts death and eternal life. If "eternal life" is taken literally as an unending gift to the righteous, "death" must equally be understood literally as the punishment for sin. The use of thanatos aligns with its common meaning throughout Scripture, such as in Acts 23:29, where it refers to physical execution. Biblical language reinforces this interpretation, with additional words like “perish,” “destroy,” and “consume” further underscoring the finality of death as the cessation of life. Extending this meaning to the second death described in Revelation 20:14—a destruction of both soul and body—solidifies the literal understanding of death as total annihilation, not ongoing existence.

Attempts to redefine death as "separation" lack scriptural support and rely on adding to God’s Word, a practice explicitly condemned (Deuteronomy 4:2, Revelation 22:18-19). While spiritual death—the state of being disconnected from God—is a present reality for non-believers, it is distinct from the second death, which entails final destruction. God's consistent use of terms like "die," "destruction," and "consume" leaves no ambiguity about the nature of the second death. The biblical evidence compels us to reject theories that twist death into a euphemism for eternal life in torment, reaffirming that death in Scripture means exactly what it says: the end of life.

What is Conditional Immortality and Why It Matters

The debate over the afterlife in Christianity often suffers from unclear terminology, with traditionalism, universalism, and conditionalism as the key positions. Traditionalism, suggesting eternal torment, is historically nuanced and often misunderstood. Universalism, implying ultimate reconciliation, can be conflated with inclusivism. Conditionalism, short for Conditional Immortality (CI), is sometimes narrowly interpreted as a view on human mortality rather than addressing ultimate destinies. Despite these challenges, the term "Conditional Immortality" is not only accurate but also deeply rooted in theological tradition and eschatological clarity.

Conditional Immortality holds that eternal life is a gift granted by God to the saved, while the unsaved face the second death—a permanent end to existence. This doctrine underscores that life is provisional and dependent on God’s judgment, contrasting eternal life for believers with the irreversible annihilation of the wicked. CI emphasizes the biblical teaching that immortality is not inherent to humanity but is bestowed conditionally based on salvation. This framework aligns with passages like Romans 6:23, which contrasts eternal life as a gift with death as the wages of sin, highlighting the permanence of these outcomes.

CI avoids confusion with anthropological debates over the nature of the soul. While acknowledging the possibility of a conscious intermediate state after death, CI primarily focuses on ultimate eschatological outcomes. It integrates the concept of final reward with final punishment, presenting a coherent vision of eternal judgment and cosmic restoration. By emphasizing both the annihilation of evil and the eternal life of the redeemed, CI provides a balanced theological perspective. This comprehensive approach makes Conditional Immortality not just a doctrinal stance but a profound articulation of God’s justice and mercy in the ultimate destiny of humanity.

Conditional Immortality: Historical Roots and Significance

Conditional immortality, the view that eternal life is a gift dependent on meeting specific conditions, has historical roots that date back to early Christian theology. Early church fathers like Theophilus of Antioch and Ephrem the Syrian articulated the idea that Adam and humanity were created neither strictly mortal nor immortal but conditionally immortal. This meant that immortality was achievable through obedience to God but forfeitable through disobedience. These theological perspectives underscore the provisional nature of life and its dependence on divine grace, as seen in Genesis with the conditional access to the Tree of Life.

Athanasius further refined this concept, describing humanity’s mortality as natural, given their creation from nothing, and immortality as a grace contingent on obedience. His writings connect protology (the study of origins) with eschatology (the study of end times), suggesting that while humanity was initially mortal, they could attain immortality through faith and obedience. Similarly, Augustine’s influential theology emphasized Adam’s original state as capable of both immortality and mortality, dependent on his free will. Augustine’s framing of Adam’s conditional state became foundational, though his interpretation of universal immortality for all souls—whether saved or damned—diverged from the annihilationist view associated with conditional immortality.

The label "conditional immortality" has been consistently used throughout theological history to capture the notion of life as a divine gift contingent upon righteousness. Writers like Philip Schaff and Benjamin Kennicott affirmed this term to describe Adam’s state before the Fall and humanity’s eligibility for eternal life through obedience. This historical perspective underscores that conditional immortality is not a modern innovation but a doctrine deeply embedded in Christian thought, bridging the theological discussions of creation, judgment, and salvation. It emphasizes life’s provisional nature and highlights the transformative promise of resurrection and eternal life for the faithful.

A Definitive Doctrine for Eschatology

Conditional immortality, the belief that eternal life is contingent upon fulfilling divine conditions, offers a compelling framework for understanding salvation and damnation. Historically rooted in Christian theology, it asserts that immortality is not inherent to all humanity but a gift selectively granted to the redeemed. This doctrine serves as both a positive assertion—that eternal life depends on meeting specific conditions—and a denial of universal immortality, challenging traditionalist and universalist perspectives that suggest all people live forever.

The term “conditional immortality” carries a dual meaning. First, it signifies that eternal life is not guaranteed but depends on conditions tied to righteousness and faith. Second, it implies a limitation: not everyone attains immortality. This contrasts with views that universalize immortality, either by natural extension or divine decree. Theologically, this distinction underscores the redemptive work of Christ, whose obedience remedies Adam’s disobedience. Where Adam’s sin brought death, Christ’s victory ensures eternal life for those who meet the conditions of faith and repentance.

In today’s eschatological context, conditional immortality integrates doctrines of salvation and damnation. The saved are granted eternal life as a divine gift, while the unsaved face annihilation—a permanent cessation of existence. This approach aligns with scriptural narratives emphasizing that life is provisional and conditional upon God’s will. By denying universal immortality and affirming that everlasting life is exclusive to the redeemed, conditional immortality provides a coherent and biblically grounded view of humanity’s ultimate destiny. It encapsulates the gospel’s promise: eternal life for those who seek God, and the finality of death for those who reject Him.

Why Annihilationism Is Biblical

Annihilationism posits that eternal life is a gift from God granted only to the saved, while the unsaved face permanent destruction. This doctrine stands in contrast to the traditional view of eternal torment in hell. Annihilationists argue that the Bible does not teach inherent immortality for all humanity but instead presents a consistent message: immortality is conditional and reserved for the redeemed. For the unsaved, the consequence of sin is not eternal suffering but ultimate death, a cessation of existence.

The case for annihilationism rests on three main arguments. First, the Bible teaches that immortality is a gift, not a universal trait. From Genesis, where access to the Tree of Life is restricted, to New Testament passages like 1 Corinthians 15, the theme is clear: immortality is restored through Christ alone. Second, the biblical vision of eternity depicts a world free of evil, where God's enemies are eradicated, not perpetually sustained in torment. Passages such as 1 Corinthians 15:24-28 and Ephesians 1:10 emphasize the ultimate victory of God, leaving no room for a dualistic existence of eternal suffering alongside eternal joy. Third, the language of destruction used in the Bible—terms like “perish,” “destroy,” and “consume”—consistently conveys the finality of judgment, aligning with the annihilationist view.

Traditional arguments for eternal torment often hinge on specific passages, such as Revelation 14:9-11 and 20:10, but annihilationists contend that these texts, when understood in their symbolic and biblical contexts, support the view of total destruction rather than eternal suffering. The annihilationist interpretation aligns with the broader biblical narrative, which emphasizes God’s justice, mercy, and the restoration of creation without the perpetual existence of evil. This perspective challenges believers to reconsider entrenched views and explore what Scripture truly teaches about humanity’s ultimate destiny.

A Hermeneutical Defense of Conditionalism

Edward Fudge's groundbreaking work, The Fire That Consumes, sparked debate over conditionalism—the view that immortality is a gift granted only to the saved, while the lost face annihilation rather than eternal torment. Critics like Robert Peterson have challenged the strength of Fudge's hermeneutical methodology. However, a closer examination reveals that Fudge’s approach, which centers on thorough exegesis and reliance on biblical coherence, provides a compelling case for conditional immortality. This perspective not only aligns with scriptural themes but also challenges traditional notions of eternal torment.

One cornerstone of conditionalism is its emphasis on the telos of the gospel, which promises life from death rather than eternal suffering. Paul’s epistles, notably Romans 6:23, juxtapose death as the wages of sin with eternal life as God’s gift through Christ. Traditional interpretations that equate death with separation from God often stretch semantic boundaries, whereas conditionalism interprets death as literal and final—a cessation of existence. This focus is further supported by John 3:16, where perishing is contrasted with eternal life, underscoring the gospel’s promise of life rather than extended punishment.

Moreover, conditionalism asserts that immortality is not inherent to all humans but is a divine gift granted exclusively to the saved. Genesis 3 and texts like 1 Corinthians 15 emphasize the transient nature of human life apart from God’s intervention. The annihilation of the wicked is depicted consistently throughout scripture, with imagery of destruction—such as the flood and Sodom’s fiery end—providing templates for final judgment. By examining these texts alongside apocalyptic symbols in Revelation, Fudge and his defenders highlight a hermeneutic grounded in scriptural coherence rather than tradition. Conditionalism thus offers a biblically faithful alternative to the doctrine of eternal torment, asserting that the unsaved will not endure immortal suffering but instead face a final, irreversible death.

Chris Date, The Hermeneutics of Conditionalism: A Defense of the Interpretive Method of Edward Fudge.

The Final End of the Wicked

The concept of eternal punishment for the wicked has long been a cornerstone of traditional Christian theology. However, recent scholarship, including Edward Fudge's The Fire That Consumes, argues that the Bible portrays a different fate: the ultimate and total extinction of the wicked. Rooted in both Old and New Testament teachings, this view challenges the idea of unending torment, suggesting instead that the wicked will face irreversible destruction as their "eternal punishment."

The Old Testament consistently uses vivid imagery to describe the fate of the wicked, employing metaphors of destruction such as ash underfoot, smoke that vanishes, and fire that consumes. These word-pictures uniformly depict annihilation rather than perpetual suffering. Similarly, New Testament language builds on this foundation, presenting "eternal punishment" as the outcome of divine judgment—final and irrevocable death, rather than endless agony. The phrase "unquenchable fire," for instance, signifies destruction that cannot be resisted, not unending torment.

Moreover, the term "eternal" in biblical contexts often refers to the permanence of an outcome, not the duration of an act. Just as "eternal redemption" speaks to the lasting effect of Christ's work, "eternal punishment" reflects the finality of the wicked's destruction. This interpretation aligns with the broader scriptural narrative, which contrasts the gift of eternal life for the saved with the complete and everlasting end of the wicked. As Fudge concludes, the Bible ultimately teaches not eternal suffering, but eternal extinction for those who reject God.

Understanding Conditional Immortality

Conditional immortality is a doctrine rooted in the Bible, asserting that eternal life is granted only to those who meet specific conditions. This belief is evident throughout Scripture, where eternal life is described as a gift contingent upon faith in Jesus Christ. For instance, verses like John 3:16 and Romans 2:7 reveal that immortality is not an inherent human trait but a gracious promise extended to those who believe in Christ. This doctrine trains us to think in patterns aligned with God’s Word, encouraging us to view eternal life as both a divine gift and a responsibility.

The Bible presents this condition in two ways: direct faith in Jesus and the evidence of that faith through a transformed life. Passages like John 6:47 emphasize the central role of belief in Jesus, while others like Romans 5:21 highlight the fruits of such faith, manifested in righteous living. In both cases, the condition of faith underpins the promise of immortality. This perspective challenges the notion that all humans, righteous or unrighteous, possess eternal life inherently, instead framing it as a gift reserved for those who align their lives with God’s will.

The doctrine of conditional immortality also brings clarity to the fate of the unrighteous. While the Bible consistently offers eternal life to believers, it equally warns of the consequences for those who reject Christ. Contrary to the common belief in unconditional immortality for all, Scripture reveals that the unrighteous will face ultimate destruction rather than eternal torment. This consistency underscores the inspired nature of the Bible, teaching that eternal life is a grace-filled promise for the faithful, not an automatic guarantee for all. Understanding this doctrine invites believers to align their beliefs and language with Scripture’s consistent teaching, reinforcing the transformative power of faith in Christ.

Terrance Tiessen's Journey to Annihilationism

The journey to embracing annihilationism—the belief that the wicked face ultimate destruction rather than eternal torment—can be long and fraught with challenges, as I discovered through my own experience. Reflecting on my hesitancy, I realized it stemmed from a combination of intellectual paradigms, the influence of traditional theological frameworks, and a lack of exposure to robust arguments for annihilationism in the works I deeply respected.

One significant hurdle was the difficulty of shifting paradigms. As Thomas Kuhn explains, people hold onto their intellectual frameworks until anomalies accumulate to the point that their existing models can no longer accommodate them. This resistance was evident in my reliance on influential theologians like Millard Erickson, Anthony Hoekema, Hans Schwarz, and Donald Bloesch, whose works largely reinforced traditional beliefs in eternal conscious torment. Their arguments, while well-reasoned, often overlooked or dismissed annihilationism outright, leaving me little reason to question the status quo. Even Bloesch, the most open among them, only acknowledged annihilationism in passing, offering minimal encouragement to consider it seriously.

Ultimately, my shift required more than just encountering biblical arguments for annihilationism, such as those presented by Edward Fudge. It demanded an inner transformation—a theological conversion akin to spiritual awakening—fueled by the Spirit’s work alongside my engagement with Scripture. This process reminds us that spreading the message of conditional immortality isn’t just about presenting compelling evidence. It also requires patience and humility, recognizing that such shifts often occur through the combined power of God’s Word and His Spirit at work in the hearts and minds of believers.
