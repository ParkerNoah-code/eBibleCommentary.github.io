Old Testament

Kingdom

When Jesus proclaimed that "the kingdom of God is at hand" (Mark 1:15), He announced the fulfillment of Old Testament eschatological hopes. His statement assumes a rich theological background familiar to His audience, grounded in the Old Testament’s portrayal of God's reign.

The Old Testament believer lived with an awareness of these eschatological promises but without full clarity regarding their temporal sequence or ultimate fulfillment. The prophets themselves sought to understand the timing and nature of these events, as Peter explains in 1 Peter 1:10–12. They recognized that their messages pointed beyond their own era to a future time when God’s redemptive plan would be revealed in its fullness.

Jesus’ ministry revealed the inaugurated nature of the kingdom—God’s rule breaking into history in a new and decisive way through His person and work. The Old Testament does not use the exact phrase "kingdom of God," but the theme is pervasive in its portrayal of God as the sovereign King over Israel and the nations. The inauguration of this kingdom anticipates its full consummation at the end of the age, aligning with the Old Testament's eschatological vision.

King

The Old Testament explicitly identifies God as King both in His covenant relationship with Israel and His universal sovereignty over creation. Passages such as Exodus 15:18 ("The Lord will reign forever and ever") and Isaiah 43:15 ("I am the Lord, your Holy One, the Creator of Israel, your King") emphasize God’s kingship over His chosen people. Similarly, texts like Psalm 97:1 ("The Lord reigns, let the earth rejoice!") and Daniel 4:34-35 portray God as the King of all creation, sovereign over history and the nations.

The term malḵûṯ, often translated as "kingdom," primarily refers to God's reign or rule rather than a physical domain. This concept includes both God’s present rule over all creation and His eschatological reign, which will be fully realized in the future.

Eschatological Hope

George Eldon Ladd’s framework, as outlined in The Presence of the Future, offers a helpful lens for understanding the eschatological hope of the Old Testament.

Dynamic Hope: The Old Testament presents the kingdom of God as dynamic, emphasizing God's active rule and reign. Passages like Psalm 145:11-13 highlight the anticipation of God extending His dominion over all creation. This hope is not static or confined but anticipates God’s redemptive work in history culminating in His ultimate reign.

Eschatological Hope: As Israel increasingly turned away from God, the prophets articulated an eschatological hope—a future intervention by God to redeem and judge. This hope finds expression in passages such as Isaiah 2:2-4, which envisions a time when all nations will come to Zion to learn God's ways, and Joel 3:14-16, which speaks of the "Day of the Lord" as a time of cosmic upheaval and divine judgment. The prophetic vision points to a future inbreaking of God's kingdom into history, bringing it to its ordained consummation.

Earthly Hope: The eschatological vision of the Old Testament is grounded in the physical world. Isaiah 11:6-9, for example, portrays a transformed creation where peace and righteousness reign, and Isaiah 65:17-25 anticipates "new heavens and a new earth." The promise is not an escape from the material world but its renewal and restoration under God’s reign.

Historically Oriented Hope: The prophetic writings often intermingle immediate historical fulfillments with ultimate eschatological events. This phenomenon is sometimes referred to as "prophetic telescoping." For instance, the "Day of the Lord" frequently combines near-term judgments with ultimate eschatological realities.

Ethical Hope: The eschatological hope of the Old Testament is inherently ethical. God’s kingdom is not merely about power and dominion but about righteousness and justice. The prophets repeatedly call Israel to repentance and faithfulness, emphasizing that participation in the blessings of God's kingdom requires ethical conformity to His covenant (e.g., Amos 5:24; Micah 6:8). Judgment awaits those who persist in sin and rebellion, while the faithful are promised redemption and restoration.

Messianic Hope: This is an addition to Ladd's framework. Integral to the Old Testament concept of the kingdom of God is the messianic hope, which encapsulates Israel’s expectation of a divinely anointed ruler who would establish God’s ultimate reign on earth. The next section is devoted to this.

Messianic Dimension

This hope is woven throughout the fabric of Old Testament prophecy and theology and serves as a cornerstone for understanding the kingdom of God as fulfilled in the person and work of Jesus Christ. Geerhardus Vos provides a helpful framework for understanding the key aspects of this messianic vision in The Self-Disclosure of Jesus, highlighting five essential components:

The Imposition of Divine Rule: The Old Testament portrays the Messiah as the ultimate ruler whose dominion is imposed from above and demands absolute submission. This is evident in passages like Genesis 49:10, which declares, "The scepter shall not depart from Judah, nor the ruler’s staff from between his feet, until tribute comes to him; and to him shall be the obedience of the peoples," and Numbers 24:17–19, which prophesies a star and scepter arising from Israel to crush the enemies of God. The Messiah would be God’s chosen King whose rule is not negotiated but sovereignly decreed.

Eschatological Consummation: The messianic hope is profoundly eschatological, envisioning the Messiah as the "great final King" who ushers in the consummation of history. This new world order is characterized by a decisive and catastrophic divine intervention, breaking into the current age and inaugurating an eternal kingdom. Passages such as Isaiah 9:2–7 describe the Messiah as the one whose government and peace will have no end, reigning on David’s throne forever. Similarly, Daniel 2:44 speaks of a kingdom set up by God that will crush all others and endure eternally, while Daniel 7:13–14 presents the Son of Man receiving an everlasting dominion. This eschatological vision places the Messiah at the center of God’s redemptive plan, standing as the fulcrum of history.

Supernatural Transformation: Inseparable from its eschatological nature, the messianic hope is suffused with the supernatural. It anticipates a divinely orchestrated renewal of the created order, bringing about a return to the paradisiacal conditions of Eden. Isaiah 11:1–9 portrays the Messiah as ushering in an era of perfect peace and harmony, where natural enmity is abolished, and the knowledge of the Lord fills the earth. Isaiah 65:17–25 similarly describes the creation of "new heavens and a new earth," a world fundamentally different in quality from the present one, characterized by divine blessing and eternal joy. This supernatural dimension underscores the Messiah’s role as the agent of a cosmic renewal that reverses the effects of the Fall.

Soteric Deliverance: Central to the messianic hope is its soteric (salvific) component, in which the Messiah delivers God’s people from judgment and ushers them into the blessings of the new world to come. This deliverance is both spiritual and martial, involving the defeat of sin and evil as well as the restoration of God’s people. Isaiah 9:4–5 speaks of the Messiah breaking the yoke of oppression, while Isaiah 11:1–16 emphasizes his role as a righteous judge who delivers the meek and strikes down the wicked. Micah 5:4–5a portrays the Messiah as a shepherd providing security and peace, while Zechariah 9:9–10 highlights his humble yet victorious reign, bringing salvation to his people. This dual emphasis on spiritual renewal and physical deliverance reflects the Messiah’s comprehensive role in bringing about God’s kingdom.

The Messiah’s Religious and Divine Status: Finally, the messianic hope includes a uniquely religious dimension, with the Messiah occupying a mediatory position between God and humanity. The Messiah is identified with God Himself and is therefore right to receive worship. Psalm 2:8–12 presents the Messiah as God’s Son, receiving homage from the nations, while Psalm 110 portrays him as both King and Priest, seated at God’s right hand. Isaiah 9:6 assigns divine titles to the Messiah, including "Mighty God" and "Everlasting Father," affirming His divine nature and eternal reign. These passages reveal the Messiah as not only a human agent but also a divine figure, central to the worship and adoration of God’s people.

Eschatological Outlook

The eschatological vision of the Old Testament, though not fully developed in its temporal sequence or scope, presents a rich tapestry of revelation regarding God’s ultimate purposes for humanity and creation. Anthony Hoekema outlines seven key themes that embody this outlook in The Bible and the Future, all of which are deeply rooted in Scripture and form the foundation for the fuller revelation found in the New Testament.

The Expectation of the Coming Redeemer: The promise of a Redeemer unfolds progressively through the Old Testament, beginning with the protoevangelium of Genesis 3:15, where the “seed of the woman” is foretold to crush the serpent's head. This initial promise finds further specification as the Redeemer is revealed to be the “seed of Abraham” (Gen 22:18), through whom all nations would be blessed, a descendant of the tribe of Judah (Gen 49:10), and ultimately a son of David (2 Sam 7:12–13) whose kingdom would endure forever.

This Redeemer would uniquely fulfill multiple offices:

  • Prophet: Deuteronomy 18:15 foretells a prophet like Moses who would mediate God’s word to His people.
  • Priest: Psalm 110:4 declares the Messiah to be a priest forever in the order of Melchizedek.
  • King: Zechariah 9:9 describes the Messiah as a humble yet victorious king.
  • Suffering Servant: Isaiah 42:1–4; 49:5–7; and 52:13–53:12 reveal the Messiah as one who would suffer to atone for the sins of God’s people.
  • Son of Man: Daniel 7:13–14 portrays Him as a divine figure, given dominion and glory over all nations.

These messianic prophecies paint a composite portrait of a Redeemer who is both human and divine, who would bring salvation and establish God’s eternal kingdom.

The Anticipation of the Kingdom of God: The Old Testament prophets anticipated the day when God’s rule would be fully manifest, not only among Israel but throughout the entire world. Daniel 2:44–45 depicts this kingdom as a divine, everlasting reign that would crush all earthly kingdoms. This kingdom would embody justice, righteousness, and peace, fulfilling the promises made to Israel and extending to all nations (Isaiah 2:2–4).

The New Covenant: Jeremiah 31:31–34 promises the establishment of a new covenant in which God would forgive the sins of His people and write His law on their hearts. This covenant would not be like the Mosaic covenant, which Israel had broken, but a transformative relationship based on internal renewal and an intimate knowledge of God. Ezekiel 36:25–27 complements this vision by describing how God would cleanse His people, give them a new heart, and put His Spirit within them.

The Restoration of Israel: The prophets frequently spoke of Israel’s restoration from captivity and dispersion among the nations. Isaiah 11:11 and Jeremiah 23:3 foretell a regathering of God’s people to their land, while Ezekiel 36:24–28 emphasizes their spiritual renewal alongside physical restoration. This theme underscores God’s faithfulness to His covenant promises, even in the face of Israel’s disobedience.

The Outpouring of the Spirit: Joel 2:28–32 predicts a time when God’s Spirit would be poured out on all flesh, transcending social, gender, and age boundaries. This outpouring signifies a new era of divine blessing and empowerment, preparing God’s people for the eschatological fulfillment of His purposes. This prophecy finds its initial fulfillment at Pentecost (Acts 2:16–21) and points toward the ultimate renewal of creation.

The Day of the Lord: The “Day of the Lord” is a recurring theme in the Old Testament, representing a time of divine intervention in history. It encompasses both judgment upon the wicked and deliverance for God’s people. Obadiah 15–16, Joel 1:15, Isaiah 13, Amos 5:18–20, and Zephaniah 1:7, 14–16 describe this day as a time of cosmic upheaval and decisive judgment. Malachi 4:5 anticipates the coming of Elijah before this day, pointing to a time of ultimate reckoning and restoration.

The New Heavens and New Earth: The eschatological hope of the Old Testament includes the renewal of creation. Isaiah 65:17 and 66:22 speak of “new heavens and a new earth,” where God’s purposes will be fully realized. This transformed creation is marked by peace, justice, and the absence of suffering, reflecting a return to the paradisiacal state of Eden (Isaiah 11:6–9; 32:15; 35:7).

Resurrection and Judgment: This is an addition to Hoekema's framework. The Old Testament also anticipates the resurrection and final judgment. Job 19:25–27 expresses confidence in a bodily resurrection, and Daniel 12:2 explicitly speaks of the resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked for eternal reward or punishment. Psalm 50:4–6 and Ecclesiastes 12:14 declare that God will judge all humanity, rewarding the faithful and condemning the unrepentant.

New Testament

John the Baptist

The Gospel of Luke sets the stage for eschatological fulfillment by introducing John the Baptist as the herald of the Messiah. Gabriel's announcement to Zechariah in Luke 1:16–17 explicitly ties John's mission to Malachi's prophecy. Gabriel states that John will "go on before the Lord, in the spirit and power of Elijah, to turn the hearts of the fathers to their children and the disobedient to the wisdom of the righteous." This language mirrors Malachi 4:5–6, where the prophet foretells the coming of Elijah before the "great and dreadful day of the Lord."

John’s role as the “Elijah” who prepares the way for the Lord is reinforced by Jesus himself. In Matthew 11:14 and Matthew 17:11–13, Jesus identifies John as the fulfillment of Malachi’s prophecy, confirming the typological connection. This identification is significant for New Testament eschatology because it aligns John’s ministry with the inauguration of the Messianic age. His call to repentance and baptism prepares Israel to receive the Messiah, echoing Malachi’s call for covenantal renewal.

The disciples’ question in Matthew 17:10–13 reveals their struggle to reconcile Malachi’s eschatological timeline with their experience. Having seen Elijah at the transfiguration, they were perplexed by the apparent reversal of Malachi’s order: Elijah was to precede the coming of the Lord. Jesus clarifies that John the Baptist has already come as the "Elijah" figure, fulfilling Malachi’s prophecy and paving the way for the Messiah.

By identifying John as Elijah and himself as the "Lord who was to come," Jesus not only resolves their confusion but also asserts his divine identity. This identification affirms that the eschatological promises of Malachi are being realized in Jesus, who embodies Yahweh’s presence and ushers in the new covenant.

Davidic and Abrahamic Promises

The angel Gabriel’s announcement to Mary in Luke 1:32–33 connects Jesus to the Davidic covenant. Gabriel declares that God will give Jesus the throne of his father David, and his kingdom will be everlasting. This declaration ties Jesus to the eschatological expectation of a Davidic king who would reign forever, fulfilling promises found in 2 Samuel 7:12–16 and reiterated in the Psalms and Prophets (e.g., Isa 9:6–7; Jer 23:5–6).

Moreover, Mary’s Magnificat (Lk 1:54–55) and Zechariah’s Benedictus (Lk 1:68–75) frame these events within the context of God’s covenant with Abraham. Mary praises God for remembering His promise to Abraham and extending mercy to Israel, while Zechariah celebrates the coming redemption as a fulfillment of the oath sworn to the patriarch. This covenantal framework situates Jesus’ advent within the broader narrative of God’s plan to bless all nations through Abraham’s offspring (Gen 12:3; Gal 3:16).

The early recognition of Jesus as the eschatological fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies continues in Luke 2:29–32, where Simeon declares the infant Jesus as God’s “salvation” and “a light for revelation to the Gentiles.” This moment expands the scope of eschatology beyond Israel to include the Gentiles, emphasizing the universal dimensions of Jesus’ mission.

Simeon’s prophecy also introduces a note of paradox and suffering: Jesus is “destined to cause the falling and rising of many in Israel” and to be opposed (Lk 2:34–35). This foreshadowing aligns with Isaiah’s portrayal of the suffering servant (Isa 53) and underscores the tension between triumph and tragedy in New Testament eschatology. Jesus’ ministry, death, and resurrection will fulfill Israel’s hope.

Call to Repentance

John’s primary proclamation, as recorded in Matthew 3:2, is the call to repentance because "the kingdom of heaven has drawn near." This statement reflects the eschatological tension of the “already” and “not yet” that permeates New Testament theology. The kingdom of heaven is understood dynamically, not merely as a spatial realm but as the active reign and rule of God breaking into history. This breaking-in marks a decisive moment: the long-awaited Messianic age is at hand, and humanity stands at a crossroads.

John's call to repentance reflects the ethical and covenantal demands of this kingdom. Like the prophets before him, John emphasizes that genuine repentance must bear fruit, symbolized by his imagery of the "tree that does not produce good fruit" being "cut down and thrown into the fire" (Mat 3:10). The kingdom's arrival demands a radical reorientation of life in light of the Messiah.

Salvation and Judgment

Central to John’s eschatology is his identification of Jesus as the Messiah. He declares, "Look, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" (Jn 1:29), emphasizing Jesus' salvific mission. Yet John also underscores Jesus' role as the eschatological judge, as seen in his proclamation in Matthew 3:11–12:

"He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and with fire. His winnowing fork is in his hand, and he will clear his threshing floor, gathering the wheat into his barn and burning up the chaff with unquenchable fire."

This passage highlights a duality in Jesus' mission: he brings the Holy Spirit as the agent of salvation and fire as the instrument of judgment. The use of fire here, alongside the earlier reference to unfruitful trees being burned (Mat 3:10), points to the eschatological judgment that awaits those who remain unrepentant. The Messiah's arrival is not merely a moment of hope but also a moment of reckoning.

However, this perspective does not distinguish between the immediate and ultimate manifestations of divine action. This lack of chronological distinction leads to John’s apparent confusion when Jesus’ ministry does not immediately fulfill all aspects of his eschatological expectations. From prison, John sends his disciples to ask Jesus, “Are you the one who is to come, or should we expect another?” (Mat 11:3).

Importantly, Jesus describes John as “a reed unswayed by the wind” (Mat 11:7) and commends his steadfastness even in the face of imprisonment. Jesus’ words clarify that John’s questions do not reflect a faltering faith but rather the limitations of his prophetic understanding. John, like many of his contemporaries, expected the Messiah to enact both salvation and judgment in a single, definitive act.

Jesus’ response reassures John and his disciples by pointing to the signs of the kingdom already at work: the blind see, the lame walk, lepers are cleansed, the deaf hear, the dead are raised, and the good news is proclaimed to the poor (Mat 11:5). These acts fulfill Isaiah’s prophecies of the Messianic age (Isa 35:5–6; 61:1–2), affirming that the kingdom has indeed come, albeit in a way that unfolds progressively.

Old Testament Fulfillment

Old Testament believers anticipated a Davidic king who would triumph over Israel’s enemies and establish a global reign of justice and peace (e.g., Ps 2; Isa 9:6–7). Simultaneously, they looked for Isaiah’s Suffering Servant (Isa 52:13–53:12) who would bear the sins of the people through suffering and death.

Jesus reconciled these seemingly disparate roles by identifying himself as the king who conquers not through military might but through sacrificial love. His crucifixion, rather than being a negation of his kingship, became its ultimate expression, as the cross demonstrated God’s power and wisdom (1 Cor 1:24).

Moreover, the apocalyptic figure of the Son of Man in Daniel 7:13–14 is central to Jesus’ self-identification. This figure, who receives an everlasting dominion from the Ancient of Days, embodies both divine authority and human solidarity. Jesus uses this title (e.g., Mk 2:10, 14:62) to emphasize his eschatological role while subverting expectations by associating it with suffering (Mk 8:31).

Already/Not-Yet Framework

Jesus uniquely synthesized the various eschatological images—David’s victorious Priest-King, Isaiah’s Suffering Servant, and Daniel’s Son of Man—within the framework of his proclamation of the Kingdom of God. This synthesis interprets Old Testament eschatology in a profound way that was not yet fully understood and establishes the central paradigm for New Testament eschatology, emphasizing the dynamic tension between the present realization and the future consummation of God’s reign. This theological framework calls believers to live in the tension of the “already” and the “not yet,” participating in the Kingdom’s present reality while anticipating its future glory.

This provides a paradigm for the Church to understand its mission and hope: The Church participates in the present reality of the Kingdom through faithful proclamation, sacramental life, and acts of justice and mercy as the Church awaits the future consummation of the Kingdom, maintaining vigilance and readiness for Christ’s return, while reflecting the ethical demands of the Kingdom, rooted in repentance and obedience, both communally and individually.

Inauguration

At the heart of Jesus’ teaching is the announcement, “The time has been fulfilled. The kingdom of God has drawn near; repent and believe the gospel” (Mk 1:15). This statement is programmatic, signaling that in Jesus’ person and ministry, the eschatological reign of God had broken into human history. The perfect tense of Peplērōtai (“has been fulfilled”) in Mark 1:15 conveys that a divinely appointed period has reached its climax, inaugurating a new era. Similarly, the verb ēngiken (“has drawn near”), though debated in its precise nuance, strongly suggests an arrival rather than mere proximity, aligning with the aorist ephthasen (“has come”) used in Matthew 12:28 and Luke 11:20.

Jesus declares, “If I drive out demons by the Spirit of God, then the kingdom of God has come upon you” (Mat 12:28). Here, the casting out of demons signifies the breaking of Satan’s power and the establishment of God’s reign.

In Luke 17:20–21, Jesus states, “The kingdom of God is within you.” While this phrase has been variously translated as “within you” or “among you,” the context emphasizes the kingdom’s presence in Jesus himself and its accessibility to those who respond in faith.

Consummation

Despite its present manifestation, the Kingdom remains incomplete. It awaits its ultimate fulfillment.

The Coming Kingdom and Prayer for Its Arrival: Jesus taught his disciples to pray, “May your kingdom come” (Mat 6:10). This prayer reflects the tension between the inaugurated Kingdom, visible in Jesus’ ministry, and the final establishment of God’s rule when his will is perfectly accomplished "on earth as it is in heaven." This is eschatological hope is embedded the way we ought to pray.

Entrance into the Future Kingdom: Jesus emphasized that entrance into the future Kingdom was contingent upon doing the will of the Father (Mat 7:21–23). In this passage, the "Kingdom of Heaven" refers to the consummated reign of God that will be fully realized at the final judgment. The warning that mere verbal acknowledgment (“Lord, Lord”) is insufficient highlights the ethical and relational dimensions of eschatological readiness. Despite outward appearances of religiosity, such people will be excluded from the Kingdom, revealing the justice of God’s rule.

The Renewal of All Things: In Matthew 19:28, Jesus speaks of the "renewal of all things" (palingenesia) when the Son of Man will sit on his glorious throne. The term palingenesia denotes a cosmic renewal, echoing Old Testament prophecies of a new creation (Isa 65:17; 66:22). At this climactic moment, Jesus promises that his disciples will share in his authority, sitting on twelve thrones to judge the twelve tribes of Israel. This passage reveals both the restoration of creation and the fulfillment of God’s covenantal promises to Israel.

The Son of Man’s Glorious Return: In his Olivet Discourse, Jesus offers a detailed description of his future coming in glory (Mat 25:31–34). The Son of Man will return with the angels and sit on his heavenly throne to judge all nations. This vision aligns with Daniel 7:13–14, where the Son of Man is granted everlasting dominion. The separation of the sheep and goats represents the final judgment, where the righteous inherit the Kingdom prepared for them since creation.

The Eschatological Banquet: Jesus portrayed the future Kingdom as a banquet where “many will come from the east and the west and take their places at the feast with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob in the Kingdom of Heaven” (Mat 8:11–12). This image evokes the prophetic vision of the eschatological gathering of the nations (Isa 25:6–8) and emphasizes the inclusion of Gentiles in God’s redemptive plan. However, the warning that “subjects of the kingdom” (unfaithful Israelites) will be cast into darkness underscores the reality of judgment alongside the blessings of the Kingdom.

The Last Supper: During the Last Supper, Jesus told his disciples, “I will not drink from this fruit of the vine from now on until that day when I drink it anew with you in my Father’s Kingdom” (Mat 26:29). Reinforcing the theme of future celebration and fellowship in the fully realized Kingdom of God.

Parables of the Kingdom

The eschatological dimensions of Jesus' teaching are profoundly evident in his "kingdom of heaven" parables, particularly in Matthew 13. These parables, rich in imagery and layered with theological nuance, unveil critical "mysteries" concerning the nature and progression of God’s redemptive reign, which reflect already-and-not-yet dynamic of the kingdom.

Jesus declares in Matthew 13:11 that his parables disclose "the mysteries of the kingdom of heaven," signifying truths previously hidden but now revealed. These mysteries do not represent a radical divergence from Old Testament expectations but rather the unveiling of a deeper reality previously obscured. The kingdom is present in Christ's ministry but not in the cataclysmic, apocalyptic fashion anticipated by many first-century Jews.

The parables in Matthew 13 articulate the dual-stage development of the kingdom. While Daniel 2 envisions the kingdom as a singular, cataclysmic event that annihilates earthly powers, Jesus reframes this expectation. He introduces a kingdom that is both inaugurated and awaiting consummation—a kingdom presently operative in grace and power but not yet manifest in its ultimate fullness.

The Sower (Mat 13:3–9, 18–23): This parable emphasizes the responsiveness required to enter the kingdom. The seed, representing the word of the kingdom, faces varied receptions. Eschatologically, the present age is marked by both fruitful and fruitless responses, a period of testing and growth.

The Wheat and the Tares (Mat 13:24–30, 36–43): Here, the kingdom encompasses both the righteous and the wicked until the eschaton, when divine judgment will separate them. The coexistence of good and evil reflects the "mystery" aspect of the kingdom's present phase, which tolerates imperfection while awaiting the harvest at the "end of the age."

The Mustard Seed and the Leaven (Mat 13:31–33): These parables depict the kingdom's seemingly insignificant beginnings yet inevitable, transformative growth. Eschatologically, they foreshadow the kingdom's ultimate dominion despite its current hiddenness.

The Hidden Treasure and the Pearl (Mat 13:44–46): Underscores the inestimable value of the kingdom, demanding complete devotion and sacrifice. This echoes the eschatological reality that the kingdom's ultimate consummation will validate the radical commitment required to inherit it.

The Parable of the Net (Mat 13:47–50): This portrays the eschatological judgment when the righteous and the wicked are definitively separated. This reinforces the kingdom's dual nature—present in grace but culminating in power and judgment.

The Growing Seed (Mk 4:26–29): The kingdom's growth is not dependent on man, emphasizing the sovereign and supernatural nature of its expansion. This subverts Jewish expectations of a militaristic or apocalyptic intervention and highlights the grace-laden reality of God's redemptive plan.

Through these parables, Jesus challenges preconceived notions of divine rule, inviting his audience to recognize the kingdom's hidden yet powerful reality. This eschatological tension—the already and not yet—defines the Christian hope, as believers await the day when the King returns in glory to consummate his reign over all creation.

Parallels

Theologically, these parables align with the New Testament's portrayal of eschatological tension. The present age ("this age") is characterized by the inaugurated kingdom, while "the age to come" awaits its consummation (Lk 18:30). Pauline and Johannine writings echo this framework, distinguishing between present salvation (e.g., Eph 2:8) and future glory (e.g., Rom 8:18–25).

Paul’s declaration that "the end of the ages has come" (1 Cor 10:11) reflects the inaugurated eschatology introduced by Christ. Simultaneously, texts such as 1 Thessalonians 4:16–17 and Revelation 21–22 point to the consummative eschatology, when the kingdom will be fully realized.

The Olivet Discourse

The Olivet Discourse, recorded in Matthew 24–25, Mark 13, and Luke 21:3–36, stands as Jesus' most comprehensive teaching on eschatology. In this prophetic discourse, Jesus addresses the destruction of the temple, his Second Advent, and the "end of the age," offering crucial corrections to his disciples' assumptions. The discourse is steeped in apocalyptic imagery and prophetic language, drawing heavily from the Old Testament while revealing new dimensions of God's eschatological plan.

The discourse is set against Jesus' denunciation of the scribes and Pharisees in Matthew 23, where he pronounces seven woes, culminating in the declaration of divine judgment on the current generation (Mat 23:32–36). This condemnation not only indicts Israel's leaders for their rejection of the prophets but also anticipates their ultimate rejection of the Messiah. The reference to the cumulative guilt of shedding righteous blood, from Abel to Zechariah (Mat 23:35), portrays a covenantal solidarity in sin that results in impending judgment—fulfilled historically in the destruction of Jerusalem in A.D. 70.

As Jesus exits the temple, his prediction of its total destruction (Mat 24:2) prompts the disciples to ask, "When will these things happen? And what will be the sign of your coming and of the end of the age?" (Mat 24:3). This compound question reflects their conflation of three events:

  1. The destruction of the temple.
  2. Jesus' return (parousia).
  3. The consummation of history ("the end of the age").

A key to interpreting the Olivet Discourse lies in recognizing the dual focus. Jesus’ response separates these events temporally, distinguishing the immediate judgment on Jerusalem from his eschatological return. The near-term fulfillment in 70 A.D. is the destruction of the temple and the tribulations of the Jewish War. The eschatological fulfillment is the Second Coming and final judgment.

Signs of the Temple's Destruction

Jesus begins by describing the immediate context leading to the destruction of Jerusalem:

False Messiahs and Deception: Jesus warns of individuals claiming messianic status (Mat 24:5)—a phenomenon observed before A.D. 70.

Wars and Disturbances: While conflicts and upheavals are mentioned, they are not signs of the eschaton but the "beginning of birth pains" (Mat 24:6–8).

Persecution and Apostasy: The church will face persecution, betrayals, and the rise of false prophets (Mat 24:9–12).

The Abomination of Desolation: Rooted in Daniel 9:27, 11:31, and 12:11, this refers to a desecration of the temple, likely fulfilled in events surrounding A.D. 70 (Mat 24:15–16) but it also foreshadows end-time desecration. Luke’s account (Lk 21:20–24) explicitly identifies the "desolation" with the Roman siege of Jerusalem, emphasizing its historical fulfillment and catastrophic impact on the Jewish people.

The Second Coming and the End of the Age

The shift to Jesus' Second Coming is marked by cosmic disturbances:

Celestial Signs: The darkening of the sun and moon and the falling of stars (Mat 24:29) employ apocalyptic imagery common in Isaiah 13:10 and Joel 2:10, 30–31. Cosmic imagery serves as both metaphor for cataclysmic upheaval and a literal depiction of divine intervention.

The Son of Man’s Parousia: Jesus’ return will be unmistakable, as described in Matthew 24:30 with a clear reference to Daniel 7:13–14. This event leads to the gathering of the elect (Mat 24:31), a motif linked to ultimate redemption and judgment.

Parables and Exhortations

Jesus employs parables to emphasize watchfulness and readiness for his return:

The Fig Tree (Mat 24:32–35): Just as budding leaves signal summer, so the signs of the temple's fall point to the reliability of Jesus' words.

The Days of Noah (Mat 24:36–41): Jesus underscores the unexpected nature of his coming, comparing it to the sudden flood in Noah's time.

The Faithful and Wicked Servant (Mat 24:45–51): Faithful vigilance is contrasted with complacency.

The Ten Virgins (Mat 25:1–13): Readiness for the bridegroom (the parousia) is paramount, illustrating the eschatological importance of spiritual preparation.

The Talents (Mat 25:14–30): Accountability for stewardship during the master's absence parallels the responsibility of believers in the present age.

The Sheep and the Goats (Mat 25:31–46): This climactic parable portrays the final judgment, where nations are divided based on their treatment of "the least of these," highlighting the ethical implications of kingdom living.

Already/Not-Yet

The destruction of Jerusalem serves as a historical microcosm of divine judgment. These are the "beginning of birth pains" (Mat 24:8), the present tribulations that come before and lead to the parousia, while the Second Coming itself is the culmination of the kingdom in power, fulfilling promises of resurrection and judgment.

The emphasis on watchfulness and preparedness reflects this eschatology. Believers are called to live faithfully in the present while anticipating the consummation of God's kingdom.

Warning and Admonition

The early portion of Jesus' Olivet Discourse (Mat 24:4–14) serves as a prelude to his more specific eschatological teachings. In this section, Jesus warns his disciples against misinterpreting certain events as definitive signs of the imminent destruction of Jerusalem or the eschaton. Instead, he emphasizes perseverance in the face of hardship and the necessity of the gospel's worldwide proclamation.

Jesus begins with an admonition:

“Watch out that no one deceives you. For many will come in my name, claiming, ‘I am the Messiah,’ and will deceive many.” (Mat 24:4–5).

The rise of false messianic figures, particularly during times of upheaval, reflects the expectation of deliverance among oppressed populations. Historical records, such as those of Josephus, confirm that such figures proliferated before the Jewish War (A.D. 66–70). Jesus' warning underscores the danger of misplaced trust in such individuals, highlighting the need for discernment.

Jesus lists wars, rumors of wars, famines, and earthquakes (Mat 24:6–7) but cautions that these events do not herald "the end." This statement challenges apocalyptic fervor, which tends to interpret every calamity as a definitive eschatological sign. While these events are significant, they are not to be mistaken for immediate precursors to either Jerusalem’s destruction or the eschaton.

That the "end is still to come" appears to refer to the climactic judgment upon Jerusalem and the termination of Israel's national identity as God's covenant people. Jesus' use of "end" here emphasizes that these preliminary events are not the conclusion of God's redemptive timeline.

Jesus transitions from general warnings to specific admonitions about the trials his disciples will face:

“Then you will be handed over to be persecuted and put to death, and you will be hated by all nations because of me.” (Mat 24:9).

The early church experienced severe persecution, as seen in the martyrdoms of Stephen (Acts 7:54–60) and James (Acts 12:2), as well as broader hostility from both Jewish and Roman authorities. Jesus predicts that such persecution will lead to widespread apostasy:

“At that time many will turn away from the faith and will betray and hate each other.” (Mat 24:10).

Jesus warns of false prophets who will deceive many (Mat 24:11). This phenomenon is evident in the New Testament epistles, where apostles confront heresies and false teachings (e.g., 2 Pet 2:1, 1 Jn 2:18–22).

The prevalence of sin will cause love to "grow cold" (Matthew 24:12). This reflects the relational and communal breakdown that accompanies lawlessness—a reality that was manifest in the social and political turmoil preceding Jerusalem’s fall. Also reflected in the apostacy which must precede the second coming in 2 Thessalonians 2:3.

Jesus emphasizes the necessity of endurance and the global proclamation of the gospel:

“But the one who stands firm to the end will be saved. And this gospel of the kingdom will be preached in the whole world as a testimony to all nations, and then the end will come.” (Mat 24:13–14).

The term “end” here most likely refers to the destruction of Jerusalem in A.D. 70. Jesus links this "end" to the completion of the gospel’s proclamation to the known world. The temporal distinction between the destruction of Jerusalem and the ultimate eschaton suggests that verse 14 addresses the former, not the latter.

New Testament texts corroborate the claim that the gospel had reached the known world prior to the fall of Jerusalem:

  • Acts 2:5: The Pentecost event draws God-fearing Jews from "every nation under heaven."
  • Romans 1:8: Paul celebrates the global reputation of the Roman church's faith.
  • Romans 10:18: Paul cites Psalm 19:4, affirming that the message has gone "to the ends of the world."
  • Colossians 1:6, 23: Paul declares that the gospel is bearing fruit "all over the world" and "has been proclaimed to every creature under heaven."

These statements reflect the widespread dissemination of the gospel throughout the Roman Empire by the apostles and early church, fulfilling Jesus' prediction within the temporal framework leading to A.D. 70.

A Warning About False Messiahs

Jesus warns against mistaking false claims for his actual return:

“If anyone says to you, ‘Look, here is the Messiah!’ or, ‘There he is!’ do not believe it.” (Mat 24:23)

This warning is tied to the destruction of Jerusalem. Many, seeing the catastrophic events of A.D. 70, might be tempted to believe messianic pretenders claiming to fulfill eschatological promises.

Jesus clarifies that his Second Advent will not be a hidden or localized event:

“For as lightning that comes from the east is visible even in the west, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.” (Matthew 24:27)

The metaphor of lightning emphasizes the sudden, unmistakable, and universal visibility of his coming. This description stands in stark contrast to the false claims of localized messianic appearances.

The cryptic analogy—“Wherever there is a carcass, there the vultures will gather”—likely refers to the inevitability of divine judgment. Just as vultures instinctively gather around a corpse, so God’s judgment is certain and appropriate when corruption reaches its fullness (cf. Ezek 39:17–20).

Israel’s Downfall and the New Jubilee

The vivid language of Matthew 24:29—the sun darkened, the moon failing, and the stars falling—is not unique to Jesus but reflects a common prophetic style in the Old Testament. Such language often signals the downfall of nations under God’s judgment:

  • Isaiah 13:10: Against Babylon.
  • Ezekiel 32:7–8: Against Egypt.
  • Joel 2:10, 31: Concerning locust plagues and judgment on Israel.

Here, Jesus applies this imagery to the destruction of Jerusalem. The "immediately" in Matthew 24:29 connects this imagery to the events following the fall of the temple in A.D. 70, symbolizing the end of Israel as a covenant nation with a central earthly temple.

Jesus declares:

“And then shall appear the sign of the Son of Man in heaven.” (Mat 24:30a)

The phrase is best understood not as the Son of Man physically descending but as a sign of his sovereign authority and reign. The destruction of Jerusalem functions as a historical "sign" validating Jesus’ claim to be the enthroned Messiah. This parallels his declaration to the high priest:

“From now on you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Mighty One and coming on the clouds of heaven.” (Mat 26:64)

The "sign" is not a physical manifestation but the vindication of Christ’s messianic reign through divine judgment.

“All the tribes of the earth will mourn.” (Mat 24:30b)

The term “tribes” likely refers to the tribes of Israel dispersed throughout the Roman world. Their mourning reflects the collective grief over divine judgment. Unbelieving Jews mourn as they experience God’s vengeance (cf. 1 Thes 2:14–16), and elect Jews mourn in repentance, echoing Zechariah 12:10, which anticipates a spirit of grace and supplication leading to the recognition of the pierced Messiah.

The reference to the Son of Man "coming on the clouds of heaven" (Mat 24:30) alludes to Daniel 7:13–14. In Daniel, the "coming" of the Son of Man is not a descent to earth but an ascension to the Ancient of Days to receive dominion. Here, Jesus applies the imagery to his enthronement and ongoing reign, visible through historical judgments such as the destruction of Jerusalem.

“He will send his messengers with a great trumpet and they will gather his elect from the four winds.” (Mat 24:31)

This verse shifts focus from judgment to mission. The "messengers" are likely human preachers of the gospel (cf. Mat 11:10; Mk 1:2). The "great trumpet" evokes the Jubilee imagery of Leviticus 25 and Isaiah 27:13, signaling liberation and restoration. This theme is explicitly connected to the gospel’s proclamation (cf. Lk 4:17–21).

The gathering of the elect “from the four winds” reflects the global expansion of the church as the new covenant people of God. The fall of Jerusalem marks the transition from the old dispensation centered on the temple to the universal mission of the church.

The Parable of the Fig Tree

Jesus uses the fig tree as an analogy for discerning the approach of the kingdom:

“When you see all these things, you know that it is near, right at the door.” (Mat 24:33)

The phrase "all these things" refers to the events previously described, particularly the surrounding of Jerusalem by Roman armies and the abomination of desolation. The imagery of budding leaves indicates that certain observable events will precede Jerusalem’s destruction.

The kingdom of God, as referenced here, does not signify the eschatological consummation but rather God’s judgment upon Israel through the destruction of the temple. Luke’s parallel account (Lk 21:31) clarifies this by stating:

“When you see these things happening, you know that the kingdom of God is near.”

This judgment marks the end of the old covenant system and the transition to the new covenant, centered on Christ’s messianic reign.

“Truly I tell you, this generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened.” (Mat 24:34)

The term “this generation” is consistently used in the Gospels to refer to Jesus’ contemporaries (e.g., Mat 11:16; 12:41–42). This time marker solidifies the interpretation that the events described in Matthew 24:4–33—including the destruction of Jerusalem—would occur within the first century. The destruction of Jerusalem and its temple in A.D. 70 fulfills this prophecy.

Transition to the Second Coming

At Matthew 24:36, Jesus shifts focus:

“But about that day or hour no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father.”

The singular phrase “that day” contrasts with the plural “those days” in earlier verses, signaling a new subject: the Second Coming and the Eschaton. Unlike the destruction of Jerusalem, which was preceded by observable signs, the timing of Christ’s return remains unknowable.

From Matthew 24:42–25:30, Jesus emphasizes vigilance and preparedness through a series of parables:

  • The Watchful Householder (24:43–44): Christ’s return will be as unexpected as a thief in the night, urging constant readiness.
  • The Faithful and Wicked Servants (24:45–51): Faithfulness in the present age is critical, as the master will return at an hour not anticipated.
  • The Ten Virgins (25:1–13): The need for spiritual preparedness is illustrated by the contrast between wise and foolish virgins awaiting the bridegroom.
  • The Talents (25:14–30): Faithful stewardship of the gifts entrusted by God is essential as believers await the final reckoning.

Second Coming Timing

The suggestion that Jesus miscalculated the timing of his parousia (Second Coming) arises from certain verses in the Synoptic Gospels, often interpreted as indicating an imminent eschatological fulfillment.

Matthew 10:23:

“I tell you the truth, you will not finish going through the cities of Israel before the Son of Man comes.”

Interpretive options:

  1. Post-Resurrection Appearance: Some argue that this refers to Jesus’ post-resurrection appearances to his disciples, where his authority as the risen Messiah is demonstrated.
  2. Destruction of Jerusalem: This interpretation sees the “coming of the Son of Man” as referring to God’s judgment on Israel, culminating in the Roman destruction of Jerusalem and the temple.
  3. Ongoing Mission to Israel: Others suggest that Jesus is speaking of the church’s enduring mission to Israel throughout history, with the "coming" referring to the Second Coming.

The second view aligns best with the context. The coming of the Son of Man in judgment on Jerusalem (A.D. 70) serves as a typological precursor to the eschatological parousia. The mission to Israel is framed within the immediate historical context of their rejection of the Messiah and subsequent judgment.

Mark 9:1 ( //Mat 16:28 // Lk 9:27):

“Some who are standing here will not taste death before they see the kingdom of God come with power.”

Interpretive options:

  1. The Transfiguration: This interpretation links the statement to the transfiguration (Mark 9:2–8), which occurs shortly after and reveals Jesus’ glory and kingdom authority to Peter, James, and John.
  2. The Resurrection and Ascension: The kingdom’s power is fully inaugurated through Jesus’ resurrection and ascension, witnessed by the apostles.
  3. Pentecost: The kingdom’s coming with power could refer to the outpouring of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2), initiating the church’s global mission.
  4. Destruction of Jerusalem: The destruction of Jerusalem represents the coming of the kingdom in judgment.

The first view (the Transfiguration) best explains the context, as it is immediately followed by the transfiguration account. The transfiguration prefigures the eschatological kingdom, revealing Jesus’ divine majesty and confirming the future fulfillment of his promises.

Mark 13:30 ( //Mat 24:34 // Lk 21:32)

“This generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened.”

Interpretive options:

  1. The Fall of Jerusalem: The phrase “this generation” refers to Jesus’ contemporaries, and “all these things” describes the events leading up to and including the destruction of Jerusalem.
  2. The Eschaton: Some argue that the verse includes references to the final parousia, requiring a symbolic or extended definition of “this generation.”
  3. Typological Fulfillment: “This generation” represents the ongoing unbelieving generation of Israel throughout history, suggesting a pattern of judgment and redemption.

The first view is the most straightforward and aligns with the time markers throughout the Olivet Discourse. The destruction of Jerusalem fulfills Jesus’ prophecy, providing a tangible demonstration of divine judgment while foreshadowing ultimate eschatological fulfillment.

Jesus explicitly states, "that day or hour no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father.” (Mat 24:36; Mk 13:32)" This reflects Jesus’ self-limitation in knowledge regarding the timing of his parousia during his earthly ministry. It precludes any interpretation where Jesus provides a specific timeline for the eschaton.

Jesus’ teachings balance the tension between the expectation of imminent fulfillment and the acknowledgment of delay: Verses about the kingdom being near and not knowing when the second coming will be emphasize the urgency of repentance and preparedness, while parables such as the Ten Virgins (Mat25:1–13) and the Wicked Servant (Mat 24:45–51) stress the long duration before the parousia. This tension is just another reflection of the inaugurated eschatology of the New Testament, where the kingdom is already present but not yet fully consummated.

Summary

Jesus’ eschatology envisions history as divided into two distinct and comprehensive epochs:

  • This Present Age: Characterized as an "evil age" (Gal 1:4), it is marked by sin, suffering, and opposition to God’s kingdom. This age includes the realities of human rebellion, spiritual conflict, and temporal existence under the curse of sin.
  • The Age to Come: The age to come represents the consummation of God’s redemptive purposes, marked by the full realization of the kingdom of God, eternal righteousness, and unbroken fellowship with God.

The language of Matthew 12:32 highlights the exclusivity of these two ages: “Anyone who speaks a word against the Son of Man will be forgiven, but anyone who speaks against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven, either in this age or in the age to come.” The parallel in Mark 3:29 confirms this: "but whoever may blaspheme against the Holy Spirit never has forgiveness, but is subject to eternal condemnation."

There is no indication of additional intermediary phases such as a millennial reign within Jesus’ eschatological framework. This understanding is reinforced by the consistent use of contrasting terms like "this age" and "that age" (e.g., Lk 20:34–36), which portray a sharp transition rather than a gradual or segmented progression.

The termination of this present age and the inauguration of the age to come is marked by a single, definitive event, which is the return of Christ. The parousia, or Second Coming of Christ, is described as a visible, glorious, and cosmic event (Mat 24:30; 25:31). Christ’s return brings with it key eschatological realities, including:

  • The resurrection of the dead (Jn 5:28–29).
  • The final judgment (Mat 25:31–46).
  • The renewal of creation, ushering in the new heavens and new earth (Rev 21:1–5).

This climactic event serves as the hinge of history, bringing this age to an end and ushering in the eternal age of God’s kingdom.

Eschatology in James

James’s eschatological framework mirrors that of Jesus, emphasizing the dual realities of inaugurated eschatology (the "already") and future eschatology (the "not yet"). While James does not delve into elaborate apocalyptic imagery, his writings reflect a deeply practical application of eschatological truths, serving as both a theological foundation and a moral imperative for Christian living. The certainty of Christ’s return and the coming judgment serves as both a warning and an invitation, urging Christians to live in light of their eschatological destiny as heirs of God’s eternal kingdom.

James affirms that certain eschatological promises have already begun to be fulfilled in the present age. This "already" aspect of eschatology is evident in the following themes:

“He chose to give us birth through the word of truth, that we might be a kind of firstfruits of all he created.” (Jam 1:18)

This statement reflects the inaugurated aspect of salvation, where believers are already born anew through the word of truth. This transformation establishes their identity as God’s redeemed children, foreshadowing the ultimate restoration of all creation.

In Acts 15:13–17, James interprets the multiethnic expansion of the church as the fulfillment of the Old Testament prophecy regarding the rebuilding of David’s fallen house (Amos 9:11–12). This realization of God’s promises demonstrates that the messianic kingdom has already been inaugurated in the person and work of Jesus Christ and is advancing through the church.

The "already" aspect provides a present hope and calling for believers as they are empowered by their new identity in Christ to live as the firstfruits of God's redemptive plan, exemplifying righteousness and holiness in a world still awaiting full restoration.

James also emphasizes the future aspects of eschatology, anticipating the culmination of God’s redemptive work in the return of Christ:

“Be patient, then, brothers and sisters, until the Lord’s coming. See how the farmer waits for the land to yield its valuable crop… You too, be patient and stand firm, because the Lord’s coming is near.” (Jam 5:7–8)

The emphasis on the imminence of Christ’s return reflects the “not yet” dimension, where believers anticipate the parousia as the decisive moment when God will fully establish his kingdom.

James envisions the return of Christ as a moment of judgment. This is not a vague or distant possibility but an impending reality, calling believers to live in holiness and obedience in light of the coming evaluation:

“The Judge is standing at the door!” (Jam 5:9)

James further declares:

“Listen, my dear brothers and sisters: Has not God chosen those who are poor in the eyes of the world to be rich in faith and to inherit the kingdom he promised those who love him?” (Jam 2:5)

The future inheritance of the kingdom underscores the eschatological hope of believers. While they experience God’s reign in part, the fullness of the kingdom remains a future reality tied to Christ’s return.

Believers are called to live by the "royal law that gives freedom" (Jam 2:8, 12–13) in anticipation of Christ’s judgment. The promise of Christ’s imminent return encourages perseverance in trials, akin to the patience of the farmer awaiting the harvest (Jam 5:7–11).

James’s eschatology lacks any reference to an intermediate period between this age and the age to come. He does not describe a future millennial reign of peace or any temporal phase distinct from the present and eternal states. This absence aligns with the broader framework of eschatological dualism taught by Jesus: Two ages with no gaps.
