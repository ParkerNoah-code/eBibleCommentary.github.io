Niccolò Machiavelli

1469: Birth

May 3, 1469: Niccolò Machiavelli is born in Florence, Italy, into a middle-class family.

1489-1498: Education and Early Career

1489: Machiavelli receives a humanist education typical of the Renaissance, studying Latin, classical literature, and history.

1494: The Medici family is expelled from Florence, leading to the establishment of the Florentine Republic. This political shift creates opportunities for individuals like Machiavelli.

1498: Beginning of Political Career

1498: Machiavelli becomes the head of the Second Chancery of the Republic of Florence, responsible for diplomatic missions and military affairs.

1499-1512: Diplomatic Missions and Political Involvement

1499: Machiavelli embarks on a diplomatic mission to the court of Louis XII of France, where he observes French military tactics and political strategies.

1500: He travels to Rome to observe the papal court under Pope Alexander VI, gaining insights into the complex relationship between religion and politics.

1502-1503: Machiavelli is sent to meet Cesare Borgia, the son of Pope Alexander VI, in the Romagna region. Borgia’s use of ruthlessness, cunning, and strategic alliances deeply influences Machiavelli’s political philosophy.

1503: Machiavelli advocates for the establishment of a citizen militia in Florence, arguing that reliance on mercenaries is dangerous. He believes a militia composed of citizens will be more loyal and effective in defending the republic.

1504: Machiavelli is part of a delegation sent to the court of the Holy Roman Emperor Maximilian I to negotiate alliances.

1506: The Florentine militia, under Machiavelli’s organization, successfully defends the city against external threats, bolstering his reputation as a military strategist.

1512-1513: Fall from Power and Exile

1512: The Medici family returns to power in Florence with the support of Spanish forces. Machiavelli is removed from his position, accused of conspiracy against the Medici, and imprisoned. He is tortured but eventually released, largely due to lack of evidence.

1513: In exile at his family estate in Sant'Andrea in Percussina, Machiavelli writes The Prince, which he dedicates to Lorenzo de' Medici in an attempt to regain favor. The book is a practical guide for rulers, emphasizing the need for pragmatism, realism, and occasionally ruthless measures to maintain power and stability.

1515: The Art of War

1515: Machiavelli writes The Art of War, a dialogue on military strategy and tactics, reflecting his views on the importance of military readiness and civic responsibility.

1519-1525: Historical and Political Writings

1519: Machiavelli begins writing the Discourses on Livy, a comprehensive analysis of the Roman Republic and the benefits of republican government. He contrasts the stability of republics with the volatility of principalities.

1520: He is commissioned by the Medici to write a history of Florence.

1521: Machiavelli writes The Life of Castruccio Castracani, a semi-biographical work that illustrates his ideas about leadership and power through the story of a historical figure.

1525: Machiavelli completes the History of Florence, presenting it to the Medici rulers. The work reflects his deep understanding of historical forces and political dynamics. This work provides a detailed account of the city’s history and politics from its founding to his own time.

1527: Death and Legacy

June 21, 1527: Niccolò Machiavelli dies in Florence at the age of 58, shortly after the Medici are expelled and the Republic is restored.

Machiavelli's writings, particularly The Prince, leave a lasting legacy on political thought, with his name becoming synonymous with political cunning and realpolitik. His ideas on power, leadership, and the nature of human behavior continue to be studied and debated.

Machiavelli's work laid the foundation for modern political science by separating politics from ethics and emphasizing the importance of empirical observation and pragmatic decision-making.

His writings influenced numerous political leaders and thinkers, including Thomas Hobbes, John Locke, and Friedrich Nietzsche.

Machiavelli is often regarded as the father of modern political theory, with his ideas continuing to resonate in contemporary discussions on power, governance, and statecraft.

The Prince

Excerpts from The Prince, (Penguin Classics: Britain, 2014), translated by Tim Parks.

The central theme of the book: the acquisition and maintenance of political power. Written for Lorenzo de’ Medici to study and "achieve the greatness that your status and qualities promise" (62).

1. "All states and governments that ever ruled over men have been either republics or monarchies" (63). This chapter sets the stage by categorizing the types of principalities and how they are acquired. Machiavelli distinguishes between hereditary principalities and new principalities, outlining the challenges and advantages associated with each.

2. "A ruler who inherits power has less reason or need to upset his subjects than a new one and as a result is better loved. If he doesn’t go out of his way to get himself hated, it’s reasonable to suppose his people will wish him well" (64). Machiavelli explains that hereditary principalities are easier to maintain than new ones because the ruling family has a long-standing authority and the populace is accustomed to their rule. The main challenge is avoiding the pitfalls of complacency and ensuring continuity through capable governance.

3. A "ruler is bound to upset the people in his new territories, first with his occupying army and then with all the endless injustices consequent on any invasion. So not only do you make enemies of those whose interests you damaged when you occupied the territory, but you can’t even keep the friendship of the people who helped you to take power, this for the simple reason that you can’t give them as much as they expected" (65).

It is easier to hold on to a territory if it has the same language and if it is in a similar geographical location. If the people are not used to self-rule, the old ruling family is killed so that they are dependent on you. Soon enough the occupied territory will become one with the other lands of the king.

However, if they have different customs, languages and institutions, "perhaps the most effective solution is for the new ruler to go and live there himself" (67). Another solution is creating colonies. The principle is: "you must either pamper people or destroy them; harm them just a little and they’ll hit back; harm them seriously and they won’t be able to" (68). Establish friendship with a weaker neighbor and you will have an ally.

"Louis made five mistakes: he eliminated the weaker states; he enhanced the power of one of Italy’s stronger states; he brought in an extremely powerful foreign king; he didn’t go to live in the territory he’d acquired and he didn’t establish colonies there" (72).

4. Machiavelli examines the structure of the Persian Empire under Darius, highlighting the centralized nature of its government. He contrasts this with decentralized states like France, arguing that centralized states are harder to conquer but easier to maintain, as they depend on the singular authority of the prince.

"The reason why it’s hard to conquer a country like Turkey is that there are no barons to invite you in and you can’t expect anyone to make your invasion easier by rebelling against the king" (75). It's hard to start a rebellion because everyone is indebted to the king.

5. Machiavelli outlines three strategies for maintaining control over cities that previously enjoyed self-rule: destroy them, reside in them, or allow them to maintain their laws while imposing a loyal government. The most effective method is complete destruction to prevent rebellion. In a republic, the "memory of old freedoms lingers on and won’t let them rest. In these cases, your only options are to reduce the place to rubble or go and live there yourself" (78).

6. Machiavelli examines leaders who rise to power through their own merit and military prowess. He uses examples such as Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, and Theseus to illustrate how capable leaders establish new states.

7. This chapter explores how leaders gain power through the help of others, such as through alliances or the favor of influential individuals. Cesare Borgia is cited as a key example.

8. Machiavelli analyzes leaders who attain power through immoral or unscrupulous means. He uses examples like Agathocles of Syracuse to show how cruelty can be a tool for maintaining control.

9. The chapter discusses how leaders can rise to power with the support of the populace or the nobility, and the implications of each path for maintaining power.

10. This chapter discusses the importance of a principality's ability to defend itself without external assistance. Machiavelli advises that a prince should focus on building fortifications and cultivating military strength to withstand sieges.

11. Machiavelli considers ecclesiastical principalities unique because they are sustained by religious institutions and are less reliant on conventional political strategies. He points out that the Church's spiritual power and influence make these states stable and hard to challenge.

12. Machiavelli warns against the use of mercenary forces, which are unreliable and motivated by profit rather than loyalty. He argues that mercenaries pose a significant risk to rulers who rely on them, as their interests can easily shift.

13. Continuing his discussion on military forces, Machiavelli differentiates between auxiliary troops, which are borrowed from allies, and native armies. He stresses that a prince should build and rely on his own military forces to ensure loyalty and effectiveness.

14. Machiavelli emphasizes that a prince must prioritize military preparedness and study the art of war. He advises rulers to constantly train and equip their forces, as military strength is the foundation of political power.

15. Machiavelli discusses the virtues and vices attributed to rulers, arguing that a successful prince must be willing to act immorally when necessary.

16. Machiavelli discusses the balance between generosity and frugality. He suggests that while being liberal can win favor, it often leads to resource depletion and higher taxes. Therefore, it is better for a prince to be considered miserly, which ensures financial stability and the ability to respond to emergencies.

17. Machiavelli explores the balance between fear and love in governance, concluding that while both are important, it is safer for a ruler to be feared than loved if they cannot be both.

18. Machiavelli argues that a prince should not be bound by his word if it is detrimental to his interests, highlighting examples of successful leaders who employed deception.

19. This chapter advises princes on how to avoid being despised or hated by their subjects, emphasizing the importance of maintaining a strong, respected image.

20. Machiavelli examines the strategic use of fortresses and other defensive measures. He concludes that while fortresses can be useful, a ruler's best defense lies in gaining the trust and loyalty of his subjects, which makes physical fortifications less necessary.

21. Machiavelli outlines strategies for a prince to gain a positive reputation, such as taking bold actions, aligning with popular causes, and displaying visible successes. He advises rulers to balance diplomacy with decisive actions that capture the admiration of the populace.

22. This chapter stresses the importance of choosing competent advisors. Machiavelli argues that the quality of a prince's counselors reflects on the ruler himself and that wise advisors can significantly enhance a prince's governance.

23. Machiavelli warns against the dangers of flattery, which can lead to poor decision-making. He advises rulers to seek honest feedback and maintain a circle of trusted advisors who are not afraid to speak the truth.

24. Reflecting on recent history, Machiavelli analyzes why Italian rulers lost their territories, attributing their failures to a lack of military preparedness, dependence on mercenaries, and failure to adapt to changing circumstances.

25. Machiavelli discusses the role of fortune in human affairs and argues that while fortune controls half of our actions, the other half is shaped by our actions.

26. The concluding chapter is a passionate call to action for Italian leaders to unify and free Italy from foreign domination.
