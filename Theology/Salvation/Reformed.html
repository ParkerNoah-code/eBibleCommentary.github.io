Reformed Theology

Introduction

Reformed Theology is a God-centered theology that seeks to understand the Bible's teaching on salvation and God's sovereignty in all things. "Reformed" is synonymous with the term "Calvinism" named after the 16th-century Reformer John Calvin, but I prefer "Reformed" as it emphasizes its biblical nature.

Unfortunately, Calvinism is often misunderstood and caricatured by its critics. Some accuse it of portraying God as a tyrant who arbitrarily damns people to hell, or of undermining human responsibility and the need for evangelism. Others confuse it with hyper-Calvinism, a distortion that denies the universal offer of the gospel.

In reality, Calvinism teaches that God is absolutely sovereign over all things, including salvation. Human beings are responsible for their choices, yet because of the Fall, they are in a state of Total Inability, unable to choose God apart from His grace (Rom 3:10-12).

In His sovereign mercy, God has chosen to save some, the elect, not based on any foreseen merit in them, but solely according to His Unconditional Election (Eph 1:4-6). For these elect, Christ provided atonement, a definite, Particular Redemption that actually secured their salvation, not just made it possible (Jn 10:11, 15).

God brings the elect to salvation through Effectual Calling, a work of the Holy Spirit that overcomes their resistance and brings them to faith (Jn 6:44). Those who are truly saved will inevitably persevere in faith to the end by God's sustaining grace, a doctrine known as the Perseverance of the Saints (Phil 1:6).

Sovereignty

At the heart of Reformed Theology is the doctrine of God's sovereignty. The Bible consistently portrays God as the King of the universe, the One who "works all things according to the counsel of his will" (Eph 1:11). He is not a passive observer, but actively governs all things, from the smallest atom to the grandest galaxy.

In relation to salvation, God's sovereignty means that He is the ultimate decisive factor in who is saved. As Paul writes in Romans 9:16, "So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy." This doctrine is often called "monergism," which means that God alone brings about salvation, as opposed to "synergism," which suggests that salvation is a cooperative effort between God and man.

If salvation ultimately depended on human choice, no one would be saved, because in our fallen state, we are enslaved to sin and unable to choose God (Rom 3:10-12). Therefore, if anyone is saved, it is because God has sovereignly chosen to have mercy on them and draw them to Himself (Jn 6:44).

Far from encouraging sin or passivity, the doctrine of God's sovereignty is a great encouragement to holiness and evangelism. If God is sovereign, we can trust that our efforts to live for Him and share the gospel are not in vain, but will bear fruit according to His will (1 Cor 15:58).

Total Inability

The doctrine of Total Inability, also known as Radical Depravity or Total Depravity, is the teaching that, as a consequence of the Fall, every aspect of human nature is corrupted by sin, and we are completely unable to choose God or do anything truly good in His sight apart from His grace.

This doctrine is based on the biblical teaching that all humans are sinners by nature and by choice (Eph 2:1-3; Rom 3:9-18, 23). Our sinfulness is not partial but total, affecting every part of our being - mind, will, emotions, and body. As Jesus said, "out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder, adultery, sexual immorality, theft, false witness, slander" (Mat 15:19).

Importantly, Total Inability does not mean that we are as evil as we could possibly be, or that we cannot do things that are outwardly good. Many non-Christians live morally commendable lives according to human standards. However, even our best actions are tainted by selfish motives, are not for done for God's glory, and fall short of God's perfect standard (Isa 64:6). More crucially, we are unable to understand spiritual truth (1 Cor 2:14), to seek God (Rom 3:11), or to come to Christ for salvation unless the Father draws us (Jn 6:44).

Some object that this doctrine destroys human free will. However, according to biblical categories, our will is not free. It is a slave of sin. Our will, far from being neutral, is opposed to God from birth. Our actions flow from the heart, but because of the Fall, our desires are oriented away from God. So even though we are free to choose what we want and responsible for our choices, the real problem is that what we want is sinful.

Others worry that this doctrine will lead to a fatalistic passivity. If people are naturally unable to follow God, why bother to evangelize or strive for holiness? But God's sovereign grace is the solution to our inability and our evangelism is the means God uses. In His mercy, God regenerates our hearts, enabling us to believe and obey, and the gospel message comes with the power of God to bring a person to faith and everlasting life. Far from encouraging passivity, this truth motivates us to seek God, knowing that if we have love for Him, it is because He loved and worked in us first.

Moreover, the doctrine of Total Inability is essential for a proper understanding of grace. If we think we have some innate ability to choose God, then we inevitably think that our salvation is partly due to our own wisdom and goodness. But if we recognize our complete helplessness apart from God, then we can only attribute our salvation to God's unmerited favor.

Unconditional Election

Unconditional Election is the doctrine that God, before the foundation of the world, chose those whom He would save, not based on any foreseen merit or faith in them, but solely according to His sovereign will to the praise of His glorious grace.

This doctrine is rooted in passages like Ephesians 1:4-6, which states that God "chose us in him before the foundation of the world, that we would be holy and without defect before him in love, having predestined us for adoption as children through Jesus Christ to himself, according to the good pleasure of his desire, to the praise of the glory of his grace, by which he freely gave us favor in the Beloved." Similarly, Romans 9:11 affirms that God chose Jacob over Esau "not because of works but because of him who calls" "in order that God’s purpose of election might continue" (ESV).

Unconditional Election is a corollary of the doctrine of Total Inability. If humans are totally unable to choose God, then the only way any can be saved is if God chooses to save them. This choice is not conditioned on anything in the person, but is an expression of God's free grace.

This doctrine is often misunderstood as arbitrary favoritism. But election is not about God arbitrarily loving some people and hating others, but about God mercifully choosing to save some out of a fallen humanity that universally deserves condemnation for His glory. God is not obligated to save anyone. His choice to save some is an act of pure grace, which we praise and thank Him for.

Moreover, Unconditional Election is the only way that salvation is entirely by grace. If God's choice was based on foreseen faith, then faith would be the ultimate determining factor in salvation, and humans could take some credit for that part of their salvation. But if faith itself is a gift of God given to the elect (Eph 2:8-9), then salvation is entirely of grace from start to finish, from first to last it is entirely of God.

Critics often object that this doctrine is unfair. Why would God choose some and not others? But this objection presupposes that humans deserve salvation. In reality, because of our sin, we all deserve condemnation. That God chooses to save any is a marvel of grace, not a matter of fairness. Fairness is everyone being cast into Gehenna.

Others worry that this doctrine undermines evangelism. If God has already chosen the elect, what's the point of preaching the gospel? But God's sovereign election is the guarantee of evangelism's success. We preach because God uses preaching to call His elect to Himself (Rom 10:14-17). Moreover, since we don't know who the elect are, we must preach the gospel to all, trusting that all who believe are elect and that all the elect will believe.

In the end, Unconditional Election humbles us, reminding us that our salvation is not our own accomplishment, but a gift of God's grace. It comforts us, assuring us that our salvation is not dependent on our creaturely wills, but on God's unchanging purpose. And it motivates us to evangelism and holiness, knowing that God's purpose will triumph.

"I believe the doctrine of election, because I am quite sure that if God had not chosen me I should never have chosen him; and I am sure he chose me before I was born, or else he never would have chosen me afterwards; and he must have elected me for reasons unknown to me, for I never could find any reason in myself why he should have looked upon me with special love." - Charles Spurgeon, Autobiography (ed. Banner of Truth, 1962).

Particular Redemption

Particular Redemption, also known as Limited Atonement, is the doctrine that Christ's death on the cross actually saves the elect, not merely makes salvation possible for people. The key difference between Particular Redemption and unlimited atonement is in the effect of Christ's death. The Reformed argue that Christ's death was for believers and that it actually secures their salvation. It causes at-one-ment with God. Jesus' death propitiated God's wrath, taking the sins of His people on Himself.

This doctrine is based on passages that speak of Christ dying for "his people" (Mat 1:21), "his sheep" (Jn 10:11, 15), "his church" (Acts 20:28; Eph 5:25), and "the elect" (Rom 8:32-34). If Christ died for all people in the same way, then all would be saved, which the Bible clearly denies (Mat 7:13-14). This view goes hand in hand with Penal Substitutionary Atonement.

Moreover, Particular Redemption flows nicely from other Calvinistic doctrines. If God has unconditionally elected some to salvation, and if humans are totally unable to choose God, then it follows that Christ's death must actually secure the salvation of the elect, not just make it possible. The ugly alternative to this is that Christ's death does not actually save, atone, or propiate God's wrath for those whom He died for. Proponents of this view make the effectiveness of Christ's death dependent on our free will choice to follow God rather than God's sovereign election to save those whom He died for.

Some object that Particular Redemption undermines the motivation for evangelism. If Christ has already secured the salvation of the elect, why bother preaching the gospel? But God has ordained the means as well as the ends of salvation. He has decreed that the elect will be saved through the preaching of the gospel (Rom 10:14-17). Moreover, the doctrine of Particular Redemption actually enhances the motivation for evangelism, because it assures us that our preaching will bear fruit among the elect.

Particular Redemption magnifies the grace of our God and the love of Christ. It assures us that Christ's death does amount to a mere possibility of salvation but an actual accomplishment that secured the salvation of His people. It leads us to marvel at the depth of Christ's love for His bride, the church, for whom He gave Himself up (Eph 5:25-27). Particular Redemption is ultimately not about us, but about God. It reminds us that salvation is the Lord's work from beginning to end, and that He will not fail to accomplish all His purposes.

Effectual Calling

Effectual Calling, also known as Irresistible Grace, is the doctrine that when God calls His elect to salvation, He does so in such a way that they inevitably come to faith in Christ. This is distinguished from the general call of the gospel, which goes out to all people but can be resisted.

This doctrine is based on passages that speak of God's calling as an effective, sovereign act that always achieves its purpose. For example, Romans 8:30 says, "Whom he predestined, those he also called. Whom he called, those he also justified." Similarly, John 6:37 says, "All that the Father gives me will come to me," and John 6:44 adds, "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him."

Effectual Calling is a necessary implication of Total Inability and Unconditional Election. If humans are totally unable to choose God, and if God has unconditionally elected some to salvation, then it follows that God must effectually call the elect to faith.

This calling is not a coercion against the will, but a powerful transformation of the will. When God effectually calls us, He gives us a new heart that delights to choose Him (Ezek 36:26-27).

Critics often object that this doctrine destroys human free will and makes people mere puppets. However, we are only truly free when we are liberated from our bondage to sin. Effectual Calling does not override our will, but frees our will to choose God, which is the highest freedom.

Others worry that this doctrine leads to complacency. If God will irresistibly draw His elect to Himself, why bother with evangelism or personal holiness? But God's sovereign calling is the guarantee of our evangelism and sanctification, not an excuse for neglecting them. We evangelize because God uses the preaching of the gospel to call His elect (2 Thes 2:13-14), and we pursue holiness because God's effectual call leads to sanctification (1 Thes 4:7), which confirms our calling and election (2 Pet 1:10).

Effectual Calling is a source of immense comfort and assurance. It reminds us that our salvation rests on the power of God's grace, not the strength of our faith, and that even our faith is a gift of God's grace working in our lives (Eph 2:8-9). It comforts us, by assuring us that God's purpose in our salvation cannot be thwarted. And it motivates us, by showing us that our evangelism and discipleship are not in vain, but are the very means by which God calls and sanctifies His elect.

Let us stand in awe of the God who "calls things that are not as though they were" (Rom 4:17), the God whose call pierces even the deadest of hearts and brings life out of death. This is the God we serve, and this is the God who saves.

Perseverance

Perseverance of the Saints, also known as Preservation of the Saints, is the doctrine that all who are truly saved will persevere in faith to the end and will certainly be saved. It is often misunderstood as "once saved, always saved," but it is more accurately described as "if saved, always saved."

This doctrine is based on passages that emphasize the security of believers and the preserving power of God's grace. Jesus said, "I give eternal life to them. They will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand" (Jn 10:28). Paul wrote, "I am sure of this, that he who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ" (Php 1:6 ESV).

If God has unconditionally elected us, effectually called us, and redeemed us by Christ's blood, then it follows that He will also preserve us to the end because our salvation is not dependent on our own strength, but on God's unchanging grace.

This does not mean that Christians will never struggle with doubt or sin. In fact, all true Christians will struggle with sin throughout their lives (1 Jn 1:8). But they believe that God's grace will always prevail, and that "he who is in you than he who is in the world" (1 Jn 4:4).

The objection has been raised that this doctrine encourages complacency and licentiousness. If we are guaranteed to persevere, why bother to pursue holiness? But this objection misunderstands the nature of perseverance. Perseverance is not a license to sin, but a promise that God will sanctify us.

In other words, perseverance is not just about our eternal security, but about our gradual transformation into the likeness of Christ. God not only guarantees that we will persevere, but He also guarantees that we will grow in holiness. As Paul wrote, "He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it" (1 Thes 5:24 ESV).

Others worry that this doctrine leads to a lack of assurance. How can we know if we are truly saved and will persevere to the end? We can have assurance based on God's promises and the evidence of His grace in our lives. As John wrote, "We know that we have passed out of death into life, because we love the brothers" (1 Jn 3:14).

Ultimately, the doctrine of Perseverance is a source of profound comfort and motivation for believers. It assures us that our salvation is secure, not because of our own strength, but because of God's unbreakable covenant. It motivates us to pursue holiness, not to earn our salvation, but out of gratitude for the salvation we have already received. And it fills us with hope for the future, knowing that "he who promises is faithful" (Heb 10:23).

It is not just a theological doctrine, but a lived reality for every true believer. It is the assurance that "neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord" (Rom 8:38-39). Let us rest in this promise, and let us run with endurance the race that is set before us, looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith (Heb 12:1-2).
