Nicaea

The Council of Nicaea, held in 325 AD, was a pivotal moment in the history of Christianity. It was the first ecumenical council of the Christian Church, convened by the Roman Emperor Constantine I in Nicaea (present-day Iznik, Turkey). This council was primarily called to resolve the Arian controversy and to achieve unity in the Christian Church.

History Behind the Council

Before the Council of Nicaea, Christianity was heavily persecuted within the Roman Empire. However, the conversion of Constantine I to Christianity and his subsequent Edict of Milan in 313 AD granted religious tolerance throughout the empire, dramatically changing Christianity's status and setting the stage for the Council.

The immediate cause of the Council was the Arian controversy, which began in Alexandria when Arius, a presbyter, taught that the Son of God (Jesus Christ) was not co-eternal with the Father, suggesting there was a time when the Son did not exist. This teaching challenged the traditional understanding of the Trinity and created significant theological division within the Church.

The Council of Nicaea

In response to the Arian controversy, Constantine summoned the council, which was attended by approximately 300 bishops from different regions of the empire. The primary objective was to address the relationship between the Father and the Son (Christ) within the Holy Trinity, which Arius had put into question.

The council ultimately condemned Arius and his teachings as heretical, leading to the formulation of the Nicene Creed, a statement of faith that affirmed the co-eternity and consubstantiality (of the same substance) of the Son with the Father. The Creed became a central element of Christian orthodoxy.

After the Council

The Council of Nicaea did not immediately end the Arian controversy. In the years that followed, Arianism continued to have supporters, especially among the Gothic tribes and in certain imperial circles. The debate over the nature of Christ and the Trinity persisted through several subsequent councils.

The Nicene Creed itself underwent further development, particularly at the First Council of Constantinople in 381 AD, which expanded the section on the Holy Spirit and affirmed the Nicene faith against ongoing Arianism.

Arianism

"there was a time when He was not"

Subordinationism: Arius, a presbyter in Alexandria, espoused a form of subordinationism. He argued that the Son (Jesus Christ) was created by the Father and, therefore, was not co-eternal or of the same essence as the Father. Arius used the term "there was a time when He was not" to emphasize that the Son did not exist before being begotten by the Father. This view implied a hierarchy within the Trinity, with the Son being subordinate to the Father.

Never Not the Son

Consubstantiality: The opposing view, which came to be recognized as orthodox, argued that the Son was "consubstantial" (homoousios) with the Father, meaning of the same essence or substance. This term was crucial in expressing the belief that the Son was not a created being but eternally begotten of the Father, thus sharing the same divine nature.

Greek

The term "homoousios" (ὁμοούσιος) was adopted by the Council of Nicaea to affirm that the Son is of the same essence (ousia) as the Father, directly opposing Arianism, which proposed a different nature of Christ in relation to God the Father.

Homoiousios (ὁμοιούσιος): This term means "of similar essence" or "of like substance." While it might seem like a minor linguistic difference from "homoousios," the implication is significant. "Homoiousios" suggests that the Son is similar to, but not exactly the same as, the Father, implying a sort of subordination or difference in essence that could align with Arian views, though not as radically separated as Arianism suggested. This term was preferred by those who sought a middle ground, attempting to maintain a notion of the Son's divinity while also addressing concerns about asserting too direct a parity with the Father, which some feared could imply two co-equal gods and thus challenge monotheism.

Conclusion

The Council's decision to use "homoousios" was pivotal, marking a clear doctrinal stance against Arianism by affirming the full divinity of the Son, equal to that of the Father, thereby maintaining the unity and co-eternality of the Godhead. This choice underscored the rejection of any form of subordinationism or suggestion that the Son was a lesser or different kind of divine being than the Father. The debate over these terms was not merely semantic but touched on the very heart of Christian understanding of the nature of God, the relationship between the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, and the mystery of the Incarnation.

Manuscipts

Homer’s Iliad: One of the best-attested ancient texts outside the New Testament, with about 1,757 surviving manuscripts. However, the earliest complete copies of the Iliad are from the 10th century AD, almost 500 years after the New Testament's earliest manuscripts, and over 2,000 years after the Iliad was composed.

Works of Plato: There are fewer than 210 manuscripts, with the earliest copies dating from about 900 years after the original compositions.

Tacitus’ Annals: Only two major manuscripts that contain portions of Tacitus’ work, with the earliest from the 9th century, which is about 700 years after Tacitus wrote.

Caesar’s Gallic Wars: About ten manuscripts are known, with the earliest copies dating to 900 years after Caesar's time.

NT

P52 (Rylands Library Papyrus P52): Dated to around 125 AD, this small fragment contains parts of John 18:31-33, 37-38. It is the earliest known NT manuscript, providing a crucial link to the text's original form.

P66 (Papyrus Bodmer II): From around 200 AD, this nearly complete codex contains a large part of the Gospel of John. It is written in a professional hand and demonstrates the transition from scroll to codex form.

P46 (Chester Beatty Papyrus II): Dated to around 200 AD, it is one of the oldest manuscripts of the epistles of Paul, containing almost all of them with some lacunae.

Preservation

Quantity: There are over 5,800 known Greek manuscripts of the New Testament, in addition to thousands more in Latin, Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, and other languages. This makes the New Testament the best-attested ancient document.

Time Gap: The earliest New Testament manuscript fragments date from about 125 AD, with significant portions of the New Testament available from the 3rd and 4th centuries. This is within 100 years of the original compositions for the earliest fragments.

Quality: The quality of the manuscripts varies, with some being partial or fragmentary and others being more complete. Significant efforts have been made over the centuries to preserve, copy, and correct these manuscripts.

OT

Masoretic

The most authoritative Hebrew text of the Old Testament is the Masoretic Text (MT), compiled and preserved by Jewish scribes known as Masoretes between the 7th and 10th centuries CE. The Masoretes not only copied the text with meticulous care but also developed a system of vocalization (adding vowels to the previously consonant-only text) and cantillation marks for pronunciation and syntax. They added marginal notes called "Masorah" to preserve details about the text's pronunciation, spelling, and usage. The Leningrad Codex (circa 1008 CE) and the Aleppo Codex (circa 920 CE) are among the oldest and most complete manuscripts of the Masoretic Text.

Dead Sea

The discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls in the mid-20th century at Qumran, near the Dead Sea, significantly impacted the study of the Old Testament. Dating from the 3rd century BCE to the 1st century CE, these scrolls include the oldest known manuscripts of the Hebrew Bible. They contain partial or complete copies of almost every book in the Hebrew Bible, plus other texts not included in the canonical Hebrew Bible. The Dead Sea Scrolls have provided valuable insights into the textual diversity and development of the Old Testament before the standardization of the Masoretic Text.

Septuagint

The Septuagint (LXX) is a significant ancient Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, completed by Jewish scholars in Alexandria, Egypt, between the 3rd and 1st centuries BCE. It includes translations of all the books found in the Hebrew Bible, along with additional texts not included in the canon of the Hebrew Bible but recognized in various Christian Old Testament canons. The Septuagint is a crucial source for understanding the text of the Old Testament, especially where it preserves readings older or divergent from the Masoretic Text.
