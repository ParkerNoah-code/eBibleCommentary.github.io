What is Theology?

The word "theology" is commonly used to describe a more theoretical displine looking at the existence and sovereignty of God, creation, and other similar topics. Christian theology considers matters from the vantage point of Scripture and is concerned with a broader range of topics like ethics, Bible translation, and redemption:

Theology is the science of God and of the relations between God and the creation. Theology is the science of the sciences because it reveals the underlying ground which all other sciences require for their complete explanation. Theology binds and orients.

Christian theology is the application of Scripture by believers to all aspects of life.1 Theology has to do with the good, the truth, and the beautiful. It involves the whole of the believer: the head, the heart, and the hands (what we think, how we feel, and what we do). It's goal is to allow us to know and serve God, to meet the spiritual needs of his people and the world, and to promote godliness.

Methodological Points

Theology must be centered on the Bible and its application. Adequate theological arguments must show their biblical basis to be persuasive and authoritative within the Christian community. Without this connection, theological ideas risk becoming disconnected from the foundational truths of the Christian faith. Biblical application involves discerning how biblical truths speak to contemporary issues, guide moral decisions, and shape Christian living.

While the Bible is the primary source for Christian theology, understanding it fully often requires insights from various other fields of knowledge. For example, knowledge of the biblical languages (Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek) is significant for accurate interpretation of the biblical texts. Similarly, understanding the cultural and historical context of the Bible enhances our ability to grasp its meaning. Creeds and confessions are distilled reflections of centuries of theological thought and debate. Fields such as psychology, sociology, politics, and the natural sciences provide insights into the human condition and the world that can inform how theology is applied today

In recent times, there has been a tendency in theological scholarship to prioritize historical and philosophical analysis over direct engagement with Scripture. This shift has led to a form of theology that is more concerned with understanding past theological thought or engaging with contemporary philosophical ideas than with directly applying biblical truths to life. Theologians should ensure that they remain rooted and grounded in the Bible.

The current system for training theologians, particularly in liberal universities, emphasizes autonomous reasoning over the application of Scripture, leading to a disconnect between academic theology and the needs of the church. Evangelical theologians who are trained in such environments may struggle to reconcile their academic training with the expectations of the church. This tension can contribute to doctrinal drift within evangelical institutions, as the pressures to conform to secular academic norms may overshadow the commitment to biblical fidelity.

Evangelical institutions should develop new methods for training theologians which prioritize deep engagement with the Bible. This would involve a shift away from the current focus on auxiliary disciplines and toward a more Scripture-centered approach. To achieve this, evangelical institutions may need to distance themselves from the secular academic establishment, including potentially rejecting the standards and accreditation systems that require conformity to secular academic norms.

Finally, while academic rigor is important in theology, it is not sufficient on its own. The task of applying Scripture requires more than intellectual ability; it demands a personal relationship with Jesus Christ, characterized by love, devotion, and a desire to follow Him.

A theologian must also possess a prayerful spirit, meaning they should approach their work with humility and dependence on God, seeking His guidance in understanding and applying His Word. Additionally, they must be attuned to the needs of the people they serve, understanding the real-life issues and struggles that believers face.

This holistic approach to theology recognizes that true wisdom comes from both the mind and the heart and leads to action. The best theological work is done by those who not only understand the Bible intellectually but who also live out its teachings in their own lives and seek to help others do the same. This ensures that theology remains relevant and practical, serving the church and its mission in the world.
