Calvinism

The Gospel

The Gospel as the good news of salvation through Jesus Christ, who reconciled sinful men to a holy God.

There are fundamentally two types of religion: the religion of faith and the religion of works. Calvinism is portrayed as the purest form of the religion of faith, while Arminianism is seen as compromised by the religion of works, making it unstable.

Sovereignty

God's will is supreme and unconditional, independent of creation and stemming solely from His eternal counsel. God determines the course of nature and history down to the smallest details. His decrees are eternal, unchanging, holy, wise, and sovereign.

Man's Helpless Condition

In his fallen state, man fears and hates God, and cannot seek Him on his own. Only a supernatural act of God can rescue him from this condition. God must take the initiative to pay the penalty, cleanse from guilt, and restore righteousness. This is what God does for the elect - those chosen by God for salvation. Christ, as God incarnate, lived a sinless life and suffered the penalty for sin on behalf of His people. His perfect obedience earned righteousness for them, and His suffering was equivalent to what they would have endured in hell.

As the benefits of Christ's redemptive work are applied to those chosen by the Father, they are regenerated by the Holy Spirit - made spiritually alive or "born again."

Jesus taught that one must be born again to see the kingdom of God. The unregenerate cannot understand spiritual things on their own. Just as a physically dead person cannot give themselves life, a spiritually dead person cannot give themselves spiritual life. This requires a supernatural act of God. The Holy Spirit sovereignly takes the sinner out of Satan's kingdom and places them in God's kingdom through spiritual rebirth.

Once a person is spiritually reborn into God's kingdom, they cannot become "unborn." Just as it took supernatural action to bring them to spiritual life, it would require another supernatural act to remove them from that state. This leads to the doctrine of eternal security or perseverance of the saints. This gift of eternal life is not given to all people, but only to those God chooses. However, this doesn't mean that anyone who wants to be saved is excluded. The invitation in Revelation 22:17 is open to "whoever will."

As Jesus states in John 6:44, no one can come to Him unless drawn by the Father. Only those made spiritually alive by the Holy Spirit have this desire; these are the elect.

In contrast, there are the non-elect. God doesn't actively prevent their salvation but simply leaves their sinful nature unchanged. The idea of a cruel God refusing to save willing people is a mischaracterization. God saves all who want to be saved, but only those whose nature has been changed by God actually want salvation.

Atonement

The atonement is either highly efficient and perfectly accomplished for some, or widely extended but imperfectly accomplished. The concept of unlimited atonement would mean God punishes sins twice for the lost: in Christ and in themselves.

God's sovereignty in salvation and punishment and the ultimate purpose of creation is for the displaying God's glory and attributes.

Sin's existence allows for the revelation of God's most glorious attributes - grace, mercy, love, and holiness - as displayed in His redemption of sinners.

Foreknowledge

The evangelical Arminian acknowledges God's foreknowledge and ability to predict future events. However, this creates a logical problem: if God foreknows an event, it becomes as fixed and certain as if it were foreordained.

Arminians typically explain election as based on God's foreknowledge - God elects those He foresees will respond to His offer of salvation. However, this explanation is problematic. If God foresees who will be saved, He also foresees who will be lost. Why does God create those He knows will be lost? He's not obligated to create them, and if He truly wants all to be saved, He could refrain from creating those He knows will be lost.

The question remains: Why would God create those He knows will go to hell? The Arminian is left with two problematic options: Deny God's foreknowledge, which results in a limited, ignorant, finite God, or Accept an election based on foreknowledge, which becomes meaningless - God electing and choosing those who elect and choose God first.

The Big Three

The Arminian view often cites universalistic passages in Scripture to support their position. The three most commonly quoted verses are:

  • "...not willing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance" (2 Peter 3:9)
  • "Who will have all men to be saved, and to come unto the knowledge of the truth" (1 Timothy 2:4)
  • "...Christ Jesus; Who gave himself a ransom for all..." (1 Timothy 2:5-6)

These verses teach God's benevolence, not that He delights in His creatures' suffering. The word "will" in Scripture can mean "desire" or "purpose," similar to how a judge may not desire to punish but still sentences the guilty.

2 Peter 3:9 is addressed to Christians ("them that have obtained like precious faith with us" - 2 Peter 1:1), not mankind at large. It's primarily about Christ's second coming, not salvation. The "us-ward" refers to the elect, and could be read as "not willing that any of us should perish, but that all of us should come to repentance."

1 Timothy 2:4-6 uses "all" to mean all types of people, not every individual. This interpretation is based on historical context:

  • Jews had long been the primary recipients of God's grace.
  • Early Christians initially limited the Messiah's mission to themselves.
  • Gentile salvation was a "mystery" (Ephesians 4:6; Colossians 1:27).
  • Jewish exclusivism regarded Gentiles as "unclean" or "sinners" (John 4:9, Acts 10:28, 11:3).

"All" in Scripture often means "many" or "representative," not literally everyone:

  • Mark 1:5: "All" of Judaea and Jerusalem were baptized by John.
  • Acts 4:21: "All men" glorified God after a healing.
  • Luke 21:17: Disciples would be hated by "all men."

John 12:32: Jesus drawing "all men" means people from all nations and races, not every individual.

1 Corinthians 15:22: "For as in Adam all die, so also in Christ shall all be made alive" refers to resurrection life for those in Christ, not universal salvation.

Revelation 3:20 and 22:17: These invitations are extended to all, but fallen man is spiritually unable to respond without the Holy Spirit's regeneration.

Revelation 5:9: The redeemed are "out of every kindred, and tongue, and people, and nation," indicating Christ died for all types of people, not necessarily every individual.

John 6:37: "All" can mean all the elect or all those given to Christ by the Father.

System Thinking

Weak faith and superficial Christianity often result from not seeing the logical consistency of this system. Christians should understand not just that they're forgiven, but how and why their redemption was accomplished and made effective.

Historically, the five points of Calvinism have been held by Presbyterian and Reformed churches and many Baptists, while Arminianism has been prevalent in Methodist and Lutheran churches, as well as among many Baptists.

The 5 Points of Arminianism

Free will: The concept suggests that despite the negative impact of humanity's fall from grace, people still retain some spiritual agency. The divine offers everyone the opportunity for redemption and faith, but doesn't force these choices upon individuals. Each person has autonomy in their decisions, and their ultimate fate is determined by how they exercise this freedom. This freedom includes the capacity to choose righteousness over wrongdoing in matters of the spirit. A person's will isn't completely bound by their inherent sinful nature. Individuals have the ability to either work alongside divine guidance and experience spiritual renewal, or to reject divine assistance and face spiritual demise. While those who are spiritually lost benefit from divine support, they don't necessarily need to be spiritually reborn before they can have faith. The act of believing is seen as a human action that comes before spiritual rebirth. In this view, faith is portrayed as humanity's offering to the divine, representing the individual's personal contribution to their own salvation.

Conditional election: This concept proposes that divine selection of individuals for salvation was based on foreknowledge of their future responses to spiritual calling. The idea suggests that only those who would independently choose to believe were selected. In this view, the act of divine election was contingent upon human actions rather than being an unconditional choice. The faith that formed the basis of this selection is seen not as a divine gift or the result of spiritual regeneration, but as a product of human free will alone. This perspective places the ultimate decision for belief, and consequently for election to salvation, entirely in human hands. It suggests that the divine chose individuals based on the foresight of who would voluntarily choose to follow spiritual teachings. Therefore, according to this concept, it's the individual's choice to believe - not divine selection - that is considered the primary cause of salvation.

General atonement: This concept proposes that the sacrificial work of Christ created the potential for universal salvation, but didn't guarantee salvation for any specific individual. The idea suggests that while Christ's death was for all of humanity, salvation is only realized by those who actively choose to believe. In this view, Christ's sacrifice allows for the possibility of divine forgiveness, but this forgiveness is conditional upon individual belief. The atonement itself is not seen as directly removing anyone's transgressions. Instead, it creates the opportunity for forgiveness, which must then be actively embraced by each person. The redemption only becomes active and applicable when an individual decides to accept it.

Uneffectual call: This concept suggests that the Holy Spirit's influence can be effectively resisted by individuals. According to this view, the Spirit internally calls all those who receive the external invitation of the gospel. The Spirit is seen as doing everything possible to guide each person towards salvation. Faith, viewed here as a human contribution, is seen as a prerequisite for spiritual regeneration. The Spirit is portrayed as only being able to draw to Christ those who willingly allow themselves to be influenced. As a result, divine grace is not seen as irresistible or invincible. Instead, it's portrayed as something that can be—and often is—resisted and counteracted by human choice.

Losing salvation: This concept, known as "falling from grace," suggests that individuals who have genuinely believed and been saved can potentially lose their salvation if they fail to maintain their faith. However, it's important to note that not all who adhere to Arminian theology agree on this point. Some maintain that believers are eternally secure in Christ, meaning that once a person is spiritually regenerated, they can never lose their salvation. According to the Arminian view, salvation is achieved through a collaborative effort between God, who initiates the process, and humans, who must respond. The human response is seen as the determining factor in this process. At the critical juncture, human will is viewed as playing a decisive role. As a result, this suggests that it is ultimately the individual, rather than God, who determines who will receive the gift of salvation.

The 5 Points of Calvinism

Total depravity: Due to humanity's fallen nature, individuals are inherently incapable of choosing salvation on their own. This doctrine posits that humans are spiritually dead, unable to perceive or respond to divine truth, and possess a corrupted will bound to evil. Consequently, salvation requires more than mere assistance from the Holy Spirit; it necessitates complete spiritual regeneration. In this view, faith is not a human contribution to salvation but rather a divine gift, integral to God's salvific work in the believer's life.

Unconditional Election: This posits that God's selection of individuals for salvation is based solely on His sovereign will, rather than any foreseen merit or action of the chosen. This doctrine asserts that God's election occurs before the world's creation and is not influenced by anticipated faith, repentance, or any other human response. Instead, God bestows faith and repentance upon those He has chosen. Thus, these spiritual acts are consequences of God's election, not its cause. This view emphasizes that salvation ultimately stems from God's choice of the individual, rather than the individual's choice of God, highlighting divine sovereignty in the salvation process.

Particular redemption: Christ's sacrificial death was specifically intended for and effective only for the elect. This view holds that Jesus' atonement fully secured salvation for those chosen by God, including the gift of faith necessary for their redemption. It argues that Christ's death was a substitutionary punishment, bearing the penalty of sin for specific individuals rather than for all of humanity. The doctrine maintains that the Holy Spirit unfailingly applies the gift of faith to all those for whom Christ died, thereby ensuring their salvation. This concept emphasizes the efficacy and particularity of Christ's redemptive work for the elect.

Irresistible grace: God's saving grace is irresistibly applied to the elect. This concept distinguishes between an external call to salvation available to all who hear the Gospel, which can be rejected, and an internal call exclusive to the elect that invariably leads to salvation. The doctrine holds that the Holy Spirit draws the elect to Christ in a manner that cannot be resisted or thwarted by human will. It emphasizes that God's grace operates independently of human cooperation, effectively causing the elect to willingly embrace faith, repentance, and Christ. This view underscores the invincible nature of divine grace in securing the salvation of those to whom it is extended.

Perseverance of the saints: Those chosen by God, redeemed by Christ, and given faith by the Holy Spirit are eternally saved and will persevere in faith until the end. This doctrine emphasizes that salvation is entirely the work of the triune God: the Father's election, the Son's redemption, and the Spirit's effectual calling. It maintains that God's power preserves believers in faith, ensuring their ultimate salvation. This view underscores that the entire salvation process is by God's grace alone, with God determining the recipients of salvation. The passage concludes with an evangelical appeal, urging those who haven't accepted Christ to seek God's mercy and put their trust in Jesus for salvation, citing biblical promises of God's forgiveness and assurance to those who believe.
