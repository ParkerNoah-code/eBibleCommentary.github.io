The Doctrine of the Word of God

Purpose: Nature shows God's attributes, but not enough for salvation. Thus, God revealed Himself in various ways, and the Bible provides all necessary knowledge for God's glory, salvation, and living in faith.

Attributes: The Bible, consisting of the Old and New Testaments, is the inspired and infallible Word of God, authoritative in matters of faith and practice, clear to understand with the Holy Spirit's aid, and without the need for any infallible human interpreter.

Canon: The Bible, the written Word of God, contains the Old and New Testaments. The books commonly called the Apocrypha were not inspired and are not part of the Bible canon. Therefore, they should be viewed and used as other merely human writings.

Proofs: Belief in the Bible's divine origin is due to the Holy Spirit's testimony within us, supported by the church's testimony, its heavenly content, effective teachings, stylistic majesty, harmonious parts, focus on God's glory, and many other incomparable qualities and complete perfections.

Rule of Faith: The Bible alone is the complete revelation from God intended to be the standard for faith and practice. This excludes all unwritten traditions and decrees of the visible Church; all resolutions of conventions, or other public bodies, declaring this or that to be right or wrong, true or false.

Hermeneutics: The words of Scripture are to be taken in the sense attached to them in the age and by the people to whom they were addressed, and under the Holy Spirit's guidance. God cannot teach in one place anything which is inconsistent with what He teaches in another.

Translation: The Bible was written in Hebrew and Greek, preserved by God over time, and should be translated into all languages. This way, the Bible can dwell richly in everyone, and everyone can both worship God acceptably and have hope through the patience and comfort of the Bible.

Textual Criticism: The Church acknowledges that translations and existing copies of original texts are not verbally infallible and do contain discrepancies, yet these do not compromise essential Christian doctrines. Modern textual criticism seeks to restore the most accurate text.

Objections: Difficulties in understanding the Bible are expected and do not invalidate its divine inspiration, no more than mysteries in nature disprove divine creation. Many of these challenges diminish over time, are solved, but some remain that serve to deepen inquiry and faith.
