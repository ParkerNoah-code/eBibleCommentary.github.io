Sola Ecclesia

The Roman church claims far-reaching authority for itself as the only proper identifier and interpreter of Scripture and Tradition, which is considered to be the written and unwritten Word of God respectively. It teaches that those who presume to be able to interpret Scripture or the Fathers for themselves are mistaken; it belongs to the church alone to judge the true sense and interpretation of such writings. In terms of ultimate authority, this represents Sola Ecclesia, the belief that the Roman church alone is the supreme authority for all Christian faith and practice in contrast to the Protestant belief in Sola Scriptura, that the Scriptures alone are the sole sufficient and infallible grounds for all Christian faith and practice.

Fourth Session Council of Trent, Decree, Concerning the Canonical Scriptures, 1546:

Furthermore, to check unbridled spirits, it decrees that no one relying on his own judgment shall, in matters of faith and morals pertaining to the edification of Christian doctrine, distorting the Holy Scriptures in accordance with his own conceptions, presume to interpret them contrary to that sense which holy mother Church, to whom it belongs to judge of their true sense and interpretation, has held and holds, or even contrary to the unanimous teaching of the Fathers, even though such interpretations should never at any time be published. Those who act contrary to this shall be made known by the ordinaries and punished in accordance with the penalties prescribed by the law.

Second Vatican Council, Dei Verbum, 1965:

But the task of authentically interpreting the word of God, whether written or handed on, has been entrusted exclusively to the living teaching office of the Church, whose authority is exercised in the name of Jesus Christ. This teaching office is not above the word of God, but serves it, teaching only what has been handed on, listening to it devoutly, guarding it scrupulously and explaining it faithfully in accord with a divine commission and with the help of the Holy Spirit, it draws from this one deposit of faith everything which it presents for belief as divinely revealed.

It is clear, therefore, that sacred tradition, Sacred Scripture and the teaching authority of the Church, in accord with God's most wise design, are so linked and joined together that one cannot stand without the others, and that all together and each in its own way under the action of the one Holy Spirit contribute effectively to the salvation of souls.

The Roman Catholic Church (RCC) points to Scripture and Tradition to defend its complete and comprehensive authority over the Church. However, when one looks in Scripture, no teaching about an infallible teaching office can be found. The RCC also forbids its people from interpreting the Bible in any way contrary to its official declarations. The faithful Papist looks at the text of Scripture and only finds support for the RCC's authority claims because they are forbidden from any other interpretation and only allowed to follow what the RCC says it says. A similar analysis holds for Tradition. The RCC defines what counts and does not count as Tradition and arrogates only to itself the right to interpret said Tradition.

Thus, defenses of Roman Catholicism and its beliefs and practices always boil down to, "Because the RCC says so." Rather than being a three-legged stool based on church heirarchy, Tradition, and Scripture, the RCC is a leaning tower of Piza held up solely by weak and baseless authority claims. The fundamental difference between Protestants and Catholics is not based on what the text of Scripture actually says or what we actually find in church history. Both of those clearly contradict the claims of the Papists. The real dividing line is one's ultimate authority and allegiance: one must either follow Scripture above all else or follow the teachings of the Popes above all else. One cannot follow both.
