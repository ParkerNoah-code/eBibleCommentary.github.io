The Nature of True Knowledge of God

False Knowledge and Gnosticism

In 1 Timothy 6:20–21, Paul warns against “what is falsely called knowledge,” a reference to proto-Gnostic teachings that emerged fully in the second century A.D. These teachings urged believers to move beyond their Christian faith toward an esoteric “knowledge” that promised spiritual enlightenment. However, such knowledge devalued the material world, regarded the body as a prison for the soul, and offered mystical practices and secret insights as the path to spiritual fulfillment.

Gnostic teachers redefined Jesus as merely a supernatural teacher, denying both His Incarnation and His Atonement. They replaced His call to holy living with either asceticism or licentiousness. Paul’s letters to Timothy, along with writings by Jude, Peter, and John, explicitly reject these heretical beliefs and practices (e.g., 1 Tim. 4:1–7; Jude 4, 8–19; 2 Peter 2; 1 John 4:1–6). These epistles affirm the centrality of Christ’s person and work, the integrity of creation, and the transformative power of true faith.

Knowing God Through Faith

Scripture presents “knowing” God as the ideal for the spiritual person. This knowledge arises from a faith-relationship that brings salvation, eternal life, and produces love, hope, obedience, and joy. As Jeremiah 9:24 states, true boasting lies in understanding and knowing God, who delights in kindness, justice, and righteousness. Knowing God involves intellectual, volitional, and moral dimensions:

  • Intellectual: Understanding the truth about God’s nature and works (Deut. 7:9; Ps. 100:3).
  • Volitional: Trusting, obeying, and worshiping God based on that truth.
  • Moral: Living out justice, love, and righteousness in one’s life (Jer. 22:16; 1 John 4:7–8).

Faith-knowledge centers on Jesus Christ, the incarnate God and mediator between humanity and the Father. Through Christ, believers come to know God as their Father, seeking deeper knowledge of Christ and His power (John 14:6; Phil. 3:8–14).

The Fruit of Regeneration and Illumination

True knowledge of God is the fruit of regeneration, in which God bestows a new heart (Jer. 24:7; 1 John 5:20), and illumination by the Holy Spirit (2 Cor. 4:6; Eph. 1:17). This knowledge creates a reciprocal relationship between God and His people: we know God as our own because He knows us as His (John 10:14; Gal. 4:9; 2 Tim. 2:19).

All Scripture has been given to facilitate this knowledge-relationship with God. Believers are called to labor in studying and applying Scripture to grow in their understanding of and communion with God, fulfilling its ultimate purpose.
