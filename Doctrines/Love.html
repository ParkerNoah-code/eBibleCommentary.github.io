Loving Is Basic to Christian Behavior

The Foundation of Christian Love

New Testament Christianity is fundamentally a response to the revelation of God as a God of love. God, in His nature as a tripersonal Being, expresses love in a way that is unparalleled in human experience. The Father loves the ungodly so much that He has given His Son, Jesus Christ, to die for them. In turn, the Son gives His life, and together, the Father and the Son send the Holy Spirit to save sinners from eternal separation and lead them to eternal glory. This amazing reality of divine love, which begins with God's sacrificial love for us, becomes the foundation and motivation for Christians to love God and their neighbors.

This divine love is not only the basis for salvation but also the ongoing fuel for Christian life and behavior. The love that Christians are called to express is a direct reflection of the love God has shown to us. As Christians experience God's love in their lives, they are moved to love others—first and foremost God Himself, and then their neighbors, as Christ commanded (Matt. 22:35–40). The love Christians show is a response of gratitude for God's gracious love toward them and is meant to be modeled on the selfless love demonstrated by Christ.

The Hallmark of Christian Life: Love

Love is the hallmark of Christian life. As 1 John 5:3 and John 14:15, 21, 23 highlight, the measure of our love for God is seen in our wholehearted and unqualified obedience to His commands. A true expression of love for God involves not only affection but a deep, abiding commitment to follow His will. Similarly, the measure of love for our neighbors is not only in what we feel but in what we do. The ultimate test of love for others is the willingness to lay down our lives for them (1 John 3:16; John 15:12–13). This sacrificial love entails giving, spending, and even impoverishing ourselves for the well-being of others, reflecting the love Christ demonstrated for us.

Jesus’ story of the Good Samaritan, found in Luke 10:25–37, serves as a profound illustration of neighbor-love. In this parable, the Samaritan shows mercy to a Jewish man who, in cultural norms, would have been considered his enemy. The Samaritan’s love transcends societal boundaries, showing that true neighbor-love is not determined by who the other person is but by the need they have. Love for one's neighbor is self-sacrificial, seeking the good of others even at personal cost.

Neighbor-Love as Described in 1 Corinthians 13

The apostle Paul’s description of love in 1 Corinthians 13:4–7 offers a clear and challenging picture of what neighbor-love looks like in action. It is not based on emotion or feelings but is characterized by active service. Love is patient and kind, not envious, proud, or self-seeking. It does not boast or delight in evil, but it rejoices in the truth. True love always protects, always trusts, always hopes, and always perseveres. This love is radical in its focus on the well-being of the other person, devoid of any selfish motivation. It is a love that seeks the good of others above all else.

The lack of self-concern in love, as outlined in 1 Corinthians 13, is breathtaking. Neighbor-love, as defined by Jesus and modeled by the apostle Paul, is not about what we get from others, but what we give to them. It is a love that requires effort, sacrifice, and sometimes even personal discomfort, all for the sake of another’s good. The true measure of love is not how much we receive but how much we are willing to give.

Love as a Principle of Action

Love is not merely a feeling but a principle of action. It is a purposeful decision to honor and benefit others, driven by compassion for their needs. It is a commitment to do things for others, even when personal affection or sentiment is not present. Christian love is not contingent on how others behave or how we feel in the moment, but on the need to demonstrate Christ-like care for them.

Jesus made it clear that love is to be the defining characteristic of His followers. In John 13:34–35, He commands His disciples to love one another as He has loved them, and by this love, the world will know that they are His disciples. This active love, expressed in service to one another, is the outward sign of an inward transformation that reflects God's love for humanity.

Conclusion: The Call to Love

Christian love is not an optional or secondary virtue but the very essence of what it means to live as a follower of Christ. It is a love that mirrors God’s love for us—selfless, sacrificial, and unconditional. This love is active, not passive; it is demonstrated through deeds, not just words. Christians are called to love God with all their heart, soul, and mind, and to love their neighbors as themselves. This love, empowered by the Holy Spirit, is the foundation for all Christian behavior and is the hallmark of true discipleship.
