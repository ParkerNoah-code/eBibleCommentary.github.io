God Makes His People His Children

The Doctrine of Adoption

The Apostle Paul presents the doctrine of adoption as a central aspect of the Christian’s relationship with God. In Galatians 4:4-5, Paul teaches that through Christ, believers receive the full rights of sonship: "But when the fullness of time had come, God sent his Son, born of a woman, born under the law ... so that we might receive adoption as children." Adoption, in this context, refers to being granted a new status—being brought into the family of God as sons and daughters, not by natural birth, but through God's gracious act of adopting sinners into His family.

In the cultural context of the New Testament, adoption typically involved young adult males of good character, chosen to maintain the family name and inherit the wealth of childless families. However, Paul radically expands this concept, proclaiming that God adopts people of bad character, sinners, into His family. They are made heirs of God and co-heirs with Christ (Rom. 8:17), despite their undeserving status. This teaching underscores the generosity and grace of God in accepting believers, not because of their merit, but purely through His mercy and the work of Jesus Christ.

Adoption: A Crowning Blessing

Justification—the declaration that a believer is declared righteous before God—is the foundational blessing that enables adoption. Without justification, there would be no access to the status of sonship. Yet, adoption is considered the crowning blessing, as it establishes a personal and intimate relationship with God as Father. Adoption is not just a legal term; it signifies a deep, relational connection that transforms the believer’s identity and position in God's family. Through Jesus Christ, believers are granted this intimate relationship, and they are loved by God as He loves His own Son, Jesus Christ (Rom. 8:17).

This adoption entails being under God’s fatherly care, protection, and discipline. Believers are now called to live in light of this new identity, recognizing that God is their Father. They are encouraged to pray to Him as such (Matt. 6:5-13), imitate His character (Matt. 5:44-48; Eph. 4:32–5:2), and trust in His providence and care (Matt. 6:25–34). This new filial relationship is not a passive status but a dynamic calling that involves actively participating in the life of the family of God.

Adoption and Regeneration: Two Aspects of Salvation

Adoption and regeneration are distinct yet closely related aspects of the salvation Christ offers. While adoption refers to the bestowal of a new relationship with God, regeneration pertains to the transformation of the believer’s moral nature. In regeneration, the believer is spiritually renewed and made alive to God, receiving a new heart that desires to follow His will. Adoption, on the other hand, confers a new status and relationship with God as Father. Though they are distinct, both aspects of salvation are inseparable, for God desires His adopted children to reflect His character. Thus, the transformation in moral nature (regeneration) works hand-in-hand with the new identity as a child of God (adoption), as the believer is called to live in a way that mirrors God's holiness.

The Fatherly Care of God

Being adopted into God’s family is not just a theological truth but a practical reality that affects the believer’s daily life. As children of God, believers are under His care and discipline. Hebrews 12:5-11 speaks of God's fatherly discipline, which is not punitive but corrective, aiming to train and shape His children into the likeness of Christ. This discipline, though sometimes difficult, is a sign of God's love and care for His children. It is a sign of His investment in their growth, purity, and holiness.

As God’s children, believers are encouraged to live in a manner worthy of their calling, imitating their heavenly Father. This means living with love, forgiveness, and mercy, as God has shown these qualities to His children (Matt. 5:44–48; Eph. 4:32–5:2). The Christian life, then, is not simply about doctrinal knowledge but about reflecting the love and holiness of God as His beloved children. This dynamic relationship forms the core of Christian living and discipleship.

Conclusion: The Joy of Adoption

The doctrine of adoption brings believers into the fullness of God’s love and care. Through Christ, God does not merely forgive sins but invites believers into His family, making them His sons and daughters. This intimate relationship is a profound privilege and a source of joy, as believers experience the security and affection of being loved by God as their Father. Adoption transforms not only the believer's legal status but their entire identity, calling them to live in a manner that reflects their new family relationship with God. It is a relational, transforming gift that makes God’s people heirs of His promises and co-heirs with Christ.
