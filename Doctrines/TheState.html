Christians Must Respect Civil Government

The Role of Civil Government in God’s Order

In Romans 13:1–2, the Apostle Paul writes, “Everyone must submit himself to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except that which God has established. The authorities that exist have been established by God. Consequently, he who rebels against the authority is rebelling against what God has instituted, and those who do so will bring judgment on themselves.” This passage clearly emphasizes the divine origin of civil authority and the Christian responsibility to respect and obey the governing powers. Civil government is part of God’s ordained order, created to maintain peace, justice, and order within society.

Civil government, like other institutions such as the church, the family, and education, functions as part of God’s "common grace." These structures of authority provide a bulwark against chaos and lawlessness in a fallen world. Civil authorities have a responsibility to act for the public good, and their role is to protect citizens from harm, punish wrongdoers, and maintain the general welfare of society. However, each of these institutions must operate within its defined sphere and should not overstep its boundaries, as Christ is the ultimate ruler over all.

The Purpose of Civil Government

According to the Westminster Confession, civil magistrates are ordained by God “for his own glory, and the public good.” Their authority is derived from God, and they are entrusted with the power of the sword to enforce laws, protect the innocent, and punish evildoers (Rom. 13:4). Christians are called to respect and obey the government because it serves as a means of God's order in society. However, civil authorities do not have the right to wield this power in ways that violate God's moral law or to use it for purposes contrary to His will.

While Christians are to submit to the governing authorities, civil authorities should never use their power to persecute any religion or to promote evil practices. Governments should not compel believers to act against their conscience or to violate the commands of God. When the state requires what God forbids or forbids what God commands, Christians are called to engage in civil disobedience, accepting the consequences of their actions as a witness to God’s higher authority (Acts 4:18–31; 5:17–29). The Christian’s duty is to obey the government insofar as it does not conflict with God’s law.

Christians' Responsibility to Civil Government

Christians are to respect civil authority, pray for their leaders, and encourage them to fulfill their God-ordained roles. In 1 Timothy 2:1–4, Paul urges believers to pray for all those in authority, so that they may lead in a way that promotes peace, justice, and the well-being of society. Christians should be engaged in the public life of their communities, advocating for justice and righteousness, while also reminding governments of their responsibility to serve the common good.

At the same time, Christians must be vigilant against any form of tyranny or overreach by the government. While democratic systems that limit the power of rulers may be more effective in preventing tyranny, the Christian duty to oppose injustice remains essential. Christians must strive to live in peace with the government while holding it accountable to God’s moral order. This balance ensures that civil authority serves the public good and operates within its rightful boundaries.

The Relationship Between Church and State

The Bible makes it clear that civil government is distinct from the church. While civil authorities have the power to govern, they are not to assume responsibilities that belong to the church, such as administering the Word or the sacraments (Westminster Confession XXIII.3). The church is called to preach the gospel and administer the sacraments, while the state is responsible for the administration of justice and the well-being of society. Both are part of God’s order, and each has its own distinct role.

The state’s role is to maintain order and enforce justice, while the church’s role is to preach the gospel, make disciples, and foster spiritual growth. This separation ensures that the state does not interfere with the religious life of the church, while the church remains free to carry out its mission without undue influence from the state.

Conclusion: Christians and Their Role in Society

Christians are called to respect and submit to civil government as part of God’s ordained order, recognizing that civil authority is established by God for the common good. However, Christians are also called to engage in society with a sense of moral responsibility, ensuring that civil governments fulfill their proper role in protecting justice, peace, and the welfare of all people. When governments fall short of their divine mandate, Christians must stand firm in their commitment to God’s law, even if it means engaging in civil disobedience. Ultimately, Christians must live as good citizens, serve the public good, and advocate for justice, while always remembering that their ultimate allegiance is to God’s kingdom.
