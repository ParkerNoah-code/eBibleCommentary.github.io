God Chooses His Own

The Meaning of Election

The biblical doctrine of election refers to God’s sovereign act of choosing certain individuals for salvation before the foundation of the world. This choice, made with a view to humanity’s fallen condition, is a demonstration of God’s mercy and grace, entirely independent of human merit or effort. As the Apostle Paul writes, “For [God] says to Moses, ‘I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion.’ It does not, therefore, depend on man’s desire or effort, but on God’s mercy” (Rom. 9:15–16).

Election highlights God’s initiative in salvation, as expressed in passages like Romans 8:28–39 and Ephesians 1:3–14. It emphasizes that salvation is wholly God’s work, grounded in his eternal purpose and love, and accomplished through the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. This divine choice calls for humility and gratitude, recognizing that God owes humanity nothing but judgment, yet chooses to extend mercy and grace to many.

The Mystery and Pastoral Nature of Election

While election is a profound and often controversial doctrine, it is intended in Scripture to encourage believers. It reveals the depth of God’s grace and assures believers of their security in Christ. Election fosters humility, as it is not based on human merit, and it inspires confidence and joy in knowing that God will complete the saving work he has begun (Phil. 1:6; 1 Thess. 5:23–24).

Peter exhorts believers to confirm their calling and election by cultivating qualities such as faith, goodness, knowledge, and love (2 Pet. 1:5–7, 10). These attributes are evidence of God’s work in their lives and serve as reassurance of their election. Similarly, Paul observed the Thessalonians’ faith, hope, and love as proof of their election (1 Thess. 1:3–6).

Election and Christ

The elect are described in Scripture as the Father’s gift to the Son, and Jesus’ mission is closely tied to their salvation. Jesus himself testifies that he came to save those the Father had given him, to keep them secure, and to bring them to eternal life (John 6:37–40; 10:14–16, 26–29; 17:6–26). Election underscores the intimate relationship between the Father, the Son, and the believers who are united with Christ.

Salvation is thus a Trinitarian act, with the Father electing, the Son redeeming, and the Spirit applying the benefits of salvation to the lives of believers. This divine coordination ensures the certainty and completeness of the elect’s salvation.

Reprobation

The doctrine of reprobation refers to God’s decision to leave some individuals in their sin, allowing them to face judgment for their rebellion. This decision reflects God’s justice, as sinners receive the judgment they desire and deserve. Scripture describes this process as God “hardening” their hearts or “giving them over” to their sinful desires (Rom. 1:24–28; 9:18; Ps. 81:12).

Although reprobation is a biblical reality, it is not for Christians to identify the reprobate. Instead, believers are called to view all people as potential recipients of God’s saving grace and to proclaim the gospel with the hope that many will repent and believe in Christ.

Living in Light of Election

The doctrine of election calls believers to a life of worship, service, and holiness. Recognizing that salvation is entirely God’s work fosters gratitude and motivates faithfulness. It reminds believers that their salvation is secure in Christ and that God will bring his purposes to fulfillment.

At the same time, election does not diminish the urgency of evangelism or the responsibility of individuals to respond to the gospel. It encourages Christians to engage with others, knowing that God’s mercy extends widely and that the gospel is the means through which he calls his elect to himself.
