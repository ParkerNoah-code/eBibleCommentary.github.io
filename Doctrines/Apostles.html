Jesus' Representatives Exercised His Authority

The Role and Authority of the Apostles

The New Testament distinguishes between the terms “disciples” and “apostles.” While both refer to individuals closely associated with Jesus, their roles differ significantly. A disciple is a “pupil” or “learner” who follows a teacher to be instructed, while an apostle is an “emissary” or “representative,” one who is sent out with full authority from the sender. In this sense, the apostles were not just followers of Jesus but were divinely appointed representatives sent to spread His teachings and establish His church. Just as Jesus was sent by the Father, the apostles were sent by Jesus, carrying His authority and message (Mark 3:14; 1 Pet. 1:20).

The apostles were given a unique role in the New Testament narrative, functioning as evangelists, church planters, and pastors. In these roles, they carried forward the work that Jesus had initiated during His earthly ministry. Jesus, as God’s Son, claimed divine authority for His words (John 12:49–50), and the apostles, as His representatives, also claimed Christ's divine authority for their own words (1 Thess. 2:13; 2 Thess. 3:6). This connection between Christ's authority and the apostles' authority was crucial in establishing the foundation for the early church's beliefs and practices.

The Appointment of Matthias and the Role of Paul

In Acts 1:15–26, we see the apostles before Pentecost praying and casting lots to choose a successor to Judas. This process of casting lots may seem unusual, but it was seen as a way of seeking divine guidance. The question of whether Matthias was truly Christ's intended replacement for Judas, or whether Paul, as the “apostle to the Gentiles” (Romans 11:13; Galatians 2:8), was the legitimate thirteenth apostle, remains an open question. Paul himself, however, insisted that his apostleship was authentic because he had encountered the risen Christ on the road to Damascus and was personally commissioned by Him (Acts 26:16–18).

Paul’s apostleship was affirmed by James, Peter, and John, who accepted him as a fellow apostle (Gal. 2:9). Furthermore, God confirmed Paul’s apostolic authority through signs and wonders (2 Cor. 12:12) and the success of his ministry (1 Cor. 9:2). This indicates that Paul’s apostleship was as legitimate as that of the original twelve, despite the unusual circumstances surrounding his calling.

The Apostles as Agents of Revelation

The apostles played a crucial role in the unfolding of God’s revelation. They were not just leaders of the early church but also agents of divine revelation, conveying truths that would become the foundation of Christian belief and practice. As Jesus' appointed representatives, they had the authority to speak and teach in His name. This authority was recognized in the early church and was integral to the establishment of Christian doctrine and practice (2 Cor. 10:8; 13:10).

While there are no apostles today, the apostolic ministry continues through the writings of the apostles, which form the core of the Christian Scriptures. The teachings of the apostles, preserved in the New Testament, continue to hold authoritative weight in the life of the church. The absence of new apostles does not place the contemporary church at a disadvantage because the Holy Spirit continues to apply the apostolic teachings through the Scriptures. The Christian community today is still guided by the teachings that were given by the apostles, who spoke with the authority of Christ.

Conclusion

The apostles were foundational to the early church, not only as witnesses of Jesus’ life, death, and resurrection but also as divinely appointed representatives who exercised Christ’s authority in spreading the gospel and establishing the church. Their unique role as bearers of God’s revelation and as the first leaders of the Christian movement has left an indelible mark on the church’s history. While there are no apostles today, the apostolic teachings continue to guide the church through the Scriptures, and the Holy Spirit continues to apply these teachings to God’s people.
