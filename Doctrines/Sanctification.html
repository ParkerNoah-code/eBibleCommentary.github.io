The Christian Grows in Grace

The Doctrine of Sanctification

Sanctification, according to the Westminster Shorter Catechism (Q.35), is "the work of God’s free grace, whereby we are renewed in the whole man after the image of God, and are enabled more and more to die unto sin, and live unto righteousness." This process represents the transformation of a believer’s character through the ongoing work of the Holy Spirit, as they grow in holiness and reflect the moral image of Christ. Sanctification is not about the complete eradication of sin in this life, but rather the progressive renewal of the believer's heart, mind, and actions, leading them away from sinful habits and toward Christlike virtues.

In practical terms, sanctification is both a relational and moral process. Relationally, it means being permanently set apart for God’s purposes, a status secured by the cross of Christ, where believers are purchased and claimed as God’s own (Acts 20:28; Heb. 10:10). Morally, sanctification involves a gradual transformation, wherein the believer is increasingly shaped into the likeness of Christ through the indwelling power of the Holy Spirit (Rom. 8:13; 2 Cor. 3:18; Eph. 4:22–24). God calls His children to holiness, and He gives them the strength to obey His commands and live in accordance with His will (1 Thess. 4:4; 5:23).

Regeneration and Sanctification: Birth and Growth

Regeneration is a one-time, monergistic (God-alone) act where God quickens the spiritually dead, giving them new life. Sanctification, however, is an ongoing, synergistic process in which the believer cooperates with the Holy Spirit in their spiritual growth. While regeneration implants new desires for God and holiness, sanctification involves the believer actively working out these desires, “working out salvation” in their daily life through sustained obedience (Phil. 2:12–13). The process is one of gradual transformation, with the believer becoming increasingly Christlike as the virtues of the fruit of the Spirit (Gal. 5:22–25) are formed in them (2 Cor. 3:18).

As Christians grow in sanctification, they are conformed more and more to the moral profile of Christ. Paul views this transformation as a process of glorification that begins in sanctification. The believer’s character, gradually shaped to reflect Christ’s love, humility, and patience, is the beginning of the eternal glory that will one day be fully realized when they receive a transformed body like Christ’s (Phil. 3:20–21; 1 Cor. 15:49–53).

God-Dependent Effort

The process of sanctification is not a matter of mere self-effort but is God-dependent. While believers are called to exert themselves in obedience, they must recognize that it is only through Christ’s enabling power that they can grow in holiness (Phil. 4:13). This cooperative effort between the believer and the Holy Spirit is neither passive nor self-reliant but requires active dependence on God. The believer is encouraged to “abide in Christ” (John 15:5), relying on the Spirit’s power for all that they do in their pursuit of holiness.

The standard by which sanctification is measured is the revealed moral law of God, exemplified by Christ’s own life. Believers are called to imitate Christ’s love, humility, and obedience in all their relationships and actions (Eph. 5:2; Phil. 2:5–11; 1 Pet. 2:21). This Christlike attitude is part of what it means to fulfill the law in a way that pleases God.

The Battle Between Spirit and Flesh

While sanctification is a transformative process, believers still struggle with the remnants of their old nature—the "flesh" (Gal. 5:16–17). Though the power of sin has been broken, fallen human desires and instincts continue to wage war against the believer's new nature. Paul poignantly describes this internal conflict in Romans 7:14–25, where he expresses his frustration with the ongoing struggle to do what is good and resist sin. However, the believer is not left without hope; by the Spirit’s power, they are able to "mortify" (i.e., weaken and ultimately kill) particular sinful habits, growing in victory over them (Rom. 8:13; Col. 3:5).

Christians will never be free from the struggle with sin while they are in this body, but through the Holy Spirit’s work, they experience moments of victory and deliverance from temptation. Importantly, no temptation is beyond what believers can resist with God’s help, for He provides the strength and escape from all temptation (1 Cor. 10:13).

Conclusion: The Ongoing Process of Sanctification

Sanctification is a lifelong process that leads believers from their initial justification to their ultimate glorification. It is God’s work in the believer’s life, transforming them into the image of Christ through the power of the Holy Spirit. While believers still face struggles with sin and moral imperfection, they are not left to fight alone. The Holy Spirit empowers them to grow in holiness, producing the fruit of the Spirit in their lives, and enabling them to live more fully in alignment with God's will. Sanctification is not only about moral change but also about deepening one’s relationship with God, living in the freedom of His grace, and becoming increasingly conformed to the image of Christ.
