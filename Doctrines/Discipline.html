The Church Must Uphold Christian Standards

Understanding Church Discipline in the Christian Faith

In the Gospel of Matthew, Jesus provides clear instructions on how to deal with offenses within the church: "If your brother sins against you, go and show him his fault, just between the two of you. If he listens to you, you have won your brother over. But if he will not listen, take one or two others along, so that 'every matter may be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.' If he refuses to listen, tell it to the church; and if he refuses to listen even to the church, treat him as you would a pagan or a tax collector" (Matt. 18:15–17). This passage highlights the importance of maintaining Christian conduct through a process of accountability and correction within the community of believers.

The concept of discipline in the Christian tradition is rooted in the Latin word "disciplina," which encompasses a broad range of activities designed to nurture, instruct, and train believers in their spiritual lives. It is not merely about correction but about guiding individuals towards righteousness, holiness, and service to God. Reformed theology emphasizes that a church without discipline lacks spiritual health and that discipline is a vital mark of a true church. Church discipline involves not only judicial actions against immoral behavior but also the ongoing processes of personal growth and accountability within the body of believers (Matt. 28:20; John 21:15–17; 2 Tim. 2:14–26; Titus 2; Heb. 13:17).

Discipline as an Integral Part of Church Life

The New Testament outlines that discipline within the church is multifaceted. It is not limited to punitive measures but encompasses a holistic approach to discipleship. Judicial correctives—measures taken to address specific instances of sin—have a legitimate place within this broader context of instruction and growth. Passages such as 1 Corinthians 5:1–13, 2 Corinthians 2:5–11, and Titus 1:10–14 underscore the necessity of corrective discipline for both the health of the individual and the collective well-being of the church. Discipline, when properly administered, contributes to the maturation of both the church and its members.

Jesus' authority over the church's discipline is clearly articulated in Matthew 18:18, where He grants His apostles the authority to bind and loose—that is, to prohibit or permit actions, including the declaring of sins remitted or retained (John 20:23). This authority has been understood as a divine prerogative given to the apostles, and by extension, to the church and its leaders. The "keys of the kingdom" (Matt. 16:19), first entrusted to Peter, signify this power to formulate doctrine and impose discipline, ensuring that the church remains faithful to Christ's commands.

Purpose and Process of Church Discipline

The purpose of church discipline is not punitive but restorative. The ultimate goal is to bring about repentance in the wayward individual and to restore them to fellowship within the body of Christ. The Westminster Confession of Faith outlines several reasons for church censures, including the reclaiming of offenders, the deterrence of others from sin, and the protection of the honor of Christ and the gospel (Westminster Confession, XXX.3). The process begins with personal admonition, followed by escalating steps—first private correction, then involvement of witnesses, and finally public discipline if the person refuses to repent. Exclusion from the Lord’s Supper and, in the most severe cases, excommunication, are seen as steps taken only after other efforts have failed, with the goal always being repentance and reconciliation.

Jesus' teaching in Matthew 18:15–17 outlines a structured approach to resolving offenses. The first step is a private confrontation between the offended party and the offender, aimed at reconciliation. If the person refuses to listen, the matter should be brought before two or three witnesses to establish the truth. If the issue remains unresolved, the matter should be taken to the church, and if the person still refuses to listen, the individual may be treated as an outsider—much like a pagan or tax collector. This process is not intended to be punitive but to restore the offender to a right relationship with God and with the community.

Restoration Through Repentance

The ultimate goal of church discipline is restoration. As long as the individual shows signs of repentance, the church is called to forgive and welcome them back into fellowship. There is only one reason for excommunication: impenitence. When a person refuses to repent and remains unyielding in their sin, the church may need to take the difficult step of expelling them from the community, symbolizing that they have been handed over to the influence of the world (1 Cor. 5:1–5). However, once repentance is evident, the church is tasked with welcoming the individual back, forgiving them, and celebrating their return to the fold. The aim is always to call the lost sheep back to the shepherd, to restore what was broken, and to renew the individual’s walk with Christ.
