Interpretation: Rightly Handling God’s Word

The Responsibility and Right to Interpret

All Christians have both the right and the duty to interpret Scripture, drawing upon the church's heritage of faith while engaging personally with the text. Although the Roman Catholic Church has historically cautioned against individual interpretation due to the risk of misinterpretation, proper rules and principles of exegesis can safeguard against this danger. By carefully observing these rules, Christians can avoid error and come to a fuller understanding of God’s Word.

At its core, every book of Scripture is a human composition, though divinely inspired. As such, it must be interpreted first and foremost as a human work intended to communicate with its original audience. Allegorical interpretations that ignore the expressed meaning of the human author are inappropriate. Instead, Christians must consider the language, historical context, and cultural norms of the time to accurately discern the intended message.

Understanding Through the Spirit

While comprehension of the historical and cultural context is foundational, spiritual understanding—discernment of God’s nature, will, and relationship with humankind—requires the work of the Holy Spirit. According to 2 Corinthians 3:16 and 1 Corinthians 2:14, the veil over one’s heart must be lifted to fully grasp the text’s divine implications. This spiritual clarity comes through prayer, as modeled in Psalm 119:18–19 and Ephesians 1:17–19. Prayer for God’s Spirit to illuminate the text and stir a passion for knowing and honoring Him is essential for true understanding.

The Progressive Revelation of Scripture

Each book of Scripture has its unique place in the unfolding of God’s revelation, which began in Eden and culminated in Jesus Christ, Pentecost, and the apostolic writings. For example, while the Psalms reveal the devotion of godly hearts in every age, their prayers and praises reflect the realities of a pre-Christian context. Interpreters must account for the progression of revelation to understand Scripture in its proper framework.

Furthermore, the entirety of Scripture originates from the same divine mind, ensuring a self-consistent and complementary message across all sixty-six books. Apparent contradictions in Scripture reflect human misunderstanding, not errors in the text. The principle of interpreting Scripture with Scripture, known as the "analogy of faith," emphasizes the unity and coherence of biblical teaching.

Applying Scripture to Life

Biblical interpretation ultimately leads to the reapplication of timeless truths to present circumstances. Scripture reveals unchanging principles about God, humanity, and morality, illustrated through specific historical situations. Modern readers, like Josiah in 2 Kings 22:8–13, must discern how these truths speak to their own contexts. Jesus’ use of Genesis 2:24 (Matt. 19:4–6) and Paul’s application of Genesis 15:6 and Psalm 32:1–2 in Romans 4:1–8 exemplify this process of reapplication.

It is crucial to avoid imposing meanings on Scripture that cannot be definitively supported by the text itself. Instead, all interpretations must emerge clearly from the inspired words of the human authors. Faithful and careful handling of Scripture, as instructed in 2 Timothy 2:15, is the hallmark of a mature Christian interpreter.
