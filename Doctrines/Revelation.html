Revelation: Scripture as Divine Word

The Centrality of Revelation in Christianity

Christianity, as the true worship and service of God—the Creator and Redeemer of humanity—rests firmly on the foundation of revelation. Without God’s proactive disclosure of Himself, humanity would remain in ignorance, unable to discern or engage with the divine. This act of revelation is enshrined in the Bible, a collection of sixty-six books: thirty-nine written before Christ’s incarnation (the Old Testament) and twenty-seven written after (the New Testament). These books collectively serve as the record, interpretation, expression, and embodiment of God’s self-disclosure, with the unifying themes of God and godliness permeating their content.

The term "Scriptures" refers to sacred writings that, on one level, bear the testimony of the godly to their Creator and Redeemer. On a deeper level, the Scriptures represent God’s own testimony, communicated through human authors. Through divine inspiration, the authors produced texts that are at once fully human and fully divine. Therefore, the church rightly refers to the Scriptures as the Word of God, affirming both their divine origin and authoritative content.

Jesus and the Apostolic View of Scripture

Assurance of Scripture’s divine origin stems from the testimony of Jesus Christ and His apostles. Jesus, the incarnate Word of God, viewed the Old Testament as the written instruction of His heavenly Father, to which He Himself submitted (e.g., Matt. 4:4, 7, 10; 5:19–20; 19:4–6; Luke 4:16–21). Furthermore, He affirmed its divine authority by fulfilling its promises and prophecies (Matt. 5:17–18; John 5:46). The apostles, following Christ’s example, recognized the Old Testament as “God-breathed,” inspired by the Spirit (2 Tim. 3:15–17; 2 Pet. 1:21). This acknowledgment underscores the divine source and purpose of Scripture.

The New Testament writings continue this divine legacy. Apostolic teachings, considered revealed truth (1 Cor. 2:12–13), are viewed as completing the canon of Scripture. Peter’s reference to Paul’s letters as Scripture (2 Pet. 3:15–16) and Paul’s apparent citation of Luke’s Gospel as Scripture (1 Tim. 5:18) reflect the early church’s recognition of these texts’ divine authority.

The Role of Scripture in Godly Living

The principle of written directives from God for guiding His people has its roots in the Decalogue, inscribed by God on stone tablets (Exod. 32:15–16; 34:1). Moses, under divine instruction, further recorded laws and historical narratives to instruct Israel. Throughout Israel’s history, engaging with Scripture was central to personal and communal devotion (Josh. 1:7–8; Neh. 8; Ps. 119). In Christianity, the Old and New Testaments together form the basis for faith and practice, ensuring that believers align their lives with divine will.

The Bible, being both fully human and fully divine, demands reverence and acceptance of all its contents—whether history, poetry, prophecy, or teaching—as God’s authoritative instruction. Christians are called to respond with gratitude for the gift of Scripture, anchoring their faith and conduct in its teachings. Only by doing so can believers truly honor and please God.
