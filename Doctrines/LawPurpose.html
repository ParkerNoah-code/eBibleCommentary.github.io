God’s Moral Law Has Three Purposes

The Mirror: Revealing Sin and Righteousness

In Romans 7:7, Paul writes, "I would not have known what sin was except through the law." This highlights the law’s first purpose: to act as a mirror reflecting God’s perfect righteousness while exposing human sinfulness and shortcomings. The law’s standard reveals the moral gap between humanity and God’s holiness, driving individuals to recognize their need for divine grace.

Augustine expressed this function eloquently, noting that the law leads us, in our weakness, to seek God’s help. By making sin evident (Rom. 3:20; 4:15; 5:13), the law alerts us to our peril of judgment and directs us to repentance and faith in Christ (Gal. 3:19–24). It functions as an indispensable tool for conviction, urging humanity to seek redemption through God’s provision.

The Restraint: Inhibiting Evil

The second purpose of God’s law is to restrain evil and promote civil order. While the law cannot transform the heart, its threats of judgment and enforcement through civil governance serve to inhibit lawlessness. Scripture highlights this function in passages such as Deuteronomy 13:6–11 and Romans 13:3–4, where the law’s role in maintaining justice and protecting the righteous is evident.

By curbing human tendencies toward sin, the law creates an environment of relative stability and justice, reflecting God’s concern for order and the protection of the vulnerable. Though imperfect, this function underscores the value of law in shaping societal behavior and restraining the effects of sin on a communal level.

The Guide: Leading the Regenerate

The law’s third purpose is to guide believers in the way of righteousness. For those redeemed by Christ, the law serves as a family code, detailing the behaviors that please their heavenly Father. Jesus emphasized this role when He taught that His disciples must obey His commands as a demonstration of their love for Him (John 14:15). The law provides a roadmap for living out the good works that God has prepared for His people (Eph. 2:10).

Christians are no longer under the law as a system of salvation (Rom. 6:14; 7:4, 6) but are instead "under Christ’s law" as a rule of life (1 Cor. 9:21; Gal. 6:2). This freedom from the law’s condemnation transforms obedience into an act of love and gratitude rather than a burden. Through the Spirit’s empowerment, believers are enabled to fulfill the law’s righteous requirements as part of their sanctified lives.

The Threefold Unity of the Law

The three purposes of God’s law—revealing sin, restraining evil, and guiding the regenerate—work together to accomplish His redemptive purposes. As a mirror, the law brings conviction and drives humanity to Christ. As a restraint, it preserves societal order and protects against chaos. As a guide, it shapes the lives of believers into a reflection of God’s holiness.

This threefold use of the law, articulated by Calvin, remains a cornerstone of Christian theology. It demonstrates that while the law cannot save, it plays a vital role in God’s plan of redemption, pointing sinners to Christ and instructing His followers in the path of obedience.
