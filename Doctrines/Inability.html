Fallen Human Beings Are Both Free and Enslaved

The Deceptive Heart

Jeremiah 17:9 declares, "The heart is deceitful above all things and beyond cure. Who can understand it?" This verse captures the essence of human fallenness: the internal corruption of human nature. It vividly portrays the human heart as intrinsically inclined toward sin, demonstrating humanity's profound need for divine intervention.

A proper understanding of this condition requires discerning the concepts of free agency and free will, which have been topics of theological reflection for centuries. These terms help illuminate how fallen human beings can be simultaneously free in their decisions yet enslaved to sin.

Free Agency

Free agency refers to the human capacity to make decisions and act according to one's desires and understanding of right and wrong. As moral agents, humans are accountable to God and others for the choices they make. This freedom to act is inherent to humanity and persists regardless of moral or spiritual condition.

Adam, both in his pre-fallen and post-fallen states, exercised free agency. Even in glorified sainthood, where the ability to sin is removed, free agency remains. In heaven, the saints' choices will align perfectly with their renewed nature, demonstrating that freedom to act according to one's nature does not diminish with the absence of sin.

Free Will

The concept of free will, historically defined as the ability to choose any moral option in a given situation, is more complex. While free agency remains intact after the Fall, free will in this fuller sense has been compromised. Augustine, against Pelagius, argued that original sin has so deeply corrupted humanity that people lack the natural ability to choose God’s way. This bondage of the will to sin reflects Paul’s teaching in Romans 6:16–23, where only those liberated by Christ can choose righteousness.

Free will, as such, is restored through regeneration by grace. When Christ frees individuals from sin, He inclines their hearts toward God and instills a lasting love for righteousness (John 8:34–36; Gal. 5:1, 13). This freedom is not a return to an abstract capacity to choose good or evil but a transformation of desires that aligns the believer’s will with God’s purposes.

The Nature of Will and Moral Responsibility

Will is not an independent part of the self that can be moved like a physical limb; it is the person actively choosing and engaging in action. Understanding free agency and the liberation provided by Christ becomes clearer when the focus shifts from will as an abstraction to the individual as a morally responsible being.

Each person must recognize: "I am the morally responsible agent; I am the slave to sin who needs Christ’s liberation; I am the fallen being incapable of choosing God until He renews my heart." This perspective emphasizes the profound transformation required to move from enslavement to freedom in Christ.

Christ’s Work of Liberation

Humanity’s inability to choose God apart from His grace highlights the necessity of Christ's redemptive work. Without regeneration, fallen human beings remain enslaved to sin, incapable of pursuing the moral and spiritual good that aligns with God’s will. Through the gospel, Christ frees the enslaved, enabling them to live in obedience and love toward God.

This liberation is not merely a return to neutral moral ground but a positive transformation—a realignment of desires and choices toward righteousness. As Paul teaches, the freed person is empowered to live for God, reflecting the new life given through the Spirit. This truth underscores the hope of the gospel: that fallen human beings, though enslaved to sin, are invited into freedom through Christ.
