The Christian Is Born Again

The Concept of Regeneration

Regeneration is a profound New Testament doctrine, first introduced by Jesus in his conversation with Nicodemus. In John 3:3, Jesus states, “I tell you the truth, no one can see the kingdom of God unless he is born again.” This concept highlights the necessity of a spiritual transformation that transcends mere external adherence to religious practices. Even for a devout Jew like Nicodemus, Jesus emphasized that entering the kingdom of God required an inward renewal wrought by God.

Jesus likened this transformation to being “born again,” a metaphor that vividly illustrates the comprehensive and foundational nature of the change. It signifies a divine renovation of the heart, where a new disposition is implanted—one characterized by godly desires, intentions, and actions that lead to a life aligned with the gospel. This new birth results in a positive response to Jesus as Savior and Lord, marking the beginning of eternal life.

The Role of Water and the Spirit

In John 3:5, Jesus elaborates on the nature of regeneration, saying, “No one can enter the kingdom of God unless he is born of water and the Spirit.” This statement recalls Ezekiel 36:25–27, where God promises to cleanse his people from sin’s defilement (symbolized by water) and to give them a new heart by placing his Spirit within them. This Old Testament prophecy finds its fulfillment in the new birth, which cleanses and renews the individual, enabling them to live in obedience to God.

Jesus reproaches Nicodemus for his lack of understanding, pointing out that as a teacher of Israel, he should have recognized the scriptural foundation of spiritual renewal. This underscores the importance of recognizing regeneration as a divine act, essential for true faith and discipleship.

The Evidence of Regeneration

Regeneration is not merely a theoretical concept; it manifests tangibly in the lives of believers. According to John’s epistles, belief in Jesus’ incarnation and atonement, accompanied by faith, love, holiness, and righteousness, are all fruits and evidences of being born of God (1 John 2:29; 3:9; 4:7; 5:1, 4). These traits demonstrate the transformative power of regeneration, confirming the presence of new spiritual life.

This connection between new birth and conversion is inseparable. While regeneration is a sovereign work of God, it invariably leads to a conscious turning to Christ in faith and repentance. Conversely, genuine conversion is impossible without the preceding work of regeneration.

Regeneration and Effectual Calling

Regeneration is closely linked to the doctrine of effectual calling. In most cases, the new birth occurs in the context of hearing and understanding the gospel, as the Holy Spirit illuminates its truth and relevance. This illumination draws individuals to recognize their sin and embrace Christ as their Savior, marking the decisive moment of spiritual awakening.

While infant regeneration is a possibility when God so wills (as evidenced in Luke 1:15, 41–44), the typical experience of regeneration aligns with the gospel call and its Spirit-enabled reception. In this sense, regeneration is the decisive element in effectual calling, bringing the spiritually dead to life in Christ (Eph. 2:1–10).

The Sovereign Work of God

Regeneration is entirely the work of God, accomplished by the Holy Spirit. It is monergistic, meaning that it is solely initiated and performed by God without any contribution from human effort or will. This divine act raises the elect from spiritual death to spiritual life, making faith in Christ its immediate fruit rather than its cause.

As Augustine described it, regeneration is an act of “prevenient grace”—the grace that precedes and enables human responsiveness to God. This foundational work ensures that salvation is wholly of grace, highlighting God’s initiative and glory in the redemption of sinners.

Conclusion

Regeneration is the cornerstone of the Christian life, marking the transition from spiritual death to life in Christ. It is a sovereign work of God, effecting an inward renewal that manifests in faith, obedience, and a transformed character. As Jesus declared, no one can see the kingdom of God without this new birth, for it is the beginning of a life reconciled to God and aligned with his eternal purposes.
