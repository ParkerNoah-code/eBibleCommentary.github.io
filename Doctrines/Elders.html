Pastors Must Care for the Church

The Role and Responsibility of Elders

In Titus 1:9, Paul outlines a key responsibility for pastors and elders: "He must hold firmly to the trustworthy message as it has been taught, so that he can encourage others by sound doctrine and refute those who oppose it." Elders are entrusted with the spiritual care of the church, tasked not only with teaching sound doctrine but also with providing guidance and correction where necessary. The role of elders is integral to the health and vitality of the church, and it is one of leadership, instruction, and protection of the congregation.

The apostles emphasized the collective responsibility of all Christians to care for one another through prayer and encouragement (Gal. 6:1–2; 1 John 3:16–18), but they also appointed specific leaders within the congregation to take on the primary role of shepherding God's people. These leaders, called "elders" (Greek: presbuteroi), were not only responsible for teaching the Word but also for guiding and guarding the flock from spiritual dangers (Acts 14:23; Titus 1:5). The terms "pastors" (Greek: poimenes) and "overseers" (Greek: episkopoi) are also used to describe elders, highlighting their multifaceted leadership role in the church (Acts 20:28; 1 Pet. 5:1–4).

The Biblical Foundations of Eldership

The concept of shepherding is not new in the New Testament; it originates in the Old Testament where God is described as the Shepherd of Israel (Ps. 80:1). Kings, priests, prophets, and even local rulers were appointed to serve as His agents in shepherding God's people (Num. 11:24–30; Ezek. 34). In the New Testament, Jesus is portrayed as the Good Shepherd (John 10:11), the ultimate model of shepherding, and the Chief Shepherd (1 Pet. 5:4). Elders, in this sense, serve under the authority of Christ, taking up His shepherding role as His representatives within the church.

The apostle Peter, in his own writings, acknowledges his role as an "elder" under Christ's authority (1 Pet. 5:1), perhaps reflecting on his personal commission from Jesus to shepherd His flock (John 21:15–17). This sets a precedent for all elders: their role is not one of personal ambition or authority but is a service to Christ and His people, carried out with humility and a deep sense of responsibility.

The Teaching and Pastoral Functions of Elders

Elders, though tasked with leading, may not all serve as teachers. Some may focus on pastoral care and oversight, while others, as seen in Ephesians 4:11–16, may hold a teaching role, equipping the church for service through the development of spiritual gifts. This dual role of "pastor-teacher" is one of the ways Christ has equipped the church to mature and grow in knowledge and service. Elders are expected to lead by example (1 Pet. 5:3), guiding their congregation in the ways of God.

The pastoral role requires elders to model Christ-like character and to maintain a well-ordered personal life. In 1 Timothy 3:1–7 and Titus 1:5–9, Paul outlines the qualities necessary for those who would serve as elders: they must be mature, stable Christians who are above reproach, capable of teaching and managing their households well. A faithful, wholehearted commitment to the ministry will bring rewards, both for the elders themselves and for the church they serve (Heb. 13:17; 1 Pet. 5:4).

Other Ministerial Roles in the Church

While elders bear the primary responsibility for teaching and guiding the congregation, other roles exist within the church to support the broader ministry. For example, deacons (Greek: diakonoi, or "servants") assist in areas such as mercy ministry and church administration (Acts 6:2–6; 1 Tim. 3:8–13). Deacons play a crucial role in supporting the elders, particularly by taking care of practical needs and helping to maintain the order of the church. Their work is essential to the smooth operation of the congregation, but it is distinct from the pastoral leadership provided by elders.

The Importance of Eldership in the Church

Every church must establish a wise and thoughtful process for selecting and appointing elders, ensuring that those chosen are spiritually mature and committed to their roles. The appointment of elders is not just a functional task but a divine calling to shepherd God's people. Their leadership is a reflection of Christ’s love and authority, and their commitment to the well-being of the congregation directly impacts the health of the church. The pattern set forth in the New Testament for eldership emphasizes the importance of godly leadership in fostering a thriving, obedient, and faithful church community.
