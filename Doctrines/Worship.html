God Gives a Liturgical Pattern

The Nature of Worship

Psalm 95:6–7 invites believers into worship with the words, "Come, let us bow down in worship, let us kneel before the LORD our Maker; for he is our God and we are the people of his pasture, the flock under his care." Worship is the rational and heartfelt response of creatures to their Creator, honoring and glorifying Him for His greatness and graciousness. It is an act of offering back to God all He has given, including praise for His attributes, thanks for His deeds, and a desire for His continued glory through acts of mercy and power.

Worship encompasses a spectrum of emotions, from awe-filled wonder to exuberant celebration. Examples from David’s life illustrate this: he danced with zeal before the ark (2 Sam. 6:14–16) and sat in humble amazement when promised an eternal dynasty (2 Sam. 7:18). Both acts pleased God. Learning from God’s Word is also a form of worship, as it honors Him through attentive listening and obedience.

The Covenant Basis of Worship

The foundation of worship lies in the covenant relationship between God and His people. This relationship, established through salvation and God’s promises, binds worshipers to Him. Old Testament worship reflected this covenant with a blend of awe and joy, humility, and confession of sin. These elements remain central to Christian worship today, where gratitude for Christ’s sacrifice enhances the understanding of God’s holiness and humanity’s need.

Worship will also be the central activity of heaven (Rev. 4:8–11; 5:9–14; 7:9–17), and thus it should hold a primary place in the life of the church and individual believers on earth. Whether private or corporate, worship is the primary way believers express their love and devotion to God (Col. 3:17).

The Liturgical Pattern in the Old Covenant

Under Mosaic law, God provided a comprehensive pattern for worship, which included various rituals, feasts, and sacrificial systems. These practices served to structure Israel’s worship, reminding them of God’s holiness, their dependence on Him, and the promise of a coming Savior.

Key features included:

(a) The Sabbath: A holy day of rest every seventh day, commemorating God’s creation and redemption (Gen. 2:3; Exod. 20:8–11; Deut. 5:12–15). Sabbath-keeping was a critical component of Israel’s covenant obligations.

(b) National Feasts: These included Passover, the Feast of Weeks, and the Feast of Tabernacles, which celebrated God’s provision and acts of deliverance (Exod. 23:14–17; Lev. 23; Deut. 16:1–15).

(c) The Day of Atonement: This solemn day focused on atoning for Israel’s sins through the high priest’s intercession (Lev. 16).

(d) The Sacrificial System: Regular sacrifices were offered to acknowledge sin and express devotion to God. These included daily, monthly, and personal offerings, all emphasizing the necessity of a flawless sacrifice (Lev. 17:11).

Christian Worship in the New Covenant

With the coming of Christ, the Old Testament patterns were fulfilled and transformed. Christ’s priesthood and sacrifice replaced the Mosaic system (Heb. 7–10), while baptism and the Lord’s Supper became the new covenant ordinances (Matt. 28:19; 26:26–29). The ceremonial aspects of the law, such as dietary restrictions and purification rites, no longer bind believers, as their symbolic purpose has been fulfilled in Christ (Mark 7:19; Col. 2:16).

Worship now centers on Christ’s resurrection, celebrated on the first day of the week, known as the Lord’s Day (Acts 20:7; Rev. 1:10). This shift reflects the new creation inaugurated by Christ’s victory over sin and death. Despite these changes, the essence of worship—praise, thanksgiving, trust, and service—remains unchanged.

Living a Life of Worship

Worship is not limited to specific times or places but is a holistic response to God’s grace. Believers are called to live lives of worship, offering every thought, word, and deed to God’s glory (Rom. 12:1; Col. 3:17). This includes both private devotion and corporate participation in the church’s worship.

As believers grow in their knowledge of God’s greatness and graciousness, their worship deepens. True worship, shaped by the patterns God has revealed, honors Him and brings believers into greater communion with their Creator and Redeemer.
