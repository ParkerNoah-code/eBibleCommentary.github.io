We Are Not Set Free to Sin

The Concept of Antinomianism

Antinomianism, meaning "anti-law," refers to several views that deny the direct authority of God’s law in the lives of Christians. This theological stance has manifested in various forms throughout history, each presenting a different view on how or whether God’s moral law should apply to believers. Some argue that the law no longer applies at all, while others distort the concept of freedom in Christ, leading to the false belief that Christians are free to live in sin without consequence.

John’s epistle directly counters antinomianism by stressing that “he who does what is right is righteous, just as he [Christ] is righteous” (1 John 3:7). This clearly indicates that a life of righteousness, characterized by obedience to God’s law, is the mark of a true believer. Those who claim to be in Christ but continue in sin are deceiving themselves. True salvation in Christ results in a transformed life, one that strives to live according to God’s moral law, not to dismiss it.

Forms of Antinomianism

Several forms of antinomianism have emerged, each with a different interpretation of the relationship between grace, salvation, and the law.

Dualistic Antinomianism

Dualistic antinomianism is the belief that salvation is solely for the soul, with the physical body and behavior being irrelevant to God. This view, espoused by Gnostic heretics as condemned by Jude and Peter (Jude 4–19; 2 Pet. 2), allows for sinful behavior without moral consequence, arguing that bodily actions are separate from spiritual health. This view directly contradicts biblical teaching, which holds that both the soul and body are to be redeemed and transformed.

Spirit-Centered Antinomianism

Spirit-centered antinomianism emphasizes the internal prompting of the Holy Spirit, leading to the belief that the law is unnecessary as a guide for Christian conduct. This view assumes that freedom from the law as a means of salvation also frees believers from the moral directives of the law. However, the Bible clearly teaches that the Spirit guides believers to live in accordance with God’s revealed will, which is contained in Scripture (1 Cor. 14:37).

Christ-Centered Antinomianism

Christ-centered antinomianism teaches that because believers are in Christ, who has kept the law on their behalf, they need not concern themselves with obedience to the law. The logic is that Christ’s righteousness is imputed to them, and therefore their actions do not matter as long as they continue to believe. However, 1 John 3:4–10 refutes this, teaching that those who are truly in Christ will not continue to live in sin. The evidence of salvation is not just belief, but a transformed life marked by righteousness.

Dispensational Antinomianism

Dispensational antinomianism teaches that the moral law is no longer necessary for Christians since they live under a dispensation of grace rather than the law. This view undermines the continuity of God’s moral law across dispensations, but the New Testament clearly affirms that law-keeping remains a responsibility of Christians (Romans 3:31; 1 Cor. 6:9–11). Paul explicitly states that he is not free from God’s law but is under Christ’s law (1 Cor. 9:21).

Dialectical Antinomianism

Dialectical antinomianism, advanced by theologians like Barth and Brunner, denies that biblical law is a direct command of God, suggesting that the imperative statements in Scripture are merely triggers for the Holy Spirit's present-day utterances. This view diminishes the authority of Scripture as God's inspired word, undermining the reliability of the Bible’s moral law as a guide for Christian living.

Situationist Antinomianism

Situationist antinomianism argues that the only requirement for Christians is to act out of love, with the belief that the specific commands of Scripture, including the Ten Commandments, are flexible rules that can be disregarded if love calls for it. However, Romans 13:8–10 teaches that love fulfills the law, not by disregarding it, but by fulfilling its commands in the right spirit. The view that the law is optional leads to a misunderstanding of love and a lack of respect for God's moral guidelines.

The Role of the Moral Law

The moral law, as expressed in the Ten Commandments and throughout Scripture, is one coherent law, intended to be a guide for God’s people in every age. Repentance involves not only turning from sin but also a commitment to follow God's law with the help of the Holy Spirit. The Spirit empowers believers to keep God’s law and become more like Christ, the perfect law-keeper (Matt. 5:17). Scripture holds that salvation is not for those who live in sin but for those who are committed to turning from sin and seeking righteousness (1 Cor. 6:9–11; Rev. 21:8).

Conclusion: The Importance of Law in Christian Life

While believers are saved by grace and not by works, this grace does not free them from the responsibility of obeying God's law. Salvation brings a new heart that desires to live according to God's will, and the law is a necessary guide in that process. Antinomianism, in all its forms, distorts the freedom that Christ offers by excusing sin and neglecting the call to holiness. True Christian freedom is the freedom to live in obedience to God, empowered by the Holy Spirit, and reflecting Christ’s righteousness in all aspects of life.
