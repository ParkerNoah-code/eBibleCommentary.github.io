The Authority of Scripture

God’s Means of Governance

The authority of the Bible is central to Christian faith and practice. It reflects God’s purpose to direct His people’s beliefs and behaviors through the revealed truth of Holy Scripture. This authority also means that all human ideas about God must be measured, tested, and corrected according to biblical teaching. In Christianity, authority belongs exclusively to God, the Creator, who exercises this authority through the truth and wisdom of His written Word.

From the human perspective, each biblical book was written to inspire wholehearted service to God. From the divine standpoint, the Bible as a whole serves this purpose. As 2 Timothy 3:16 declares, “All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training in righteousness.” Since Christ now holds executive authority over the cosmos (Matt. 28:18), Scripture serves as the instrument of His lordship over His followers, functioning much like His letters to the seven churches in Revelation 2–3.

Sources of God’s Truth

Different traditions and schools of thought offer varying answers to the question, "Where is God’s authoritative truth to be found today?" These answers differ in their understanding and use of Scripture:

  • Roman Catholic and Orthodox Views: These traditions believe God’s truth is found in the church’s interpretation of Scripture, embodied in its traditions and consensus. While affirming the Bible as God-given truth, they insist that the church’s interpretations are infallible.
  • Liberal and Subjectivist Views: These perspectives see God’s truth in the impressions, thoughts, and theories that Scripture inspires in individual readers. This approach often dismisses the New Testament’s concept of inspiration and treats the Bible as fallible, emphasizing personal discernment as the means of finding wisdom.
  • Historic Protestant View: This position holds that God’s truth resides in the canonical Scriptures themselves. Scripture is viewed as inspired (God-breathed, 2 Tim. 3:16), inerrant (completely true in all affirmations), sufficient (containing all that is necessary for salvation and eternal life), and clear (self-interpreting on essential matters). This view systematically submits human thoughts to the authority of Scripture as the ultimate rule for faith and life.

The Canon and Its Role

The term “canon” refers to a rule or standard. While many traditions acknowledge Scripture as canon, their practices reveal differing levels of commitment to its authority. Roman Catholic, Orthodox, and liberal approaches often place human interpretations or judgments above the biblical text, undermining its function as the ultimate rule. Historic Protestantism, by contrast, fully embraces the Bible as canon, submitting all human reasoning and tradition to its authority.

By taking Scripture seriously as the canon, Protestants affirm its role as the authoritative guide for faith and practice. This approach ensures that Christian belief remains grounded in God’s revealed truth rather than subjective or institutional interpretations.

The Importance of Biblical Authority

Accepting the Bible’s authority is essential for faithful Christian living. Scripture serves not only as the means by which God governs His people but also as the standard against which all beliefs and practices must be measured. To reject or undermine this authority, regardless of one’s sincerity, results in a flawed Christian profession. By contrast, full submission to Scripture allows believers to align their faith and lives with God’s will, honoring His sovereignty and wisdom.
