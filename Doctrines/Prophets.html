God Sent Messengers to Declare His Will

The Role of the Prophets

In Deuteronomy 18:18, God promises, "I will raise up for them a prophet like you from among their brothers; I will put my words in his mouth, and he will tell them everything I command him." Prophets were chosen instruments of divine revelation, tasked with conveying God’s mind and will to His people. The canonical prophets, whose writings constitute a significant portion of the Old Testament, stood as intermediaries between God and humanity.

These prophets, empowered by the Holy Spirit, spoke with divine authority (2 Pet. 1:19–21). They began their messages with phrases like “This is what the LORD says,” indicating that their words came directly from Yahweh. As members of God’s council (Jer. 23:22), they were entrusted with both understanding and communicating His purposes.

Foretelling and Forthtelling

While prophecy often included prediction (foretelling), its primary focus was on declaring God’s message for the present (forthtelling). Prophets called the people to repentance, urging them to turn back to covenant faithfulness. Their warnings often contained predictions of judgment, but these were paired with promises of restoration through God’s coming king and kingdom.

The prophets were reformers, striving to enforce God’s law and renew the nation’s commitment to Him. They were deeply concerned with the spiritual and moral state of Israel, calling out sin and guiding the people back to their covenant obligations.

The Prophets as Intercessors

In addition to delivering God’s messages, prophets interceded for the nation. They spoke to God on behalf of the people with the same fervency with which they spoke to the people on behalf of God. This dual role is exemplified in Moses (Exod. 32:30–32), Samuel (1 Sam. 7:5–9), and Isaiah (2 Kings 19:4). Their prayers were a vital part of their ministry, demonstrating their deep love for the people and their reliance on God’s mercy.

However, there were times when God instructed prophets not to pray for the nation because of its persistent rebellion (Jer. 7:16; 11:14; 14:11). This underscored the gravity of Israel’s sin and the necessity of repentance.

The Problem of False Prophets

False prophets plagued Israel, presenting a distorted version of God’s message. These individuals often catered to popular opinion, offering reassurance instead of truth. They claimed divine inspiration while speaking their own dreams and desires, leading the people astray (1 Kings 22:1–28; Jer. 23:9–40; Ezek. 13).

True prophets stood in stark contrast to these deceivers, speaking boldly against sin and calling for genuine repentance. Their messages often faced resistance, but their faithfulness to God’s word marked them as His true messengers.

Prophets in the New Testament

The prophetic ministry continued into the New Testament, though its focus shifted with the coming of Christ. Revelation is explicitly identified as prophecy, given by God through Jesus Christ (Rev. 1:1–3; 22:12–20). The apostles carried forward the prophetic tradition by delivering God’s instruction to His people, laying the foundation for the church (Eph. 2:20; 3:5).

New Testament prophets, such as those mentioned in Romans 16:25–27, explained the fulfillment of Old Testament promises in Christ. The book of Hebrews, with its rich exposition of Christ as the ultimate fulfillment of God’s plan, exemplifies this prophetic ministry.

The Enduring Significance of the Prophets

The prophets’ messages remain vital for understanding God’s character and purposes. They reveal His holiness, justice, and mercy, and they point forward to the ultimate Prophet, Jesus Christ, who perfectly embodies and communicates God’s will.

Today, their writings continue to guide believers, calling them to faithfulness, repentance, and hope in the promises of God. Through the prophets, God’s voice still speaks, reminding His people of His enduring covenant love and His sovereign plan for redemption.
