God Is Surpassingly Great

The Greatness of God

Scripture repeatedly affirms the surpassing greatness of God. Passages like 1 Chronicles 29:11 proclaim, “Yours, O LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the majesty and the splendor, for everything in heaven and earth is yours.” God’s greatness is not only vast but also incomprehensible, transcending human understanding (Ps. 145:3; Deut. 7:21; Dan. 9:4). Theology describes this as God’s “incomprehensibility,” meaning that while we can know true things about God, we can never fully grasp the entirety of His being.

The imagery of God dwelling in “thick darkness” (Ps. 97:2) and “unapproachable light” (1 Tim. 6:16) highlights the same reality: God is infinitely above His creation. Though we are made in His image and can know Him relationally, our finite minds cannot contain the fullness of His infinite nature. This reality should inspire both humility and awe in those who seek to know Him.

The Mystery of God

The term “mystery” in this context refers to aspects of God’s nature and works that are beyond human comprehension. Unlike the biblical use of the word to signify truths once hidden but now revealed (e.g., Eph. 3:2–6), this use of “mystery” acknowledges the limits of human understanding in grasping the infinite. For example, the Trinity, the Incarnation, the regenerating work of the Holy Spirit, and the inspiration of Scripture are truths revealed by God but not fully explainable by human reason.

While we cannot know all there is to know about God, we can trust what He has revealed about Himself in Scripture. His self-disclosure provides a genuine, though partial, understanding of His nature. The analogy of a parent’s baby talk to a young child is helpful here: though limited in scope, it communicates real truths and fosters trust and love. Similarly, God “accommodates” His revelation to human capacity so that we might know and relate to Him as Creator, Redeemer, and Father.

Anthropomorphic Revelation

God often presents Himself in Scripture using anthropomorphic language—descriptions of human traits or actions applied to God. For example, He is said to have a face (Exod. 33:11), hands (1 Sam. 5:11), and feet (Nah. 1:3). He is depicted as sitting on a throne (1 Kings 22:19) or flying on the wind (Ps. 18:10). These images do not describe God as He is in Himself but illustrate His actions and relationship with His creation.

Such descriptions serve to bridge the gap between the Creator and His creatures, enabling humans to understand and relate to Him. They remind us that God is both transcendent—infinitely greater than us—and immanent—personally involved in our lives. These anthropomorphic expressions draw us into worship, trust, and love for a God who is both near and unfathomably great.

Worshiping the Surpassingly Great God

The ultimate response to the mystery and greatness of God is worship. Theology, rightly understood, leads to doxology—a response of praise and adoration. As 1 Corinthians 13:12 reminds us, we currently “see only a reflection as in a mirror” and “know in part,” but this partial knowledge should not deter our worship. Instead, it should deepen our reverence and gratitude for a God who reveals Himself to us in ways we can understand.

The recognition of God’s greatness and our own limitations should inspire humility and trust. Knowing that His plans and purposes are far beyond our comprehension (Rom. 11:33) encourages us to depend on His wisdom and submit to His will. Worshiping God for His incomprehensibility affirms our faith in His infinite power, goodness, and love.

In every act of worship, whether through prayer, song, or obedience, we echo the words of 1 Chronicles 29:11: “Yours, O LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the majesty and the splendor.” Such worship not only honors God but also aligns our hearts with His, fostering a deeper relationship with the One who is surpassingly great.
