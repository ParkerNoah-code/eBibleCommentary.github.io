God Has a Purpose

The Sovereign Purpose of God

Malachi 1:2–3 states, “‘I have loved you,’ says the LORD. But you ask, ‘How have you loved us?’ ‘Was not Esau Jacob’s brother?’ the LORD says. ‘Yet I have loved Jacob, but Esau I have hated.’” This passage encapsulates the biblical theme of God’s sovereign purpose in creation, redemption, and history. God’s purpose is rooted in His desire for an eternal relationship of love with His creatures, a plan disrupted by sin but being restored through His redemptive work.

The authors of Scripture viewed themselves and their readers as part of this divine purpose. They reflected on God’s mighty acts of redemption, culminating in the ultimate renewal of creation, where the earth will be transformed into unimaginable glory (Isaiah 65:17–25; 2 Peter 3:10–13; Revelation 21:1–22:5). This vision of restoration underscores God’s unwavering control over history, ensuring the fulfillment of His plan for a redeemed people to love, worship, and serve Him in everlasting fellowship.

The Doctrine of Predestination

Predestination refers to God’s eternal decision regarding the final destiny of individuals, made before the world and its inhabitants existed. In the New Testament, predestination is closely tied to election, describing God’s choice of certain individuals for salvation and eternal life (Romans 8:29; Ephesians 1:4–5, 11). This divine decision underscores God’s sovereignty in salvation, affirming that He initiates and accomplishes the work of redemption.

In Protestant theology, predestination encompasses both election and reprobation—the decision to save some and to leave others to their deserved condemnation for sin. Scriptures such as Romans 9:6–29 and 1 Peter 2:8 support this dual aspect of God’s decree. While this teaching may challenge human understanding, it highlights God’s justice and mercy in working out His redemptive plan.

The Basis of God’s Choice

Some argue that God’s choice is based on His foreknowledge of who would choose Christ in response to the gospel. However, this view misinterprets the biblical meaning of foreknowledge. In passages like Romans 8:29 and 1 Peter 1:2, foreknow signifies “fore-love” or “fore-appoint,” emphasizing God’s proactive choice rather than passive observation.

Moreover, Scripture teaches that sinners, being spiritually dead, are incapable of coming to faith without God’s intervention. Ephesians 2:4–10 highlights that salvation is entirely by grace, as God regenerates hearts and enables faith. Jesus affirms this in John 6:65: “No one can come to me unless the Father has enabled him.” Therefore, sinners choose Christ only because God has first chosen them, renewing their hearts and drawing them to Himself.

Human Freedom and Divine Sovereignty

While human actions are self-determined and morally accountable, they are not free from God’s sovereign control. God’s eternal purpose and foreordination encompass all human choices, yet He works through these choices to fulfill His will. This coexistence of divine sovereignty and human responsibility remains a mystery, yet it is affirmed throughout Scripture.

God often accomplishes His purposes without miraculous intervention, allowing events to unfold naturally. This highlights His wisdom in orchestrating all things according to His perfect plan, ensuring that His glory is displayed in every circumstance.

Living in Light of Predestination

For Christians, the doctrine of predestination inspires gratitude and trust. Believers recognize that their salvation is entirely by God’s grace, leading them to thank Him for their conversion and depend on Him for perseverance in faith. This assurance fosters confidence in God’s sovereign care and hope in His ultimate triumph.

Understanding predestination also deepens the believer’s humility and worship. Recognizing that God’s choice is based on His mercy, not human merit, leads to awe and reverence for His grace. Furthermore, it motivates believers to share the gospel, trusting that God’s redemptive purpose will be accomplished through their witness.

God’s predestination reveals His sovereign love and unshakable commitment to His plan of redemption. As part of this divine purpose, Christians are called to live in worship, trust, and obedience, secure in the knowledge that the God who began a good work in them will carry it to completion (Philippians 1:6).
