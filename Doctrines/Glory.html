God’s Glory-Showing Requires Glory-Giving

The Purpose of God’s Glory

God’s goal is His glory, a purpose often misunderstood as divine egoism but better understood as an expression of divine love. God desires to be praised for His praiseworthiness, to be exalted for His greatness and goodness, and to be appreciated for who He is. However, this glory is not self-serving but relational, involving a twofold dynamic: God reveals His glory to His creatures, and they respond by giving Him glory. This interplay of revelation and adoration fulfills the purpose for which humanity and angels were created and, for fallen humans, redeemed.

The fellowship created by this exchange of glory is at the heart of human fulfillment. As we see and celebrate God’s glory, we experience the joy of communion with Him. This joy is mutual, bringing delight to both God and His people (Zeph. 3:14–17). In this way, God’s glory is both the source and the goal of the love relationship between Creator and creation.

The Revelation of God’s Glory

In the Old Testament, “glory” conveys ideas of weight, worth, splendor, and dignity. When God reveals His glory, it is often accompanied by awe-inspiring physical manifestations, such as the Shekinah—a radiant, cloud-like presence symbolizing God’s active involvement in the world. For example, God’s glory appeared to Moses in response to his plea, proclaimed through His name and characterized by His nature, power, and mercy (Exod. 33:18–34:7).

The Shekinah is a recurring feature of significant biblical moments, signifying God’s presence and majesty (Exod. 40:34–35; 1 Kings 8:10–11; Ezek. 1:28). In the New Testament, God’s glory is revealed in its fullness in the person of Jesus Christ. John 1:14 declares, “The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the one and only Son.” Through Christ’s life, death, and resurrection, the glory of God’s nature, character, and redemptive purpose is made visible (2 Cor. 4:3–6; Heb. 1:1–3).

Responding to God’s Glory

The proper response to the revelation of God’s glory is to give Him glory. This includes verbal praise, as exemplified in Revelation 4:9 and 19:7, where heavenly beings extol God’s greatness and goodness. It also involves practical expressions of worship, as believers dedicate their entire lives to honoring and pleasing God. Paul exhorts Christians to “do it all for the glory of God” in every activity (1 Cor. 10:31).

God’s insistence on receiving exclusive glory underscores His unique role as Creator and Redeemer. Isaiah 42:8 declares, “I will not yield my glory to another or my praise to idols.” In salvation, God alone deserves credit, as He accomplishes it entirely by His grace. Human beings contribute nothing but their need for redemption, a truth emphasized by the Reformation principle of “Glory to God alone” (soli Deo gloria). This principle remains essential today, reminding us that our worship must reflect an acknowledgment of God’s sovereign grace at every stage of salvation.

Glory as a Source of Joy

The exchange of glory between God and His creatures is not a burdensome obligation but a source of profound joy. As we behold God’s glory, we are transformed and fulfilled, experiencing the ultimate purpose for which we were created. This joy is not limited to individual experiences but extends to the corporate worship of God’s people, reflecting the unity and diversity of His creation.

The prophetic vision of God’s glory in Ezekiel 1:28, described as the radiance of a rainbow, serves as a reminder of God’s faithfulness and beauty. It invites believers to marvel at His majesty and to join in the eternal praise of the One who is worthy of all honor and glory. This reciprocal relationship of seeing and giving glory brings both delight to God and wholeness to humanity, showcasing the depth of His love and the purpose of His creation.
