How a Genuine Church is Identified

The Church as an Outcrop of the Universal Body

The term "church" in the New Testament often refers to specific local congregations, such as the church in Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, and Laodicea (Revelation 2:1, 8, 12, 18; 3:1, 7, 14). Each of these local churches was an expression of the one universal church, embodying the nature of the larger body of believers, who are the Father’s regenerate family, Christ’s ministering body, and a fellowship sustained by the Holy Spirit. While these churches were part of the global body of Christ, they had their own unique contexts, challenges, and responsibilities.

However, not all groups that call themselves churches are true representations of the body of Christ. Some self-styled churches, such as the Unitarian and Mormon churches, deny key doctrines like the Trinity, and thus fail to align with the biblical definition of a church. Additionally, some congregations that once held fast to the faith have drifted into error, leaving it difficult to discern whether they still function as true churches. Therefore, discernment is required to identify a genuine church, and the Reformers provided a helpful framework for this task.

The Marks of a True Church

The Reformers identified two primary criteria for determining whether a group is a true church: the faithful preaching of the Word of God and the right use of the sacraments. These marks are essential for a congregation to be considered a genuine church.

1. The Faithful Preaching of the Word of God

A church is marked by its faithful preaching of the gospel as found in the Bible. This means that the teachings of the church must align with Scripture, especially regarding key doctrines such as the Trinity, the deity of Christ, His sin-bearing atonement, and justification by faith. Groups that deny these essential doctrines, like those who reject the Incarnation and Atonement (as the docetic separatists did in 1 John 4:1–3), are not part of the true church. John warns that those who deny such central truths “did not really belong to us” (1 John 2:19). Thus, any church that distorts or denies these foundational doctrines cannot be recognized as a true church.

2. The Right Use of the Sacraments

The second mark of a true church is the right use of the sacraments—namely, baptism and the Lord’s Supper. These sacraments are not mere rituals but are signs that point to the gospel, strengthening and confirming faith in Christ. Baptism symbolizes the believer’s identification with Christ and entrance into the visible church, while the Lord’s Supper serves to nourish faith and affirm one’s place in the covenant community. Any superstition or misuse of the sacraments, such as turning them into magic rites that bypass faith, undermines their purpose and compromises the identity of the church. The right use of the sacraments involves discipline, ensuring that professions of faith are tested, and public behavior is evaluated, to maintain the integrity of the fellowship.

Additional Marks of the Church

While the faithful preaching of the Word and the right use of the sacraments are the minimal marks of a true church, there are other characteristics that can further define the identity of a Christian congregation. Martin Luther and the Reformers highlighted several additional marks, including church discipline, an authorized ministry, public worship, and suffering under the cross.

Church discipline is essential for maintaining the purity of the church and for restoring those who fall into sin (Matt. 16:19). The authorized ministry refers to the leadership of the church, which must be in line with Scripture and the apostolic tradition (Acts 14:23; 20:28). Public worship, where believers gather regularly for praise, prayer, preaching, and the sacraments, is a crucial element in the life of the church (Heb. 10:25). Finally, suffering under the cross refers to the reality that the church will endure trials and persecution as part of its identification with Christ (Acts 14:22; 20:29).

While these additional marks are important, they are not as foundational as the two essential marks of the church. A congregation that lacks one or more of these marks may be deficient but still considered a church in the broad sense. However, it is clear that a church should aim to embody these characteristics to more fully reflect the nature of the body of Christ.

Conclusion

The true church is identified by the faithful preaching of the Word of God and the right use of the sacraments. These two marks are essential for any congregation to be recognized as a legitimate expression of the body of Christ. While other marks, such as church discipline, authorized ministry, and public worship, further define a healthy church, they are secondary in importance. Ultimately, the church’s identity is rooted in its commitment to the gospel and its role in nurturing and sustaining the faith of believers.
