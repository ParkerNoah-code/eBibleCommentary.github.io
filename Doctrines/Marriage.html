Matrimony is Meant to Be a Permanent Covenant Relationship

The Biblical Foundation of Marriage

In Malachi 2:16, God expresses His strong disapproval of divorce: “I hate divorce,” says the LORD God of Israel. This declaration underscores the sanctity and permanence of the marriage covenant. Marriage, as established by God, is a sacred bond in which a man and a woman commit to each other for life, becoming “one flesh” (Gen. 2:24). The design for marriage is rooted in the idea of mutual support, companionship, and the fulfillment of God’s purposes for humanity—namely, procreation and the establishment of a godly heritage (Gen. 1:28; 2:18). This covenant relationship is meant to reflect God’s faithfulness and His love for His people.

The Westminster Confession explains that marriage serves multiple purposes: it provides mutual help for the husband and wife, facilitates the increase of humanity with legitimate offspring, and prevents immorality. Marriage is a divine institution, ordained not only for the good of the individuals involved but also for the wider community and the church (Westminster Confession XXIV.2). The relationship is designed to reflect the unity and complementary roles that God intended for the family and society.

Mutual Respect and Complementary Roles

The Bible teaches that in marriage, the man and the woman are to experience mutual completion. This is seen in Genesis 2:23, when Adam recognizes Eve as "bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh." Marriage is not merely a contractual relationship but a profound unity that transcends the individual. In Christian marriage, the roles of husband and wife are distinct but complementary. Paul, in Ephesians 5:21–33, uses Christ’s relationship with the church as a model for the relationship between husband and wife. The husband is called to love his wife sacrificially, just as Christ loves the church, while the wife is called to respect and submit to her husband as the church submits to Christ.

This distinction of roles does not imply inequality. Both husband and wife are equally created in God’s image (Gen. 1:27) and have equal dignity and worth. The husband’s leadership role is not one of domination but of love, care, and protection, while the wife’s submission is not a denial of her value but an expression of her trust in God’s design for marriage. In essence, marriage is meant to be a partnership grounded in mutual respect, love, and a shared commitment to God’s will.

Divorce and Its Biblical Context

Although God hates divorce (Mal. 2:16), He acknowledges the reality of human sin and hardness of heart. In Matthew 19:8, Jesus explains that divorce was permitted due to the hardness of people's hearts but that it was not God’s original intention. In Matthew 5:31–32 and 19:8–9, Jesus teaches that marital unfaithfulness (adultery) is the primary ground for divorce, as it breaks the marital covenant. While divorce is always tragic and contrary to God’s ideal, Jesus allows for it in cases of infidelity, though He encourages reconciliation when possible.

Jesus further teaches that a man who divorces his wife for any reason other than unfaithfulness and remarries commits adultery, as does the wife who remarries after being divorced for such reasons. This teaching emphasizes the disruption of God’s intended purpose for marriage and the sacredness of the marital bond. While divorce is a regrettable solution, it may sometimes be necessary due to the severity of the offense or the hardness of heart in either partner.

The Role of the Church and Civil Authority in Divorce

The Westminster Confession provides guidance on the proper handling of divorce and remarriage. In the case of adultery, the innocent party may seek a divorce and remarry, as if the offending spouse were dead (Westminster Confession XXIV.5–6). However, divorce is only permissible in cases of adultery or willful desertion, which cannot be remedied by the church or civil authorities. In these cases, the church and the civil magistrate must follow a public and orderly process to ensure that divorce is handled with care and justice.

In cases where a believer is deserted by an unbelieving spouse, the apostle Paul states in 1 Corinthians 7:15 that the believer is not "bound" to the marriage. This teaching suggests that the believer may regard the relationship as over, but whether this allows for remarriage remains a subject of debate within Christian circles. The key principle, however, is that the marriage covenant should be honored, and divorce should never be taken lightly. The church must approach divorce with caution, seeking to restore broken relationships whenever possible and protecting the integrity of the marriage bond.

Conclusion: The Sanctity of Marriage

Marriage, as established by God, is a sacred and permanent covenant relationship. It is a reflection of God’s love and faithfulness to His people. While divorce is permitted in certain circumstances, it is always a tragedy that disrupts God’s ideal for marriage. The church must uphold the sanctity of marriage, supporting couples in their commitment to one another and providing guidance when relationships face challenges. The goal of marriage is not merely personal happiness but the fulfillment of God's purposes for His people, the family, and the broader society. As Christian spouses seek to live out their roles with mutual respect and love, they bear witness to the gospel of Christ and the covenant relationship between Christ and His church.
