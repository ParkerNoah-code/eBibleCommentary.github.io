God’s Nature is Spiritual

The Spiritual Nature of God

Isaiah 66:1 declares, “Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool. Where is the house you will build for me? Where will my resting place be?” This verse underscores the transcendent nature of God, whose existence and presence are not confined by physical space or human constructs. Jesus’ words to the Samaritan woman, “God is spirit” (John 4:24), further emphasize that God’s being is not physical but fully spiritual, distinct from the material limitations of humanity.

While God is personal and relational, He does not inhabit a body as we do. His spirituality signifies that He is not bound by the physical dimensions of space and time, nor does He experience the effects of sin that disrupt human concentration and control. God’s infinite nature elevates Him beyond human comprehension, yet His personal attributes allow for genuine interaction with His creation.

God’s Freedom from Limits

God’s transcendence is expressed in His immensity and infinity. He is present in His fullness everywhere at all times, unhindered by spatial or temporal limitations (1 Kings 8:27; Isaiah 40:12–26). Unlike humans who are confined to specific moments and locations, God exists outside time, perceiving past, present, and future simultaneously. This omnipresence enables Him to uphold and sustain all creation while orchestrating His sovereign plan for every person and event (Daniel 4:34–35; Ephesians 1:11).

Theologians often describe God’s transcendence as a hallmark of His greatness, distinguishing Him as the Creator who is above and beyond all He has made. His perspective encompasses the entirety of creation, ensuring that nothing is outside His attention or care.

The Immutability of God

God’s immutability reflects His perfect consistency. He does not change, for He is perfect in His nature and character (Malachi 3:6; James 1:16–18). Unlike created beings who evolve and adapt, God is timeless and unchanging. Yet this immutability does not imply detachment or inactivity. On the contrary, God is continually at work in His creation, bringing new things into existence and fulfilling His purposes (Isaiah 42:9; 2 Corinthians 5:17; Revelation 21:5).

God’s steadfast nature assures His faithfulness to His promises and His responsiveness to humanity. When people change their attitude toward Him, His attitude toward them adjusts accordingly, as seen in narratives like Jonah’s mission to Nineveh (Jonah 3:10). His immutability guarantees the reliability of His word and the stability of His covenantal love.

God’s Emotions and Impassibility

While God experiences emotions such as joy, sorrow, and love, these feelings are always under His sovereign control. Theological discussions of God’s impassibility clarify that His emotions are deliberate, purposeful, and integral to His infinite being. Unlike humans, who are often at the mercy of uncontrollable emotions, God feels and acts in perfect harmony with His will.

Scriptures affirm the reality of God’s emotional engagement with His creation, showcasing His delight in righteousness, sorrow over sin, and love for His people. However, His emotions transcend human experience, reflecting His divine nature and purpose.

The Simplicity and Integration of God

God’s simplicity refers to the integration and unity of His being. Unlike humans, whose fragmented attention and divided motives stem from sin, God is wholly and undividedly present in all His thoughts and actions. He gives complete and simultaneous attention to every aspect of His creation, from the smallest details to the grandest designs (Matthew 10:29–30).

This simplicity highlights the marvel of God’s transcendent nature. While humans struggle to focus on one thing at a time, God effortlessly sustains and governs all things, demonstrating His unparalleled wisdom and power.

Worshiping God in Spirit and Truth

Jesus taught that true worship must be “in spirit and in truth” (John 4:24). This means worship arises from a heart renewed by the Holy Spirit and is grounded in the truth of God’s self-revelation. Rituals and external forms of worship are meaningless without genuine engagement of the heart, which only the Spirit can produce.

Worshiping “in truth” involves aligning our understanding with God’s revelation, particularly as seen in Jesus Christ, the incarnate Word. Through Him, we recognize our need for redemption and the glory of God’s grace. This worship transcends physical locations, for God’s presence is universally accessible through the Spirit (Hebrews 12:22–24). As Psalm 145:18 assures, “The LORD is near to all who call on him, to all who call on him in truth.”

This global availability of God’s presence is a profound benefit of the gospel, inviting all people to experience the nearness and love of the transcendent God.
