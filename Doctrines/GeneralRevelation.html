General Revelation: God's Power and Majesty Made Known

The Evident Reality of the Creator

The natural world is not a barrier obscuring God’s existence but a vivid declaration of His power and majesty. As Psalm 19:1 states, “The heavens declare the glory of God; the skies proclaim the work of His hands.” This truth is further affirmed by Paul in Romans 1:19–21, where he declares that God’s eternal power and divine nature are clearly seen in creation, leaving humanity without excuse. In Acts 17:28, Paul even cites a Greek poet to emphasize humanity’s divine origin. Moreover, God’s goodness is evident in His providential care (Acts 14:17; Romans 2:4), while His moral law is inscribed on every human conscience (Romans 2:14–15). Even the certainty of future judgment is universally known (Romans 1:32). These truths constitute the essence of general revelation.

General revelation, so called because it is universally available, has been present since the beginning of human history. Every human being receives this revelation simply by existing in God’s world. It reveals God’s power, praiseworthiness, and moral authority, leaving no one with a valid excuse for failing to acknowledge and serve Him. According to Paul’s indictment in Romans 1:18–3:19, the entire human race is guilty before God for willfully suppressing this truth in unrighteousness.

From General Revelation to Special Revelation

While general revelation declares God’s existence and moral claim, it is limited in scope. God has provided a fuller revelation of Himself as the Savior of sinners through Jesus Christ. This special or specific revelation is given in historical events, recorded in Scripture, and centers on the redemptive work of Christ. Special revelation not only reiterates the truths found in general revelation but also expands upon them, offering explicit teachings about God’s nature, purposes, and plan for salvation.

Special revelation enables believers to recognize God’s glory in the natural order, historical events, and human existence. As John Calvin aptly described, the world becomes a "theatre of the glory of God," where every aspect of creation points to its Creator. Special revelation, therefore, opens the eyes of believers to see the fullness of God’s majesty, which is evident yet veiled in general revelation.

The Implications of General Revelation

General revelation holds profound theological significance. It underscores humanity’s accountability before God, as every individual possesses an innate awareness of His power and moral authority. Denials of this knowledge, according to Scripture, are insincere and self-deceptive. This universal revelation forms the foundation for God’s judgment, as it demonstrates that humanity’s failure to honor and thank the Creator is willful rebellion against clear evidence.

However, general revelation is not sufficient for salvation. Its purpose is to reveal God’s existence, power, and moral claims, preparing the way for the acceptance of special revelation through the Gospel. Only in Christ is the full knowledge of God’s grace and the way of salvation revealed, offering hope to a world otherwise condemned by its rejection of general revelation.
