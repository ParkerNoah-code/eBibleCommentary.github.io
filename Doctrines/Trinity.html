God Is One and Three

Scriptural Foundations of Divine Unity

The concept of God’s singularity is a foundational truth of the Old Testament. Passages like Isaiah 44:6 declare, “This is what the LORD says—Israel’s King and Redeemer, the LORD Almighty: I am the first and I am the last; apart from me there is no God.” This insistence on monotheism is echoed throughout scripture, from Deuteronomy 6:4–5, which commands Israel to worship and love the Lord exclusively, to Isaiah 45:25, which underscores God’s sovereignty as the sole Creator and Redeemer.

In the New Testament, this monotheistic assertion continues with equal force. Jesus Himself quotes the Shema, affirming that “The Lord our God, the Lord is one” (Mark 12:29–30). Paul also emphasizes the oneness of God in passages like 1 Corinthians 8:4 and 1 Timothy 2:5, reminding readers that there is only one God to whom all allegiance is due. Yet, these texts also reveal the mystery of a triune God, as they introduce the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit as co-laborers in the work of salvation.

Revelation of the Trinity in the New Testament

The New Testament introduces a profound truth: the one God exists eternally as three persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. These are not mere manifestations or roles but distinct persons who share the same divine essence. This understanding arises from Jesus’ teachings and the accounts of His life and ministry. Jesus prayed to the Father and instructed His disciples to do likewise, yet He also claimed divinity for Himself, as seen in passages like John 20:28–31 and Colossians 2:9, where He is worshiped as God.

Furthermore, Jesus promised the coming of the Holy Spirit, describing Him as “another Paraclete” who would guide and empower believers (John 14:16–17). The Spirit’s arrival at Pentecost confirmed His divine personhood, as Peter equates lying to the Spirit with lying to God (Acts 5:3–4). The baptismal formula given by Jesus in Matthew 28:19—“in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit”—also reflects the unified yet distinct roles of the Trinity.

Historical Development of the Doctrine

The term "Trinity" comes from the Latin word trinitas, meaning “threeness.” While the technical language of Trinitarian theology, such as “subsistences” and “essence,” is not found in scripture, the doctrine is firmly rooted in biblical revelation. The early church recognized the need to articulate this mystery in order to safeguard against heresies like modalism (which denies the distinct persons) and tritheism (which posits three gods). Thus, the Nicene Creed and other historic formulations affirm the co-equality and co-eternality of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

Theological Significance and Practical Implications

The doctrine of the Trinity underscores the complexity of God’s unity. Each person of the Trinity is fully divine, fully personal, and fully involved in the work of salvation. The Father initiates the divine plan, the Son accomplishes it through His life, death, and resurrection, and the Spirit applies it to the lives of believers. This interdependent cooperation reveals a relational God who is love in His very essence.
