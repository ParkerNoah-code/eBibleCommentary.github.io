Two Natures

Jesus Christ Is Fully Human

The Apostle John warns of those who deny the humanity of Christ: "Many deceivers, who do not acknowledge Jesus Christ as coming in the flesh, have gone out into the world. Any such person is the deceiver and the antichrist" (2 John 7). This strong condemnation targets the heretical teachings of the Docetists, who argued that Jesus only appeared human, denying the reality of His Incarnation. Such views undermine the core of Christian belief—that Jesus, the Son of God, became fully human to redeem humanity.

Jesus' Genuine Human Experience

The Gospels provide ample evidence of Jesus' humanity. He experienced physical limitations like hunger (Matt. 4:2) and weariness (John 4:6). He displayed emotions, such as grief at Lazarus' death (John 11:35), and endured profound suffering, particularly in Gethsemane (Mark 14:32–42) and on the cross. The author of Hebrews emphasizes that Jesus’ human experiences qualify Him to serve as a compassionate helper and mediator. "Because he himself suffered when he was tempted, he is able to help those who are being tempted" (Heb. 2:18). His humanity assures believers that He understands and empathizes with their struggles.

Christians and the Minimization of Christ's Humanity

Throughout history, some Christians, focusing on Jesus’ divinity, have minimized His humanity. Early heresies like Monophysitism denied that Jesus had two distinct natures, instead asserting He possessed only a divine nature. Modern interpretations sometimes suggest that Jesus’ hunger, weariness, or ignorance were merely pretenses, attributing omniscience and omnipotence to Him at all times. However, these views distort the Incarnation, which teaches that Jesus fully embraced human experience while remaining divine.

The Incarnation means that Jesus lived a divine-human life through His human mind and body. He did not always exercise divine omniscience or omnipotence, instead relying on the Father’s will and drawing on divine resources only as needed. This complete identification with humanity allowed Him to experience life as we do, maximizing His empathy and solidarity with those He came to save.

The Unity of Two Natures

The union of Jesus' two natures—divine and human—cannot be understood as alternating states, as if He sometimes acted in His humanity and other times in His divinity. Every action, including His sufferings on the cross, was carried out by His unified divine-human person. This does not contradict the doctrine of divine impassibility, which holds that God experiences all things—including distress—by His own will and foreordaining decision.

Being divine, Jesus was impeccable, meaning He could not sin. However, this did not render Him immune to temptation. Satan's temptations, such as those in the wilderness (Matt. 4:1–11), and the constant pull to avoid the cross (Matt. 16:23; Luke 22:28), were genuine struggles. Yet, as the Son of God, it was His nature to obey the Father, resisting temptation until victory was achieved.

Christ's Temptations and Victory

The Gospels and Epistles provide glimpses into the intensity of Christ’s struggles with temptation. In Gethsemane, He faced an unparalleled spiritual battle, praying with such agony that His sweat fell like drops of blood (Luke 22:44). Despite the weight of these trials, His divine will to fulfill the Father’s purpose remained unshaken. His triumph over temptation serves as a source of hope and strength for believers facing their own challenges.

Hebrews captures the profound significance of Christ's humanity: "For we do not have a high priest who is unable to empathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are—yet he did not sin" (Heb. 4:15). His sinlessness amidst temptation assures believers of His ability to guide and sustain them in their own journeys of faith.

The Significance of Christ’s Humanity

The full humanity of Jesus is essential to Christian theology and devotion. It means that He shared in our weaknesses and limitations, providing a model of obedience and faithfulness. It also ensures that He is a sympathetic high priest, intimately acquainted with the human condition and fully equipped to intercede on our behalf. By embracing His humanity, Jesus became the perfect mediator, bridging the gap between God and humanity.

In Jesus, believers find both divine power and human empathy. His life, death, and resurrection demonstrate that the Word truly became flesh, dwelling among us to redeem and restore all who trust in Him.
