The Holy Spirit Equips the Church

The Role of Spiritual Gifts in the Church

In Ephesians 4:7, 11–12, the Apostle Paul writes, “But to each one of us grace has been given as Christ apportioned it.… He … gave some to be apostles, some to be prophets, some to be evangelists, and some to be pastors and teachers, to prepare God’s people for works of service, so that the body of Christ may be built up.” These verses highlight the essential role that spiritual gifts play in the life of the church. The Holy Spirit equips believers with gifts, enabling them to serve God and each other in ways that strengthen the body of Christ.

Spiritual gifts are not just for personal edification but are given for the benefit of the whole church. Paul stresses that each member of the church has a role to play, with the goal being to build up the church and bring it to maturity in Christ (Eph. 4:16). The gifts, both formal and informal, are given so that the church can grow in faith, love, and service. This growth occurs as every member contributes their unique gifts to the work of the body.

The Nature of Spiritual Gifts

The term “gift” in the New Testament refers to a “donation,” an expression of grace. Spiritual gifts are not earned or deserved but are given by Christ to His church. Paul explains in Ephesians 4 that these gifts include leadership roles such as apostles, prophets, evangelists, and pastors-teachers, who prepare God’s people for works of service. These gifts are given by the ascended Christ and are essential for the functioning and health of the church. The church’s leaders are called to equip others, ensuring that every believer understands and fulfills their calling to serve.

The gifts of the Holy Spirit are often referred to by two terms in the New Testament: "charismata" (gifts of grace) and "pneumatika" (spiritual gifts). Both terms underscore that these abilities are manifestations of God’s grace and the power of the Holy Spirit at work in the lives of believers (1 Cor. 12:4–1). Despite the complexities and debates surrounding the nature of specific gifts, three certainties stand out in understanding spiritual gifts.

Key Characteristics of Spiritual Gifts

First, spiritual gifts are abilities given to believers to express, celebrate, and display Christ. These gifts are intended to help build up others in the faith and communicate the truth of the gospel. The exercise of spiritual gifts is meant to reveal Christ in a way that strengthens the church and advances God’s kingdom.

Second, spiritual gifts can be divided into two categories: gifts of speech and gifts of loving, practical helpfulness. Gifts of speech, such as prophecy, teaching, and exhortation, involve communicating God’s truth to others. Gifts of helpfulness, such as serving, giving, leading, and showing mercy, involve meeting the practical and emotional needs of others. These categories are not meant to elevate one type of gift over another, but rather to highlight the diversity of gifts within the church. Both categories are essential for the church’s growth and health. No matter the gift, it must be used to build up the body of Christ and fulfill the mission of the church.

Third, every Christian has been given at least one spiritual gift. No one is without a gift, and it is each believer’s responsibility to discover, develop, and fully utilize the gifts God has entrusted to them (1 Cor. 12:7; Eph. 4:7). Spiritual gifts are not to be hoarded or ignored but used for the common good of the church. Every member plays a vital role in the body of Christ, contributing to the church’s growth and the advancement of God’s kingdom.

The Church and Spiritual Gifts

The New Testament presents the church as a community where every member is called to serve and contribute to the whole. Spiritual gifts are given not only for personal edification but for the building up of the entire body. The ideal is for all believers to be engaged in ministry, using their gifts in cooperation with one another. Church leaders, such as elders and deacons, play a vital role in facilitating and overseeing this ministry, ensuring that all gifts are used properly and in an orderly manner (Eph. 4:11–13).

The body of Christ grows as each part does its work (Eph. 4:16). Spiritual gifts are meant to be exercised in a spirit of love and humility, with the goal of glorifying God and serving others. When each believer faithfully uses their gifts, the church becomes a vibrant and mature community of faith, fulfilling its mission in the world. Spiritual gifts are not just for the church’s benefit but also for the world, as the church demonstrates the love and power of Christ through its actions.
