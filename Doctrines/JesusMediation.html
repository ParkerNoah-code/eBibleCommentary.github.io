Jesus Christ Is the Mediator Between God and Man

The Role of a Mediator

The saving work of Jesus Christ is encapsulated in His role as the “mediator between God and men” (1 Tim. 2:5). A mediator bridges the gap between two estranged parties, facilitating reconciliation. Humanity, estranged from God due to sin, is described in Scripture as “hostile to God” (Rom. 8:7), standing under His wrath for its rebellion (Rom. 1:18; 2:5–9). The role of a mediator requires a deep connection with both parties, enabling the mediator to represent their interests and foster reconciliation. In the Old Testament, Moses served as a mediator between God and Israel, communicating God’s law and interceding on behalf of the people (Exod. 20:18–21; 32:9–33:17; Gal. 3:19). However, Christ fulfills this role on a cosmic and eternal scale.

Christ’s Work of Reconciliation

Reconciliation between humanity and God required addressing two critical barriers: God’s wrath against sin and humanity’s anti-God nature. In an act of mercy, the Father sent the Son to bring about this reconciliation. It was not a case of a benevolent Son pacifying a harsh Father; instead, the Father’s initiative flowed from His love, even for those under His judgment. As John 3:16 declares, “For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son.” Christ carried out this mission in perfect obedience to the Father’s will.

On the cross, Jesus achieved reconciliation through penal substitution, bearing the punishment for humanity’s sins (Gal. 3:13). By His sacrificial death, He absorbed God’s wrath, satisfied divine justice, and made peace for sinners (Eph. 2:16; Col. 1:20). This peace means the removal of hostility and guilt, granting believers pardon and permanent acceptance before God. Those who, through faith, receive this reconciliation are justified and enjoy peace with God (Rom. 5:1, 10).

Jesus as Mediator of the New Covenant

Jesus is described as “the mediator of a new covenant” (Heb. 9:15; 12:24), establishing a relationship of peace between God and humanity that surpasses the Old Testament system. Unlike the sacrificial system, which was limited and temporary, Christ’s sacrifice is once-for-all, permanently dealing with sin and its consequences (Heb. 9:11–10:18). As mediator of the new covenant, Jesus initiates and sustains a transformative relationship between believers and God, characterized by forgiveness, peace, and spiritual renewal.

The Threefold Office of Christ

John Calvin highlighted the New Testament’s teaching that Christ’s mediatorial work is expressed through three distinct yet unified offices: prophet, priest, and king. In His prophetic office, Jesus proclaims the Word of God, revealing God’s truth and will. As priest, He offers Himself as the perfect sacrifice for sin and continues to intercede for believers before the Father. As king, He reigns with sovereign authority over all creation, guiding His church and advancing His kingdom.

These three roles converge uniquely in Jesus. Whereas in the Old Testament, prophets, priests, and kings were distinct roles held by different individuals, Jesus embodies all three offices in one person. In Hebrews, we see this multifaceted role clearly: Jesus is the exalted king seated at God’s right hand (Heb. 1:3, 13; 2:9), the great High Priest who offered Himself as the ultimate sacrifice (Heb. 2:17; 7–10), and the prophet-apostle who proclaims God’s truth (Heb. 3:1; 2:3).

The Call to Follow the Mediator

Believers are called to respond to Christ’s mediatorial work by aligning their lives with His offices. We are to learn from Him as our prophet, trusting in His Word and guidance. We rely on Him as our priest, drawing near to God through His sacrifice and intercession. Finally, we submit to Him as our king, seeking to obey His commands and participate in His kingdom work.

This comprehensive view of Christ’s mediation is central to authentic Christianity. It reminds us that Jesus is not only the means of reconciliation but also the source of life, truth, and hope. To center one’s faith on Him in His fullness is to grasp the heart of the Gospel and to live as His disciple.

Conclusion

Jesus Christ’s role as mediator is the foundation of the Christian faith. By reconciling humanity to God through His sacrifice, He fulfilled the ultimate requirement for peace with God. His mediatorial ministry, expressed through His offices of prophet, priest, and king, demonstrates His sufficiency as Savior. Believers are called to trust, follow, and worship Him in response to His unparalleled work of mediation, finding their hope and identity in Him alone.
