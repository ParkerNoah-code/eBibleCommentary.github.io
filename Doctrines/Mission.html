Christ Sends the Church Into the World

The Command to Go

In John 20:21, Jesus declares, "Peace be with you! As the Father has sent me, I am sending you." This statement captures the essence of the Christian mission: the church is sent into the world to fulfill a purpose that aligns with Christ’s own mission. The Latin word "missio," meaning "sending," forms the foundation for the Christian concept of mission. Just as the Father sent Jesus into the world to reveal His truth and offer salvation, so too are believers called to carry that message to the world. Every Christian, every local congregation, and the universal church are all part of this divine sending and have a role to play in God's plan.

The task entrusted to the church is twofold: it involves both proclamation and compassionate action. The church is to bear witness to Christ and His redemptive work in the world, and it is also called to engage in acts of mercy and neighbor-love, demonstrating the power of the gospel through tangible deeds of kindness.

Proclaiming the Gospel: The First Task

The primary responsibility of the church is to make disciples of all nations, to baptize them, and to teach them to obey everything Christ has commanded (Matt. 28:19–20). This charge includes spreading the good news of Jesus Christ, proclaiming His identity as the incarnate Son of God, Lord, and Savior, and inviting all people to turn to Christ in repentance and faith. The gospel message is to be preached everywhere, offering God’s invitation to find life in Jesus. This is the central task of the church, modeled by the Apostle Paul in his own ministry of evangelism and church planting across the Roman world (Rom. 1:14; 1 Cor. 9:19–23).

Jesus, while anticipating a global mission, also focused His earthly ministry on "the lost sheep of Israel" (Matt. 15:24). His message began with the Jewish people, but it was always intended to extend to the Gentiles. The Apostle Paul, in fulfilling his calling as the apostle to the Gentiles, first went to the Jews in every city where he preached, underscoring the importance of sharing the gospel with the Jewish people (Acts 13:5, 42–48; 14:1–7; 16:13; Rom. 1:16).

Mercy and Compassion: The Second Task

Alongside the proclamation of the gospel, Christians are called to practice mercy and compassion toward others. This neighbor-love, as demonstrated by Jesus in His own ministry, is an integral part of the mission. Jesus healed the sick, fed the hungry, and showed compassion for the marginalized (Matt. 9:36; 15:32; Mark 1:41; Luke 7:13). As followers of Christ, Christians are called to emulate this example by responding to the physical, emotional, and spiritual needs of those around them. Acts of mercy serve as a powerful witness to the transformative power of the gospel.

This dual mission of word and deed is essential. The church must proclaim the message of salvation and live out that message through loving actions. If Christians fail to live out the love they proclaim, the credibility of the gospel is undermined. However, when Christians demonstrate Christ’s love through compassionate actions, it gives credibility to their words and leads others to glorify God (Matt. 5:16; 1 Pet. 2:11–12). Good works should be visible and reflect the grace that has been shown to them.

The Mission of the Church: A Global Task

The mission of the church is universal in scope, intended for all peoples. While Jesus' ministry was focused on Israel, He also envisioned the spread of the gospel to the Gentiles (Matt. 24:14; John 10:16; 12:32). The church’s mission continues to be global in its reach. However, this global mission must also honor the divine appointment of the Jewish people as the first recipients of the gospel. Christian Jews, while freed from ceremonial law, retain their cultural heritage and should not be expected to abandon their Jewish identity. The belief that Jewish Christians should forgo their Jewishness in favor of a purely Gentile identity is a cultural prejudice with no biblical foundation.

Conclusion: Fulfilling the Mission

The church is sent into the world on the king’s business, with the charge to make disciples, to proclaim Christ, and to demonstrate His love through acts of mercy and compassion. Both aspects of this mission—evangelism and service—are essential in fulfilling the Great Commission. Christians are called to be faithful witnesses of the gospel, showing through their lives the power of Christ to transform the world. As they fulfill this mission, they participate in the ongoing work of God's kingdom, bringing glory to His name.
