Salvation Brings Freedom

The Freedom Found in Christ

The New Testament presents salvation in Christ as a form of liberation, with the Christian life characterized by freedom. Galatians 5:1 declares, "It is for freedom that Christ has set us free," emphasizing that the essence of Christ’s redemptive work is to bring freedom to those who believe in Him. This freedom is not just a sociopolitical or economic change but a profound spiritual liberty. Christ has freed believers from three main forms of bondage: the law, sin’s domination, and the superstition that wrongly treats the physical world and pleasures as inherently evil.

The freedom Christ offers is not just a one-time event but an ongoing experience. John 8:32 and 8:36 affirm that the truth of Christ sets people free, and this freedom is a core component of the believer’s identity in Christ. Christ’s liberating work brings lasting spiritual freedom, a freedom that enables believers to live joyfully in obedience to God, serve Him freely, and embrace all the good gifts He has created for enjoyment.

Freedom from the Law

First, Christ sets believers free from the law as a system of salvation. Under the law, humans were bound to obey a set of rules in order to earn favor with God, yet the law, as Paul explains in Romans and Galatians, is incapable of bringing righteousness. It can only reveal sin, make people aware of their guilt, and highlight the impossibility of perfect law-keeping (Rom. 3:19; Gal. 3:10). The law, therefore, condemns but does not redeem. The futility of seeking righteousness through legalistic obedience is made clear through Paul’s writings, and it is this very bondage to the law that Christ’s death and resurrection set believers free from.

With justification by faith in Christ, Christians are no longer under the law but under grace (Rom. 6:14). Their standing with God is not based on their own works or law-keeping but entirely on Christ's finished work. This liberation means that Christians live not by perfection but by forgiveness, knowing that their acceptance by God is secured through Christ alone, and this freedom cannot be undone by any failure on their part.

Freedom from Sin's Domination

Second, Christians are freed from the domination of sin. Before coming to Christ, believers were enslaved to sin, unable to break free from the cycle of disobedience and the inherent desire to rebel against God's law (John 8:34–36; Rom. 6:14–23). In salvation, however, believers are regenerated through union with Christ in His death and resurrection (Rom. 6:3–11). This union with Christ changes the believer’s deepest desires, enabling them to serve God and pursue righteousness. The Holy Spirit empowers them to live according to God's will, and they are no longer under the tyranny of sin’s rule. Their desire to obey God's commands is now motivated by love and gratitude rather than by obligation or resentment.

In Romans 7:6, Paul describes this change as "serving in the new way of the Spirit, and not in the old way of the written code." The freedom from sin’s domination is not merely the absence of sin, but the positive presence of God’s grace, which transforms the believer’s heart and enables them to live in a way that honors God. Sin no longer reigns over them, and they can live a life characterized by obedience and joy.

Freedom from the Superstition About the Physical World

Third, Christ’s freedom also extends to the way Christians view the physical world and pleasures. Some religious systems, especially in the first century, viewed matter and physical pleasure as inherently evil, which led to ascetic practices and the rejection of earthly joys. However, Paul teaches that Christians are free to enjoy the good gifts of creation, including physical pleasures, as long as they do not transgress the moral law or hinder their spiritual well-being or that of others (1 Tim. 4:1–5; 1 Cor. 6:12–13; 8:7–13). The Reformers revived this biblical understanding, emphasizing that Christians should not be bound by legalistic views that treat the physical world as evil.

The freedom that Christ brings allows believers to enjoy life’s pleasures as gifts from God, with the understanding that their ultimate satisfaction is found in Him. Christians are free to engage in the world, enjoying God’s creation, as long as they do so in a way that glorifies Him and serves the good of others.

The Liberating Power of Christ’s Salvation

Ultimately, the salvation Christ brings is one of total freedom. This freedom is not freedom to sin but freedom to live in obedience to God, free from the fear of condemnation and the power of sin. It is a freedom that empowers believers to live fully in the love and grace of God, to serve Him wholeheartedly, and to enjoy the fullness of life that He provides. Christ has set believers free to be all that God created them to be, living in the freedom that comes from the truth of the gospel.

Conclusion: Freedom in Christ

In conclusion, salvation in Christ brings true freedom in every aspect of life. Believers are set free from the law, from sin’s control, and from the superstition that views physical pleasures as evil. This freedom is not a license to sin, but the ability to live a life pleasing to God, empowered by the Holy Spirit. The freedom found in Christ enables believers to fully enjoy life, serve God, and love others, knowing that their acceptance by God rests solely in Christ and His work on their behalf.
