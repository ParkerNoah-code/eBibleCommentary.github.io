A Christian Changes Radically

The Nature of Repentance

The New Testament presents repentance as a profound and radical change in one's life. The term "repentance" itself means a complete transformation in one’s mind and life. This change involves a shift in views, values, goals, and behaviors, affecting the mind, judgment, will, affections, and actions. Repentance is not merely an outward modification but an inward revolution that compels a person to live in a completely new way. As stated in Acts 26:20, repentance involves a turning to God and proving that change by one’s deeds.

Repentance was central to the message of the New Testament, being proclaimed by John the Baptist (Matt. 3:2), Jesus (Matt. 4:17), and the apostles (Mark 6:12; Acts 2:38). It is also the consistent theme of the Old Testament prophets, who called Israel to return to God after their waywardness (Jer. 23:22; Zech. 1:3–6). Repentance is always presented as the path to the forgiveness of sins and reconciliation with God, while impenitence is shown as leading to spiritual destruction (Luke 13:1–8).

Repentance as the Fruit of Faith

Repentance and faith are inseparable in the Christian life. While faith is the positive aspect of turning to Christ, repentance is the negative aspect, which involves turning away from sin and turning toward God. True repentance is a necessary expression of genuine faith. The idea that one can have faith without repentance or accept Christ as Savior without submitting to Him as Lord is a dangerous misconception. To acknowledge Christ as Savior is to recognize Him as Lord as well, and true saving faith involves both aspects. Without repentance, faith becomes incomplete and ineffective, as it fails to involve a total commitment to Christ.

The Westminster Confession of Faith articulates repentance as a deep sorrow and grief for sin, born not merely from the fear of punishment but from a recognition of the offensiveness of sin against God’s holy nature and righteous law. Genuine repentance involves a sincere desire to turn away from sin and live in obedience to God's commandments. This is not just a momentary feeling of remorse but a permanent and active turning toward God.

Attrition vs. Contrition

The concept of "attrition" refers to a sorrow for sin that stems from fear of punishment rather than a true hatred of sin itself. This kind of repentance is insufficient because it does not involve a commitment to forsake sin and live according to God's will. In contrast, "contrition" is a deeper, more genuine form of repentance. It is characterized by a heartfelt sorrow for sin and a firm purpose to sin no more. This is the repentance modeled by King David in Psalm 51, where he expresses his deep regret for his actions and his resolve to live in alignment with God's will. True repentance is evidenced by a changed life—a life that strives to practice the virtues opposed to the sins repented of. As John the Baptist preached in Luke 3:8, and as Paul affirmed in Acts 26:20, repentance must be demonstrated by a transformed life.

Repentance and New Life

Repentance is not simply a matter of feeling sorry for past actions; it involves a radical shift in one's entire life direction. To repent is to turn away from sin and pursue righteousness, to forsake former ways and adopt a lifestyle that reflects one's new identity in Christ. This change is not merely external but internal, affecting one's desires, thoughts, and actions. Repentance, therefore, is not a one-time event but a continual turning toward God, a commitment to live in His light and according to His will.

Conclusion: The Necessity of True Repentance

In summary, repentance is a vital and transformative part of the Christian faith. It involves a radical reorientation of one's life, turning from sin and self-centeredness to God and His righteousness. While faith is the means by which we are justified, repentance is the evidence of genuine faith. It is the heart’s response to God’s mercy, acknowledging our sin and seeking His forgiveness. True repentance leads to a new life, characterized by obedience to God’s commands and a desire to live according to His will. Without repentance, faith is incomplete, for it is in repentance that faith is truly expressed and proven genuine.
