Good Works as an Expression of Faith

Introduction to the Relationship Between Faith and Works

The New Testament teaches that faith is the central and crucial means of salvation. Through faith, Christians are justified before God, sustained in their walk with Him, and secured in their hope for the future (Rom. 3:26; 4:1–5; Gal. 2:16; Heb. 10:35–12:3). However, the relationship between faith and works is complex. In James 2:24, it is stated, "You see that a person is justified by what he does and not by faith alone." This statement has often been misunderstood, yet it aligns with a broader biblical teaching that faith, while essential, is not a passive or purely intellectual exercise—it must be evidenced in action.

The Nature of Faith in the New Testament

Faith, according to the New Testament, is not merely a subjective feeling or a confident mindset; it is a response to the testimony of God. Faith is directed towards God Himself, His promises, and the person of Jesus Christ as revealed in the Scriptures. It is a whole-souled response that involves not just mental assent, but also the heart, will, and affections. Theologians in the Reformed tradition have historically understood faith in three dimensions: notitia (knowledge), assensus (agreement), and fiducia (trust and reliance). These aspects together form the basis of genuine faith, which goes beyond mere acknowledgment of facts to a deep personal dependence on God's grace for salvation.

As Calvin described, faith is "a firm and sure knowledge of the divine favor towards us, founded on the truth of a free promise in Christ, and revealed to our minds and sealed on our hearts by the Holy Spirit." Faith, therefore, is not merely intellectual but involves the whole person, anchored in the assurance provided by the Holy Spirit.

Faith and Justification

The doctrine of justification by faith is central to the Christian faith, famously affirmed by the Apostle Paul, particularly in Romans 3:28, where he states, "For we maintain that a person is justified by faith apart from the works of the law." This justification is not based on human merit or works but solely on the work of Christ. Any attempt to rely on works for justification is seen as the heresy of legalism. Luther's principle of "faith alone" is a cornerstone of Reformation theology, emphasizing that salvation is a gift, not something earned by human effort.

However, this does not mean that works are irrelevant. While good works do not contribute to salvation, they are the inevitable and natural fruit of genuine faith. If someone claims to have faith but lives in a way that contradicts that claim, their faith is not genuine. As James argues, "faith without works is dead" (James 2:26), indicating that true faith must be visible through a transformed life and actions that reflect the love and grace received from God.

The Role of Works in the Christian Life

Although works cannot justify us, they are the expression of the faith that justifies. Faith in God leads to a life of obedience and service, as believers are transformed by the love of Christ. This is not merely a matter of outward conformity but of inward change, where the heart's desires are aligned with God's will. Good works, then, are a natural outflow of a heart that has been regenerated by God's grace, and they reflect the love and fidelity that faith calls forth.

James 2:24 is often interpreted as emphasizing that works demonstrate the genuineness of faith, not that they contribute to salvation in a legalistic sense. James argues that faith without works is like a body without breath—it is lifeless. By contrast, works that are the result of faith show that a person's professed faith is genuine and active.

Salvation and the Rewards of Good Works

It is important to clarify that, while good works do not earn salvation, they are the basis on which God rewards His people. Paul writes in Philippians 3:12-14 and 2 Timothy 4:7-8 about the heavenly rewards awaiting those who live faithfully in Christ. These rewards, as Augustine noted, are not a repayment for good works but the gracious crowning of God's gifts. It is God’s grace that enables believers to do good works, and His grace is also the basis on which He rewards them.

Conclusion: The Inseparability of Faith and Works

In conclusion, while justification is by faith alone, the faith that justifies is never alone. It is always accompanied by good works, as the believer's life is transformed by the power of the Holy Spirit. Good works are not the means of salvation, but they are the evidence of a living, active faith. This faith, grounded in knowledge of the gospel and personal trust in Christ, produces a life of obedience, love, and service to God and others, demonstrating the authenticity of one’s faith and the hope of eternal rewards.
