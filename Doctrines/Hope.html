Hoping Is Basic to the Christian Outlook

The Nature of Christian Hope

Christian hope is rooted in the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. As stated in Romans 15:4, “For everything that was written in the past was written to teach us, so that through endurance and the encouragement of the Scriptures we might have hope.” Hope in the Christian faith is not merely wishful thinking or a vague sense of optimism but a deep, assured confidence in the promises of God, rooted in His past actions and His future promises. It is the steadfast expectation that, just as Christ accomplished salvation in the past, He will fulfill His promises in the future.

Christians live between the two comings of Christ: looking back to His first coming, which secured salvation, and looking forward to His second coming, when He will bring His followers into eternal glory. This tension between the already and the not yet shapes the Christian worldview. New Testament devotion is consistently oriented around this hope, as Christ is called “our hope” (1 Tim. 1:1) and believers are encouraged to set their hope fully on the grace to be revealed at Christ’s return (1 Pet. 1:13). This hope is not a fleeting wish but an anchor for the soul (Heb. 6:18–19), giving believers strength and endurance as they wait for the fulfillment of God’s promises.

The Ethics of Hope

The Christian ethic is deeply shaped by hope. The New Testament outlines several dimensions of this hope-driven ethic:

A Pilgrim Ethic

Christians are called to see themselves as pilgrims or strangers in this world, journeying toward their true home in heaven. This perspective, rooted in passages like 1 Peter 2:11 and Hebrews 11:13, reminds believers that their citizenship is not in this world but in the kingdom of God. As pilgrims, they are to remain focused on their final destination, not becoming overly attached to the transient things of this world.

A Purity Ethic

Christian hope also has a purifying effect on the believer’s life. As 1 John 3:3 teaches, “Everyone who has this hope in him purifies himself, just as he is pure.” The hope of Christ’s return compels Christians to live in holiness, striving to become more like Jesus in character and behavior. This ethic of purity is not driven by fear or legalism but by a deep longing for the complete transformation that will come when Christ is revealed.

A Preparedness Ethic

The hope of Christ’s return also encourages an ethic of preparedness. Christians are called to live with the readiness to meet Christ at any time, whether through His return or their own death. Passages like 2 Corinthians 5:6–8 and Philippians 1:21–24 stress that Christians should live with a sense of urgency and anticipation, always ready to leave this world for a closer relationship with their Savior.

A Patience Ethic

Hope in Christ also cultivates patience. As Romans 8:25 teaches, “If we hope for what we do not yet have, we wait for it patiently.” The Christian life is marked by perseverance, as believers endure suffering and hardship with the confidence that their hope will one day be fully realized. This patience is not passive resignation but an active trust in God’s timing, knowing that the trials of this life are temporary and that eternal glory awaits.

A Power Ethic

Finally, hope provides the believer with strength and energy to live faithfully. The power of Christian hope energizes believers for the Christian race, enabling them to endure hardships and persevere in the face of trials. As Romans 15:13 states, “May the God of hope fill you with all joy and peace as you trust in him, so that you may overflow with hope by the power of the Holy Spirit.” Hope gives believers the confidence to face challenges, knowing that their suffering is not in vain but is preparing them for a greater glory to come (2 Cor. 4:17).

Hope in the Face of Suffering

The Christian life is often marked by suffering and hardship. As 1 Corinthians 4:8–13, 2 Corinthians 4:7–18, and Acts 14:22 remind us, believers will face difficulties and trials. However, Christian hope transforms these difficulties. Our hope is not based on an immediate or temporary sense of triumph but on the eternal victory that Christ has secured. Even in the face of suffering, Christians can maintain unquenchable confidence because their hope is grounded in the finished work of Christ and the promise of future glory. As Paul writes in Romans 8:18, “I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us.”

Conclusion: Living with Hope

Hope is not merely a passive expectation of the future but a transformative power that shapes how Christians live today. It is an active trust in God’s promises, a motivation for purity and perseverance, and a source of strength in the midst of hardship. Christians are called to live as pilgrims, with their eyes set on their heavenly home, purified by the hope of Christ’s return, and strengthened to endure. The hope that Christians have in Christ gives them the confidence to face any challenge, knowing that the victory is already secured in Him.
