Depravity Infects Everyone

The Universality of Sin

In Psalm 51:5, David confesses, "Surely I was sinful at birth, sinful from the time my mother conceived me." This verse underscores the pervasive reality of sin, affirming its presence in every person from the moment of existence. Scripture consistently diagnoses sin as a universal deformity of human nature, affecting every aspect of human life (1 Kings 8:46; Rom. 3:9–23; 7:18; 1 John 1:8–10).

The Bible portrays sin as rebellion against God's authority, a deviation from His standards, and an offense to His purity. It is not merely an abstract concept but a dynamic force—a drive to resist God's rule and assert self-sovereignty. This rebellious spirit, first seen in Adam's transgression, manifests in pride and hostility against God. Sinful actions, therefore, are the external expressions of an inwardly corrupted heart.

Defining Sin

Sin, as illustrated in Scripture, is a failure to conform to God's law in every aspect of human existence, including actions, attitudes, motivations, and character. Jeremiah 17:9 describes the human heart as "deceitful above all things and beyond cure." Similarly, Jesus points to the inner nature of sin in Matthew 12:30–37 and Mark 7:20–23, showing that evil originates within the human heart.

Paul's writings, particularly in Romans 1:18–3:20 and Romans 7, expose the pervasive power of sin, depicting it as a principle that enslaves and corrupts. Galatians 5:16–21 and Ephesians 2:1–3 further highlight the manifestations of sin in human behavior and thought. The New Testament consistently identifies sin as both a state of being and a pattern of actions contrary to God's will.

Original Sin

The concept of original sin—though not a biblical term but one coined by Augustine—provides a framework for understanding humanity's inherent sinfulness. It asserts that sinfulness marks everyone from birth, existing as a twisted motivational core before any actual sins are committed. This inherited condition stems from Adam, humanity's representative, whose disobedience introduced sin into the human race.

Original sin teaches that we are not sinners because we commit sinful acts; rather, we commit sinful acts because we are inherently sinners. This doctrine emphasizes the depth of human corruption and the need for divine intervention to restore and redeem.

Total Depravity and Total Inability

Total depravity, a term closely tied to original sin, describes the extent of human corruption. It does not mean that every person is as evil as they could be but that no part of human nature remains untouched by sin. Consequently, no action or thought is entirely free from imperfection or taint in God's eyes.

This corruption results in total inability—the incapacity to respond to God or His Word sincerely and wholeheartedly. Paul characterizes this state as spiritual death (Eph. 2:1, 5; Col. 2:13), indicating the profound unresponsiveness of the fallen heart to God's call. The Westminster Confession captures this reality: "Man by his fall into a state of sin, hath wholly lost all ability of will to any spiritual good accompanying salvation; so as a natural man, being altogether averse from that good, and dead in sin, is not able by his own strength to convert himself, or to prepare himself thereunto."

Only the grace of God, revealed through Christ, can overcome this spiritual deadness. Without divine intervention, humanity remains enslaved to sin, incapable of achieving righteousness or reconciliation with God on its own.
