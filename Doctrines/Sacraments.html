Christ Instituted Two Seals of God’s Covenant

Biblical Foundation of the Sacraments

In Romans 4:11, Paul describes the covenantal sign of circumcision, stating, "[Abraham] … received the sign of circumcision, a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised." This verse underscores the concept of a covenant seal, which points to a broader biblical framework that includes the two sacraments instituted by Christ: baptism and the Lord’s Supper. These rites, essential to Christian faith, signify God's covenant with His people, offering both initiation into the faith and ongoing remembrance of the gospel.

Christ instituted baptism as a once-for-all rite of initiation (Matt. 28:19; Gal. 3:27) and the Lord’s Supper as a regular rite of remembrance (1 Cor. 11:23–26). These rites, referred to as "sacraments" in the Western church, "mysteries" in the Eastern Orthodox tradition, and "ordinances" by some Protestant groups, are critical elements of the Christian faith. While the Bible does not offer a single term for these acts, their Old Testament counterparts—circumcision and the Passover—serve as analogous rites that reinforce God's covenant relationship with His people (Gen. 17:9–14; Exod. 12:1–27).

The Meaning of Sacraments

The term "sacrament" comes from the Latin word *sacramentum*, which originally referred to a soldier's sacred oath of allegiance but later came to signify any holy rite. The sacraments, in their biblical context, are outward signs that convey spiritual grace. They are actions instituted by Christ that use visible elements—such as water, bread, and wine—to communicate the invisible blessings of God's covenant. Through these signs, God affirms His promises to believers, confirming their participation in the covenant and their allegiance to Him.

The sacraments serve to distinguish the church from the world and engage believers in a deeper commitment to God's service. The Westminster Confession of Faith states that the sacraments "put a visible difference between those that belong unto the church and the rest of the world, and solemnly to engage them to the service of God in Christ, according to his word." This idea reinforces the notion that sacraments not only symbolize God's promises but also serve as a sign of the believer's response to those promises.

Distinguishing Sacraments from Other Rites

While the medieval church mistakenly classified five other rites—confirmation, penance, marriage, ordination, and extreme unction—as sacraments, the biblical understanding restricts the term "sacrament" to baptism and the Lord’s Supper. These two rites are unique in that they serve as seals of a covenant relationship with God, offering tangible signs of God's grace. The other rites, although significant in the Christian tradition, lack the same biblical foundation as covenant seals. They do not have the same visible signs or ceremonies ordained by God, which is why they are not classified as sacraments (Thirty-Nine Articles XXV).

The Means of Grace Through Sacraments

The sacraments are rightly viewed as "means of grace," as they are instruments through which God strengthens and deepens a believer's faith. Their efficacy does not depend on the minister’s personal virtue but on God’s faithfulness to fulfill His promises. Christ and the apostles often speak of the sacramental signs as though they are the things they symbolize. For example, in Matthew 26:26–28 and 1 Corinthians 10:15–21, Christ’s body and blood are represented by the bread and wine, and receiving these elements is equated with receiving Christ Himself. This highlights the deep spiritual significance of the sacraments.

The sacraments make the gospel visible, just as the preaching of the Word makes it audible. Through these rituals, God stirs up faith, reinforcing the believer's confidence in His promises. The Heidelberg Catechism illustrates this in its answer to Question 75, where it states that Christ has given assurance through the bread and wine, offering a visible guarantee of His sacrifice and the nourishment of the soul to eternal life. The visible elements—bread and wine—become tokens of Christ's body and blood, affirming the believer's participation in His redemptive work.

Seeing Is Believing

The sacraments operate on the principle that "seeing is believing." As the believer physically sees and tastes the bread and wine, they are reminded of the invisible spiritual realities they signify. These visible signs connect Christian beliefs with the testimony of the senses, allowing the believer to engage their faith in a deeply personal and experiential way. The sacraments thus serve as means of grace, strengthening the believer's faith by correlating the spiritual truth of God's covenant with tangible experiences.
