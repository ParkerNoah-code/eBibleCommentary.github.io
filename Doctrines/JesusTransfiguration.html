How Jesus Christ’s Glory Was Revealed

The Event of the Transfiguration

The Transfiguration of Jesus, recorded in Matthew 17:1–8, Mark 9:2–8, and Luke 9:28–36, reveals a pivotal moment where Christ’s divine glory was momentarily unveiled. As described in Mark: "After six days Jesus took Peter, James and John with him and led them up a high mountain, where they were all alone. There he was transfigured before them. His clothes became dazzling white, whiter than anyone in the world could bleach them. And there appeared before them Elijah and Moses, who were talking with Jesus" (Mark 9:2–4). This dramatic event underscores Jesus' divine identity and the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy.

Jesus’ purpose in bringing Peter, James, and John was deliberate. He intended for them to witness this event and later testify to its significance (Matt. 17:9; cf. 2 Pet. 1:16–18; John 1:14). Through this revelation, they glimpsed a foretaste of the glory that Christ would fully reveal at His return and the transformation believers will experience in the resurrection (Phil. 3:20–21).

The Glory of Christ Revealed

The Transfiguration revealed Jesus as "the radiance of God’s glory" (Heb. 1:3). As Jesus prayed (Luke 9:29), His appearance changed, and a bright light emanated from Him, even through His clothing. This light was the intrinsic glory of the divine Son, temporarily unveiled to His closest disciples. It highlighted Jesus' identity as the Son of God and His unique role in God’s redemptive plan.

A voice from the cloud declared, "This is my Son, whom I love. Listen to him!" (Mark 9:7). This divine proclamation affirmed Jesus’ divine identity and authority, reinforcing the visual manifestation of His glory. The disciples were given not only a glimpse of Christ’s majesty but also a confirmation of His mission and their calling to trust Him.

Moses and Elijah: The Law and the Prophets

The appearance of Moses and Elijah alongside Jesus carries deep symbolic meaning. Moses, representing the Law, and Elijah, representing the Prophets, testified to Jesus as the fulfillment of God’s covenant promises. Their presence affirmed that the Law and the Prophets pointed forward to Christ and that His mission would supersede their roles.

The conversation between Jesus, Moses, and Elijah about His "departure" (Greek: exodos) (Luke 9:31) referred to His impending death, resurrection, and ascension. Just as Moses led Israel in the exodus from Egypt, redeeming them from slavery, Jesus' exodus would redeem humanity from the bondage of sin. His departure was not merely a physical leaving of the world but the climactic act of salvation history.

Return to Ministry and the Path to Calvary

Following the Transfiguration, Jesus once again veiled His glory and descended the mountain to continue His ministry. The disciples, still grappling with what they had witnessed, were instructed not to speak of it until after the resurrection (Matt. 17:9). This veil of secrecy reflected Jesus' focus on fulfilling His mission as the suffering Savior.

F. B. Meyer poignantly observes, “The door through which Moses and Elijah had come stood open, and by it our Lord might have returned. But he could never, under those circumstances, have been the Saviour of mankind. He knew this, so he set his face toward Calvary.” Jesus' choice to remain on the path to the cross underscores His commitment to His redemptive mission. The Transfiguration provided a momentary glimpse of His divine majesty but also pointed forward to the suffering and victory that would achieve salvation for humanity.

Conclusion

The Transfiguration of Jesus is a profound revelation of His divine glory and the fulfillment of Old Testament promises. It serves as a foretaste of the glory to be revealed in His second coming and the transformation awaiting believers in the resurrection. Through the presence of Moses and Elijah, the Transfiguration unites the Law, the Prophets, and the Gospel in a singular testimony to Jesus as the Messiah. Above all, it highlights Jesus’ unwavering commitment to His mission, setting His face toward Calvary to accomplish redemption for humanity.
