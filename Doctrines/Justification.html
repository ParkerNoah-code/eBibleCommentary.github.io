Salvation is by Grace Through Faith

The Doctrine of Justification

The doctrine of justification is central to the Christian faith, particularly in the teachings of the Apostle Paul. For Paul, justification is not merely a theological idea but the very heart of the gospel. In his letters, especially in Romans and Galatians, Paul emphasizes that justification is God's act of declaring sinners righteous, not on the basis of their works but through faith in Jesus Christ (Rom. 3:21–5:21; Gal. 2:15–5:1). This doctrine was the cornerstone of the Reformation, shaping both the theology and life of countless Christians for centuries.

Justification is a judicial act by God in which He pardons sinners, accepts them as just, and restores their estranged relationship with Himself. This act is a gift from God, made possible through the righteousness of Jesus Christ. Paul speaks of this gift of righteousness as something that is bestowed upon believers because of Jesus' obedience and His sacrificial death on their behalf (2 Cor. 5:21). Justification, therefore, is not based on human merit but on Christ's merit alone.

The Basis of Justification: The Righteousness of Christ

At first glance, the concept of God declaring sinners righteous might seem unjust, as it appears to contradict God's own law, which demands justice and punishment for sin (Deut. 25:1; Prov. 17:15). However, God's declaration of righteousness is entirely just because it is grounded in the righteousness of Jesus Christ. Jesus, as the "last Adam" (1 Cor. 15:45), fulfilled the law perfectly on behalf of humanity and endured the punishment for sin that humanity deserved. This is what Paul refers to as the "merit" of Christ's work—His righteousness is credited to those who have faith in Him (Rom. 5:18–19).

This concept of justification is not merely a future event but is applied in the present. It is as though the final judgment of God, where our eternal destiny is determined, is brought into the present moment. Once declared justified, believers are eternally secure in their relationship with God. This judgment, which is final and unchangeable, ensures that the believer's salvation is secure (Rom. 5:1–5; 8:30).

The Role of Faith in Justification

The means by which justification is received is faith in Jesus Christ. As Paul teaches in Romans 4:23–25, justification is through faith in the crucified and risen Christ. It is not by works or human effort but by placing personal faith in Christ that believers are justified before God. This faith is not a passive acknowledgment of facts but an active, trustful reliance on Christ's finished work on the cross.

The act of faith can be understood as "closing with Christ," where a believer embraces Jesus as both Savior and Lord. In doing so, they receive the righteousness of Christ, and through this divine exchange, they are pardoned and accepted by God (Gal. 2:15–16; 3:24). Faith, therefore, is the instrumental cause of justification, the means through which God's grace is applied to the individual.

The Catholic View of Justification

Official Roman Catholic theology, in contrast, defines justification as a process rather than a one-time event. It teaches that justification is not only a declaration of righteousness but also includes sanctification, which is an ongoing transformation in the life of the believer. While faith is seen as necessary for justification, Catholicism asserts that works of satisfaction and merit, such as those achieved through the sacraments (especially baptism and penance), contribute to a person's justification.

The Catholic understanding of justification involves a system of "congruous merit," wherein works done in cooperation with God's grace are deemed fitting for God to reward with further grace. In this view, believers do not have absolute assurance of salvation and must continually seek to earn God's grace through participation in the sacramental system of the Church. This teaching stands in stark contrast to Paul's gospel of grace alone through faith alone, which assures believers that salvation is entirely the work of Christ and cannot be earned or merited by human effort.

Conclusion: Justification by Grace Alone

In summary, the doctrine of justification affirms that salvation is a gift from God, granted through faith in Jesus Christ. This justification is based entirely on Christ's righteousness and is received by faith alone. It is not a process or something that can be earned through works, but rather a declaration by God that the believer is in right standing with Him. This doctrine is the foundation of the Christian hope, ensuring that believers are eternally secure in their relationship with God, all because of Christ's finished work on their behalf. The contrasting view of justification, as taught by the Roman Catholic Church, fundamentally changes the nature of the gospel and introduces uncertainty into the believer's relationship with God. For Paul and for the Reformation, the gospel is clear: salvation is by grace through faith alone.
