Working for God’s Favor Forfeits It

The Distortion of Obedience in Legalism

The New Testament presents Christian obedience as the practice of good deeds, which are to be done according to the right standard, with the right motive, and for the right purpose. Christians are called to be "rich in good deeds" (1 Tim. 6:18), as this reflects the regenerated heart that loves God and others and seeks to honor Him through acts of service. However, legalism distorts obedience by making good works into a means of earning God’s favor, turning what should be an expression of gratitude and love into a way to gain or maintain God's approval.

Legalism has several faults: first, it skews the motives behind good works, making them about earning favor rather than responding to God's grace. Second, it fosters arrogance, as those who believe their works merit God’s favor often look down on others who do not perform the same actions. Third, legalism breeds lovelessness, as the self-centered nature of working for God’s approval can squeeze out genuine compassion and humility.

The Pharisees and Judaizers: Examples of Legalism

The New Testament presents two main forms of legalism: Pharisaic and Judaizing. The Pharisees believed that their strict adherence to the law and their status as children of Abraham earned them God’s favor. They focused on the outward performance of rituals and commandments, even down to the most minute details, while neglecting the deeper aspects of the law such as justice, mercy, and faithfulness (Matt. 23:23–24). They were outwardly righteous but inwardly full of hypocrisy, seeking the approval of men rather than God (Matt. 6:1–8; 23:2–7).

The Judaizers, in contrast, taught that Gentile converts to Christianity must also follow Jewish customs, such as circumcision and observing the Jewish festivals, in order to be fully accepted by God. They propagated a "Christ-plus" message, suggesting that Christ’s work was insufficient for salvation and that additional works were necessary for completeness (Gal. 3:1–3; Col. 2:8–23). Paul vehemently opposed this idea, arguing that it undermined the sufficiency of Christ’s sacrifice (Gal. 5:2–6).

The Faults of Legalism

The Pharisees’ legalism focused on the externals of religious observance while disregarding the heart's intent and the true purpose of God’s law. They treated man-made traditions as binding and elevated them to the same level as God’s commandments (Mark 7:1–8). Jesus rebuked them for this, pointing out their hypocrisy and calling them to a deeper righteousness that came from the heart, not just outward appearances (Matt. 15:3–9; 23:16–24).

Legalism, whether Pharisaic or Judaizing, focuses on external obedience and human performance rather than the heart's transformation through grace. It turns religion into a system of works righteousness, where God’s approval is based on human effort rather than Christ’s completed work. Paul’s letters, particularly in Galatians and Colossians, emphasize that salvation is through Christ alone, and that any attempt to add works to the gospel diminishes the grace of God and insults the sufficiency of Christ’s sacrifice.

The Dangers of Legalism

Far from enriching a believer’s relationship with God, legalism destroys it. It shifts the focus away from Christ and the gospel, directing it instead towards human effort and performance. This fosters pride and self-reliance, and ultimately leads to spiritual stagnation, as the soul is starved by a lack of true reliance on God’s grace. Legalistic religion may look pious on the surface, but it is devoid of the love and humility that should characterize a true follower of Christ.

Legalism also leads to the danger of spiritual burnout. Constantly striving to earn God's favor through self-effort and rule-keeping can be exhausting and ultimately fruitless. It is a path that leads to despair rather than joy, as the legalist finds themselves constantly falling short of the standard they have set for themselves.

Conclusion: Avoiding Legalism

The New Testament calls believers to avoid legalism and to instead live by grace. Salvation is a gift from God, not something that can be earned by human effort (Eph. 2:8–9). Good works are the fruit of salvation, not its cause, and they should flow from a heart of gratitude for the grace of God (Titus 3:8). Believers are called to walk in the freedom of Christ, knowing that they are accepted by God because of what Christ has done, not because of what they have done. Legalism, in all its forms, must be rejected because it perverts the gospel and prevents believers from enjoying the full richness of God’s grace.
