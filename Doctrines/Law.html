God Legislates, and Demands Obedience

The Nature of God’s Law

Deuteronomy 5:1 captures the foundational call to obedience: "Moses summoned all Israel and said: Hear, O Israel, the decrees and laws I declare in your hearing today. Learn them and be sure to follow them." Humanity was not created autonomous—free to define its own laws—but theonomous, bound to follow the laws of its Creator. This obedience was intended to be a source of joy and fulfillment, as duty and delight would naturally align. Jesus exemplified this in saying, "My food is to do the will of him who sent me" (John 4:34).

However, fallen humanity resists God's law, rejecting both its authority and its divine origin. Conversely, those transformed by Christ find themselves drawn to the law out of gratitude for grace (Rom. 7:18–22). By the Holy Spirit's work, believers experience a heart-level obedience previously unattainable (Rom. 7:6; 8:4–6; Heb. 10:16).

The Scope of God’s Moral Law

God’s moral law, as revealed throughout Scripture, reflects His holy character and purposes for humanity. It is expressed in the Ten Commandments, other Mosaic laws, prophetic exhortations, the teachings of Jesus, and apostolic letters. The law outlines the behavior that pleases God and condemns that which grieves Him.

Jesus distilled the essence of the moral law into two great commandments: love for God and love for neighbor (Matt. 22:37–40). All moral instruction in Scripture hinges on these principles. Christ and His apostles extended the law's application, addressing life in the kingdom of God and the Spirit-filled post-Pentecost church. In these contexts, believers are called to embody heaven’s values and serve as a countercultural witness in the world.

Varieties of Biblical Law

Biblical law encompasses moral, political, and ceremonial categories. Moral laws prescribe personal and communal behaviors that remain universally binding. Political laws applied moral principles to Israel's unique status as a covenant nation. Ceremonial laws governed rituals, sacrifices, and purity regulations, serving as temporary symbols fulfilled in Christ (Matt. 15:20; Mark 7:15–19; Heb. 10:1–14).

The juxtaposition of these laws in the Mosaic code reflects the all-encompassing nature of life under God’s authority. Though ceremonial and political laws were limited in application, the moral law retains its universal and eternal relevance. Jesus affirmed the enduring validity of God’s moral law, declaring that He came not to abolish but to fulfill it (Matt. 5:17–19).

The Demands of God’s Law

God’s law demands total obedience from every aspect of human nature. It governs the entire person—mind, will, affections, and actions. True obedience requires not only external conformity but also internal righteousness. Jesus condemned superficial obedience, emphasizing that genuine worship and moral integrity must flow from the heart (Matt. 15:7–8; 23:25–28).

The Westminster Larger Catechism encapsulates the law’s comprehensive demands: where a duty is commanded, the opposite sin is forbidden, and where a sin is forbidden, the opposite duty is commanded. This principle underscores the law’s call for active and intentional righteousness in every aspect of life.

The Fulfillment of the Law in Christ

Jesus perfectly fulfilled God’s law through His sinless life and sacrificial death. In Him, the law’s demands are met, and its penalties are satisfied. Believers, united with Christ by faith, are no longer under the law’s condemnation but are empowered to live in obedience through the Holy Spirit. This obedience is not a means of earning salvation but an expression of gratitude and love for the God who saves.

The law remains a guide for believers, showing them how to live in a manner that reflects God’s holiness. It functions as both a mirror, revealing sin, and a lamp, illuminating the path of righteousness. In this way, the law continues to serve God’s redemptive purposes in the lives of His people.
