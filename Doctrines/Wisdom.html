God’s Twofold Will Is One

The Nature of Divine Wisdom

Wisdom, as described in Scripture, is the ability to choose the highest and most noble end and to employ the best means to achieve it. Human wisdom, explored in the Old Testament Wisdom books (Job, Psalms, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and Song of Songs) and the New Testament epistle of James, centers on living in reverence of God. Proverbs 1:7 captures this essence: “The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge.” Human wisdom is expressed through practical virtues such as prudence, fortitude, forbearance, and zeal, all of which are directed toward a life of worship and service to God.

Divine wisdom, however, far surpasses human understanding. It is evident in God’s creation, providence, and redemption. Psalm 104:24 marvels, “How many are your works, Lord! In wisdom you made them all.” God’s choice of His glory as His ultimate goal, achieved through the intricate workings of creation and redemption, reveals His unparalleled wisdom. This is most vividly displayed in the redemptive “wisdom” of Christ crucified, which defies human expectations (1 Cor. 1:18–2:16), and in the global church that results from His work (Eph. 3:10).

The Twofold Will of God

God’s wisdom is manifested in the outworking of His will, which is expressed in two interrelated senses: His will of events and His will of precept. The will of events refers to God’s eternal decree, His sovereign purpose by which “for his own glory, he hath foreordained whatsoever comes to pass” (Westminster Shorter Catechism, Q.7). This is the will by which God orchestrates all of history, ensuring that every event aligns with His divine purpose (Eph. 1:11).

The will of precept, on the other hand, represents God’s commands and instructions for how people should live. These commands, revealed in Scripture, include moral principles rooted in God’s holy character, such as the Ten Commandments and the two great commandments to love God and neighbor (Exod. 20:1–17; Matt. 22:37–40). They also encompass covenantal practices, such as circumcision and sacrifices in the Old Testament, and baptism and the Lord’s Supper in the New Testament. Together, these commands form a comprehensive guide for righteous living, aligning believers with God’s purposes (Eph. 2:10).

Reconciling Obedience and God’s Sovereignty

The interplay between costly obedience and God’s sovereign plan can be challenging to comprehend. Scripture acknowledges that obedience often brings worldly disadvantage, yet it is an integral part of God’s predestined plan for His glory and the believer’s ultimate good (Rom. 8:28). Paul exhorts believers to trust that even painful acts of obedience are woven into the tapestry of God’s redemptive purpose. This trust glorifies God and affirms His wisdom, even when the full picture remains unseen.

God’s will of precept, while requiring human obedience, is also instrumental in fulfilling His will of events. Even acts of unbelief and disobedience fall within God’s sovereign plan, as illustrated by Israel’s rejection of the gospel. Paul reflects on this in Romans 11:11–32, recognizing that Israel’s unbelief serves to advance the gospel among the Gentiles. This realization leads him to exclaim, “Oh, the depth of the riches of the wisdom and knowledge of God!” (Rom. 11:33). Such mysteries of divine wisdom inspire worship and trust in God’s ultimate plan.

God’s Wisdom in Redemption

Nowhere is God’s wisdom more clearly displayed than in His plan of redemption. The cross of Christ, described by Paul as “foolishness to those who are perishing” but “the power of God” to believers (1 Cor. 1:18), exemplifies God’s ability to bring glory out of apparent defeat. Through Christ’s sacrificial death and resurrection, God reconciles sinners to Himself, demonstrating both justice and mercy.

The global church, composed of people from every nation and culture, further showcases the multifaceted wisdom of God. Ephesians 3:10 declares that through the church, God’s wisdom is made known to the powers and authorities in the heavenly realms. This redemptive wisdom transcends human understanding and reflects the inexhaustible richness of God’s plan.

Living in Light of God’s Wisdom

God’s wisdom invites believers to respond with trust, obedience, and worship. Trusting in His wisdom means believing that all things, even hardships and suffering, are part of His sovereign plan for good. This trust sustains believers through life’s uncertainties, anchoring them in the assurance of God’s unchanging purpose.

Obedience to God’s will of precept is another way to honor His wisdom. By aligning their lives with His commands, believers participate in His redemptive work and bear witness to His glory. This obedience, though sometimes costly, reflects the believer’s faith in God’s ultimate plan.

Finally, the wisdom of God calls for worship. Like Paul in Romans 11:33–36, believers are moved to exclaim, “To him be the glory forever!” The contemplation of God’s wisdom leads to awe and adoration, as His plans, though often beyond human comprehension, reveal His perfect character and unsearchable ways.
