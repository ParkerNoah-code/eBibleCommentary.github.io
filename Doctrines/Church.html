God Plants His People in a New Community

The Church: A New Testament Reality

The church, referred to in Greek as "ecclesia," meaning "assembly," exists in, through, and because of Jesus Christ. It is a distinctive reality in the New Testament, marking a new phase in God’s redemptive history. While the church is a new community, it is also a continuation of God's covenant people from the Old Testament. The differences between the church and Israel are rooted in the new covenant that God established through Christ. This new covenant reflects a change in how God binds His people to Himself, ensuring that through Jesus, believers are now part of the community that He calls His own (1 Cor. 11:25; Heb. 8:7-13).

The central theme of the new covenant is summarized in God’s declaration: “I will be your God, and you shall be my people” (Exod. 6:7; Jer. 31:33). This promise is now realized in the church, which includes believers from every nation, breaking down the ethnic barriers that existed under the old covenant (Eph. 2–3). The church’s new identity is shaped by Christ’s mediation—His death, resurrection, and reigning as the crucified God-man. As a result, the church is no longer confined to a particular ethnic group but encompasses all those who are in Christ.

The New Covenant and Its Features

There are several key features of the new covenant that distinguish it from the old. First, the Old Testament priests, sacrifices, and sanctuary are replaced by the mediation of Jesus Christ, who now serves as the ultimate High Priest, offering Himself as the perfect sacrifice for sin (Heb. 1–10). Through Christ, believers find their identity as the seed of Abraham and the people of God, regardless of their ethnic background (Gal. 3:29; 1 Pet. 2:4–10).

Second, the old covenant's ethnic exclusivity is replaced by the universal invitation of the gospel. Under the new covenant, believers from all nations are welcomed into the community of God’s people, sharing equally in the blessings of salvation (Eph. 2–3; Rev. 5:9–10). This inclusivity reflects the global mission of the church to make disciples of all nations (Matt. 28:19–20).

Third, the Holy Spirit is poured out on both individual Christians and the church as a whole. Through the Spirit, believers experience fellowship with Christ, receive ministry from Him, and experience the foretaste of heaven (1 John 1:3; John 12:32; 14:18; 2 Cor. 1:22; Eph. 1:14). The Spirit's presence in the church is a mark of the new covenant and a guarantee of the church’s ultimate victory in Christ.

The Church’s Universal and Local Expressions

The church, while universal in its scope, also manifests in local congregations. The universal church is the body of Christ, the community of believers worldwide, who are united by faith in Him. Each local congregation is a microcosm, a small-scale representation of the church as a whole. Paul describes the church as the body of Christ (1 Cor. 12:12-26; Eph. 1:22-23), where each local congregation reflects this unity and the fullness of the faith in Christ.

The local church is called to fulfill the role of being a representative sample of the universal church, embodying its values and mission. Though the universal church is one, it is expressed in many different forms, denominations, and local bodies, yet all are united in Christ. The church is also characterized as "one," "holy," "catholic" (universal), and "apostolic"—one because it is united in Christ, holy because it is consecrated to God, catholic because it extends to all nations, and apostolic because it is built on the teaching of the apostles (Eph. 2:19-22).

The Visible and Invisible Church

There is a distinction between the "visible" and "invisible" church. The visible church consists of the organized community of believers—those who gather for worship, fellowship, and service. However, the invisible church refers to those who are truly regenerated and belong to Christ, though not everyone who appears to be part of the visible church is truly saved. The New Testament acknowledges that some may profess faith and even hold leadership positions, but may not be spiritually regenerated (Matt. 7:15-27; 13:24-30, 36-43). This distinction reminds believers that God alone knows the hearts of His people (2 Tim. 2:19), and the true church is known only to Him.

Participation in the Life of the Church

The New Testament assumes that all Christians will participate in the life of a local church. This includes gathering for worship, receiving teaching and discipline, and sharing in the mission of the church (Heb. 10:25; Matt. 18:15-20; Gal. 6:1). Failing to join with a local congregation when one is available can lead to spiritual impoverishment. Christians are called to engage with the body of Christ, receiving its nurture and contributing to its work.

Corporate Worship in the New Covenant

Christian worship, as outlined in the New Testament, does not prescribe the level of detail found in the Old Testament, but it does emphasize key elements: praise, prayer, preaching, and the regular observance of the Lord’s Supper (Acts 20:7-11; Eph. 5:19). Worship in the church is marked by singing psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, as well as engaging in heartfelt prayer and preaching of the Word. The church that is spiritually alive will prioritize these elements of worship as a reflection of the church's vitality and devotion to God.

Singing to God has always been a prominent feature of Christian worship, from the apostolic church (Acts 16:25) to the worship in Revelation, where new songs are sung to God's praise (Rev. 4:8; 5:9–10; 7:10). A spiritually vibrant church takes these acts of worship seriously, ensuring that singing, praying, and preaching are central to its corporate life.

Conclusion

The church is a new community formed by the new covenant, established in Jesus Christ, and empowered by the Holy Spirit. While it has a universal dimension, it also exists in local congregations, each called to represent the larger body of Christ. The church is both visible and invisible, and it is essential for Christians to engage with the life of a local church. Worship in the church is centered on the praise of God, prayer, preaching, and the sacraments, all of which reflect the life of the new covenant community.
