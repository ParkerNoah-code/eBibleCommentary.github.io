The Biblical Foundation of the Lord’s Supper

In 1 Corinthians 11:23–26, Paul recounts the institution of the Lord's Supper: "For I received from the Lord what I also passed on to you: The Lord Jesus, on the night he was betrayed, took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it and said, 'This is my body, which is for you; do this in remembrance of me.' In the same way, after supper he took the cup, saying, 'This cup is the new covenant in my blood; do this, whenever you drink it, in remembrance of me.' For whenever you eat this bread and drink this cup, you proclaim the Lord’s death until he comes." The Lord’s Supper is a sacrament, instituted by Christ, that serves as both a memorial of His death and a means of communion with Him. It is a communal meal shared by believers that embodies the new covenant relationship made possible by His sacrifice.

The Lord’s Supper is not merely a memorial but also a spiritual nourishment and a means of grace. The Westminster Confession (XXIX.1) explains that the Supper was instituted by Christ "for the perpetual remembrance of the sacrifice of himself in his death; the sealing of all benefits thereof unto true believers, their spiritual nourishment and growth in him." It is an act that encourages believers in their faith, deepening their relationship with Christ and with one another as members of His body.

The Significance of the Lord’s Supper

The theological significance of the Lord’s Supper is multi-dimensional. It is a ritual that reflects three key aspects: past, present, and future. First, it points to the past, reminding us of Christ’s sacrificial death on the cross, which brings forgiveness and reconciliation with God. Second, it addresses the present, as believers partake by faith, drawing spiritual sustenance from Christ. It is a moment of personal and communal reflection, reaffirming the bond between believers and Christ, and among believers themselves. Third, it has a future dimension, as it looks forward to Christ’s return, encouraging believers with the hope of His second coming and the fulfillment of God's promises.

This sacrament, though grounded in historical events, is not simply a remembrance of what happened but a real participation in Christ’s body and blood. It serves as a reminder of the grace given through His death, and as an invitation to commune with Him in a spiritual way, even as we physically partake of the bread and wine.

The Presence of Christ in the Supper

The nature of Christ’s presence in the Supper has been the subject of considerable debate, especially since the Reformation. The Roman Catholic Church teaches transubstantiation, which holds that the bread and wine are miraculously transformed into the actual body and blood of Christ, though they retain the appearance of bread and wine. Martin Luther proposed a doctrine known as "consubstantiation," suggesting that the body and blood of Christ are truly present in, with, and under the bread and wine, though the elements themselves remain unchanged. In contrast, Ulrich Zwingli argued that the Supper is a memorial meal, with no physical presence of Christ in the elements. John Calvin offered a middle ground, suggesting that while the bread and wine remain unchanged, Christ is spiritually present in the Supper, drawing believers into communion with Him through the Holy Spirit.

Despite these differing views, all the Reformers agreed on one essential point: the Supper does not repeat or renew the sacrifice of Christ. It is a memorial, an act of thanksgiving for His finished work on the cross. The Supper does not reoffer Christ’s sacrifice but celebrates the completed and sufficient work of His atonement.

Ritual and Meaning in the Supper

The Lord’s Supper is more than a ritual; it carries deep spiritual meaning for those who participate. The first level of meaning is its reference to Christ’s death, which is the central event of the Christian faith. The second level pertains to the present, as believers "feed on Christ by faith" during the Supper, reflecting on their spiritual unity with Him. This implies not only a personal reflection but also a communal reflection on the body of Christ as believers share in the meal together. Finally, the Supper has a future reference, pointing believers to the anticipated return of Christ, when the full realization of God's promises will be realized.

Paul advises self-examination before partaking of the Supper (1 Cor. 11:28), ensuring that participants approach the table in a manner worthy of the Lord. This moment of introspection ensures that believers are partaking of the Supper with a proper attitude, both in relation to God and to one another.

The Lord’s Supper and Christian Community

The Lord’s Supper is a profound expression of the unity believers share in Christ. As the body of Christ, the church is called to live in fellowship, love, and mutual care. By participating in this sacrament, believers affirm their shared faith and their commitment to each other as members of Christ’s mystical body. The Supper is a celebration of the new covenant, a covenant of grace, which has been sealed by Christ’s blood. It is a bond of communion with Him and with one another, strengthening the body of believers as they look forward to Christ’s return.
