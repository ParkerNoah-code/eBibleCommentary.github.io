Calvinism

The terms "Reformed" and "Calvinist" are often used interchangeably. Calvinism derives its name from John Calvin, a 16th-century French theologian and reformer, though it draws heavily from earlier theological figures like Augustine. While Calvin's influence is undeniable, it's crucial to recognize that Calvinism seeks to be biblical first and foremost.

John Calvin, Institutes of the Christian Religion, 1536.

Ryan Reeves, Calvin and Calvinism, YouTube.

Calvinism is specifically known for its emphasis on the sovereignty of God, predestination, and the five points of doctrine encapsulated in TULIP: Total Depravity, Unconditional Election, Limited Atonement, Irresistible Grace, and Perseverance of the Saints. These five points were a response to the five doctrinal challenges raised by Jacobus Arminius, and serve as a hepful way to distinguish Calvinism from Arminianism. However, the Reformed faith as a whole also focuses on other key principles such as the authority of Scripture, the priesthood of all believers, and justification by faith alone.

History

Calvinism's roots can be traced back to the early church, especially to Augustine of Hippo, whose writings on grace, predestination, and original sin laid the groundwork for many Calvinist doctrines. The Protestant Reformation of the 16th century, a movement against certain practices of the Roman Catholic Church, provided the context for the development of Calvinism. Reformers like Martin Luther significantly influenced Calvin, who systematized and expanded reformed ideas.

Ryan Reeves, Luther and Calvin, YouTube.

Calvinism has deeply influenced many traditional Protestant churches. It played a significant role in the formation of the Presbyterian, Reformed, and Congregationalist churches, among others.

TULIP

Total Depravity

This doctrine may more accurately be termed "Radical Depravity." The word "total" in this context does not mean humans are as evil as possible, but rather that sin affects every aspect of a person's being. Because of the Fall, every part of human nature has been corrupted and enslaved by sin.

As a result, humans in their natural state are morally and spiritually unable to follow God. This inability is not due to a lack of physical or intellectual capability, but a deep-seated corruption of the human will and desires. People are enslaved to sin and cannot convert themselves. This underscores the need for divine intervention for salvation, leading to the doctrines of Unconditional Election and Irresistible Grace, where God elects individuals for salvation and effectively draws them to Himself.

Total Depravity is often misunderstood to mean that people are incapable of any good whatsoever. However, Calvinists assert that unregenerate (non-Christian) people can still perform actions that are good in a societal or humanitarian sense, but these actions cannot please God or meet the standards of true spiritual righteousness.
