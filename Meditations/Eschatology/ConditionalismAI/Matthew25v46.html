The Greek text for Matthew 25:46 is:

"καὶ ἀπελεύσονται οὗτοι εἰς κόλασιν αἰώνιον, οἱ δὲ δίκαιοι εἰς ζωὴν αἰώνιον."

A literal translation of this verse is:

"And these will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life."

Here is a detailed explanation of the Greek words:

καὶ (kai): This is a common conjunction in Greek, typically translated as "and."

ἀπελεύσονται (apeleusontai): This word is a future tense form of the verb ἀπέρχομαι (aperchomai), meaning "to go away" or "to depart."

οὗτοι (houtoi): This is a demonstrative pronoun, meaning "these."

εἰς (eis): A preposition often translated as "into."

κόλασιν (kolasin): This noun means "punishment." In ancient Greek, it could refer to the pruning of trees, but in this context, it is used metaphorically to mean punishment.

αἰώνιον (aiōnion): An adjective meaning "eternal" or "everlasting." It is derived from αἰών (aiōn), which means "age" or "long period of time."

οἱ δὲ (hoi de): "οἱ" is a definite article, "the," and "δὲ" is a conjunction often translated as "but" in this context, indicating a contrast.

δίκαιοι (dikaiοi): This is the plural form of the adjective δίκαιος (dikaios), meaning "righteous" or "just."

ζωὴν (zōēn): This noun means "life." It is often used in philosophical and religious contexts to mean not just physical life, but a fuller, more meaningful existence.

αἰώνιον (aiōnion): As above, this adjective means "eternal" or "everlasting."

This literal translation and explanation focus solely on the language and grammatical structure of the verse, without venturing into theological interpretation.

In the New Testament, particularly in the Gospels, the term "Gehenna" is used by Jesus as a metaphor for hell, a place of punishment in the afterlife. The word "Gehenna" has its roots in the Old Testament and Jewish tradition, which provides important context for understanding its use in the Book of Matthew.

Old Testament Background of Gehenna:

The term "Gehenna" originates from the Hebrew phrase "Ge Hinnom," which means "Valley of Hinnom." This valley lies just outside Jerusalem and had a notorious history in the Old Testament.

In the Old Testament times, the Valley of Hinnom was associated with the worship of pagan gods, particularly the Canaanite god Molech. This worship included the abominable practice of child sacrifice, as mentioned in 2 Chronicles 28:3 and 2 Kings 23:10.

Due to its association with such detestable practices, the Valley of Hinnom came to be seen as a place of evil and curse. By the time of Jeremiah, it was referred to as the Valley of Slaughter (Jeremiah 7:31-32; 19:2-6), prophesying that it would be a place where the bodies of the dead would be thrown.

Jewish Tradition and Gehenna:

Between the Old and New Testament periods, Jewish thought further developed the concept of Gehenna. It became not just a symbol of disgrace and defilement but also a metaphor for divine judgment and the fate awaiting the wicked.

In Jewish apocalyptic literature, Gehenna evolved into a place of fiery punishment for the wicked after death, contrasting with the destiny of the righteous.

Gehenna in the Context of Matthew's Gospel:

When Jesus refers to Gehenna in Matthew (e.g., Matthew 5:22, 29-30; 10:28; 23:33), he is drawing on this rich background of Gehenna as a symbol of judgment and divine retribution.

Jesus' use of Gehenna would have been understood by his audience within this cultural and religious context as a stern warning about the consequences of sin and the reality of judgment.

In Matthew's Gospel, the references to Gehenna are often in the context of ethical teachings, where Jesus emphasizes the severity of sin and the need for repentance.

Understanding Gehenna in its historical and cultural context helps interpret Jesus' teachings in Matthew more accurately. It underscores the seriousness with which Jesus approached topics of sin, judgment, and the necessity of righteous living in view of the coming kingdom of God.

Nouns of action that are qualified as eternal (such as punishment) can either indicate that the action continues forever or that the result of the action continues forever.

ChatGPT

You're correct in noting that in Greek, especially in the context of Koine Greek (the dialect of Greek used in the New Testament), nouns of action that are modified by the adjective "eternal" (αἰώνιος, aionios) can be interpreted in two ways:

Continuation of the Action: In this interpretation, αἰώνιος signifies that the action itself is ongoing and unending. Applied to Matthew 25:46, this would mean that the action of "punishment" (κόλασις, kolasis) is a continuous, never-ceasing process.

Permanence of the Result: In this alternative interpretation, αἰώνιος is understood as emphasizing the lasting, unending impact or consequences of the action. The action might occur within a limited time frame, but its effects or results are eternal. Here, the focus is not on the duration of the action itself (the act of punishing), but on the enduring nature of its outcome.

The Greek term αἰώνιος, translated as "eternal," is often at the center of these interpretations. Its meaning encompasses both the concepts of an unending duration and the age-lasting quality, i.e., pertaining to an age or era. The context in which it is used, especially in Biblical texts, is critical to understanding which aspect of 'eternity' is being emphasized - the ongoing nature of an action or the lasting impact of its result.
