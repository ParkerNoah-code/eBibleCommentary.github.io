Isaiah 66:24 in Hebrew is written as:

"וְיָצְאוּ וְרָאוּ בְּפִגְרֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים הַפֹּשְׁעִים בִּי כִּי תוֹלַעְתָּם לֹא תָמוּת וְאִשָּׁם לֹא תִכְבֶּה וְהָיוּ דֵרָאוֹן לְכָל בָּשָׂר׃"

Translated into English, this verse reads:

"And they shall go forth, and look upon the carcasses of the men that have transgressed against me: for their worm shall not die, neither shall their fire be quenched; and they shall be an abhorring unto all flesh."

Definitions of key terms:

וְיָצְאוּ (veyatz'eu): "and they shall go forth" - This is a verb in future tense, indicating an action that will happen.

וְרָאוּ (vera'u): "and look" - Another verb in future tense, meaning to see or observe.

בְּפִגְרֵי (b'figrei): "upon the carcasses" - This refers to dead bodies or remains.

הָאֲנָשִׁים (ha'anashim): "of the men" - Referring to people or men in this context.

הַפֹּשְׁעִים (haposh'im): "that have transgressed" - This term relates to those who have sinned or committed a transgression.

כִּי (ki): "for" - A conjunction often used to introduce a reason or explanation.

תוֹלַעְתָּם (tole'atam): "their worm" - This could be interpreted literally as a worm, but often carries a metaphorical meaning in biblical texts.

לֹא תָמוּת (lo tamut): "shall not die" - This is a negation indicating that something will not happen, specifically death in this context.

וְאִשָּׁם (ve'isham): "neither shall their fire" - Referring to a fire associated with them.

לֹא תִכְבֶּה (lo tichbeh): "shall not be quenched" - Indicating that the fire will not be extinguished.

וְהָיוּ (ve'haiyu): "and they shall be" - Another future tense verb.

דֵרָאוֹן (de'raon): "an abhorring" - This term implies something that causes disgust or abhorrence.

לְכָל בָּשָׂר (le'chol basar): "unto all flesh" - Meaning to all people or all living beings.

In the Old Testament, the concept of an "unquenchable fire" is often used metaphorically to represent the certainty and thoroughness of divine judgment or wrath. This imagery is employed in various contexts to convey the idea that the fire of God's judgment, once kindled, cannot be extinguished by human means. It signifies the inevitable and complete fulfillment of God's pronouncements against sin or disobedience. Here's a brief overview of how unquenchable fire is used in different Old Testament verses:

Jeremiah 7:20 - In this verse, the unquenchable fire is a symbol of God's anger and wrath poured out on Jerusalem and Judah because of their idolatry and disobedience. It signifies the inescapable nature of divine judgment.

Jeremiah 17:27 - Here, the unquenchable fire is a warning against desecrating the Sabbath. It represents the severe consequences of disobedience to God's commandments, emphasizing that the resulting destruction cannot be averted.

Ezekiel 20:47-48 - The unquenchable fire in this passage is a metaphor for God's judgment on the southern kingdom (Judah). It emphasizes the completeness of the devastation that will occur, affecting both the righteous and the wicked.

Amos 5:6 - In this context, the unquenchable fire represents the impending judgment on the northern kingdom (Israel) for their injustice and idolatry. It serves as a warning that turning away from God leads to inescapable consequences.

In each of these instances, the imagery of an unquenchable fire serves to underscore the seriousness of sin and the certainty of divine judgment. It's used to convey the message that once God's judgment is decreed, it is sure and final. The metaphorical use of fire, a common element in ancient Near Eastern literature, was well understood in the cultural context of the time as a symbol of purification, destruction, and divine wrath.
