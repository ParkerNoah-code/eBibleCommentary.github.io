Conditionalism

Conditionalism, also known as Annihilationism, proposes that after the resurrection and judgment of all people, the unsaved will face a second, irreversible death, ceasing conscious existence forever. Only those who are saved through faith in Christ will receive the gift of immortality and eternal life.

The traditional belief in the eternal conscious torment of the unsaved, whether right or wrong, acts as a doctrinal grid that affects our interpretation of biblical texts. This grid forces texts about "death," "destruction," and "perishing" to be interpreted metaphorically to refer to seperation from God, pain, or torment even though annihilation, extinction, or cessation of consciousness would appear be the obvious interpretation of these passages otherwise.

Therefore, we must be careful not to read our presuppositions into the text. A thorough examination of passages about hell will reveal if eternal torment is taught in Scripture. If it is not taught, the belief that the wicked will face a final death after the judgment follows naturally from the typical biblical language about hell.

Method

I believe in the inerrancy of the Bible. It is both authoritative and accurate. It is the standard of truth over and against all human traditions, philosophies, creeds, councils, and authorities. It is my goal to understand the biblical texts about hell in context and to leave behind presuppositions or philosophical arguments that don't come from the Bible. I don't have a set position for or against Annihilationism, I'm still studying the issue, but I do see it as a plausible view worth considering seriously after reexamining some of the biblical texts which appeared to me to teach the traditional view until I examined them critically. I will present positive arguments for Annihilationism first and then challenge or defend them as I study and revise the article.

Motivation

What would cause someone to question a belief that seems so certain? One only needs to consider the time before the Reformation where the majority of the visible church was certain that justification was by faith and works. It was going back to the Word of God afresh that revealed that doctrinal error. So it is certainly possible for many to be mistaken about what the Bible teaches, but what in Scripture would cause someone to question eternal torment? Well, just laying out common biblical language about the final judgment of the wicked sounds like Annihilationism:

The Bible clearly states that the righteous will be resurrected and given immortality and incorruptibility. In contrast, there is no suggestion in the Scriptures that the wicked will be granted either immortality or incorruptibility. They will not recieve eternal life but perish. They will be excluded from God's presence and cast into the lake of fire, where they will undergo the second death.

This eternal punishment, the second death, represents the ultimate consequence of sin. It entails eternal and complete destruction, enacted by God who has the power to annihilate both soul and body in hell. This form of punishment means absolute and irreversible perishing, in stark contrast to eternal life, which is consistently presented in the New Testament as the desirable alternative to death, destruction, and perishing.

Glenn Peoples, Why I am an Annihilationist.

Translation of Hell

The King James Version translated the words Sheol, Hades, Gehenna, and Tartarus as hell. This is inaccurate and confusing in modern english. The word hell has developed to refer to the place of eternal torment; however, it originally only meant a covered or hidden place (think of the word "helmet"). There are still many modern translations which do this to a certain degree.

Gehenna, the shortended name for the valley of Hinnom, became notorious during the reigns of Kings Ahaz and Manasseh, who committed abominable acts there, including the sacrifice of their own children by fire to the god Molech (2 Chronicles 28:3; 33:6). The valley, we are told in Jeremiah 7:29-34 and 19:1-15, would be called a place of slaughter, where it was prophesied to be filled with the corpses of those who had turned away from God. This valley thus became synonymous with extreme divine punishment, where the bodies of the slain were left unburied and exposed to decay and scavengers, painting a vivid picture of desolation and wrath.

Matthew Easton, Hell, Easton's Bible Dictionary. "Derived from the Saxon helan, to cover; hence the covered or the invisible place."

Words Translated As Hell. "The word Sheol is used in the Old Testament 65 times. The King James Version translates it as grave 31 times, hell 31 times, and pit 3 times. Can you imagine the same word being translated as both grave and hell?"

Fallacies

Annihilationists deny eternal punishment: This is a strawman to be knocked down with a verse like Matthew 25:46. Both sides affirm eternal punishment. The disagreement is over the nature of that punishment. The destruction or annihilation of a person would be an irreversible and eternal punishment that forever seperated that person from God.

Conditionalists just want to believe in annihilation because the idea of people spending eternity in hell is awful, or they are just theological liberals: This is just poisoning the well when people begin with these kinds of accusations. The goal is not to psychoanalyze the other side but to examine arguments about the biblical view of hell.

Biblical references to fire and destruction are understood as metaphors for annihilation: Trying to claim that this view interprets these descriptions non-literally or metaphocially is not only false, it is used rhetorically to discount consideration of the view. Destruction and annihilation are synonyms, and things that are set on fire are consumed and destroyed. Rather, construing these descriptions to mean eternal conscious torment, whether or not that is justified, is clearly the metaphorical interpretation of these passages.

Conditionalists argue that their view is more consistent with God's character of love and justice: This is a red herring. The focus should remain exegetical first and foremost. There is certainly room to discuss the justness of it, but this should not be treated as the primary argument or motivation behind this view. Moreover, death, not eternal torment, is consistently said to be the price of sin, and Jesus' death is what saved us at Calvary.

Glenn Peoples, Fallacies in the Annihilationist Debate: A Critique of Robert Peterson and Other Traditionalist Scholarship, JETS 50/2 (June 2007), 329-47.

Correcting Assumptions

The soul is not inherently immortal: Everything that is not God was created by God and depends on Him at every moment for its sustained existence. The Bible never states that the soul will necessarily live on forever. God "can destroy both soul and body in hell" (Mat. 10:28).

Everyone might not spend eternity somewhere: The idea that everyone will go to either heaven or hell for eternity is an assumption that must be examined biblically. 1 Corinthians 15 is sometimes used to argue that all people will be raised with immortality and incorruptibility, and it is almost the only text used to argue this position; however, in context, this chapter is about those who are in Christ being raised and says nothing of unbelievers. Verses like John 3:16 indicate the opposite, that unbelievers are destined to perish.

Mark 9:47-48 // Isaiah 66:24

"...cast into the Gehenna of fire, 'where their worm doesn’t die, and the fire is not quenched.'"

Gehenna became notorious during the reigns of Kings Ahaz and Manasseh, who committed abominable acts there, including the sacrifice of their own children by fire to the god Molech (2 Chronicles 28:3; 33:6). The valley, we are told in Jeremiah 7:29-34 and 19:1-15, would be called a place of slaughter, where it was prophesied to be filled with the corpses of those who had turned away from God. This valley thus became synonymous with extreme divine punishment, where the bodies of the slain were left unburied and exposed to decay and scavengers, painting a vivid picture of desolation and wrath.

Gehenna in the New Testament represents not just physical destruction, but the culmination of divine judgment, where the opportunity for salvation is irrevocably withdrawn, and the wicked face complete destruction by God. Jesus uses Gehenna eschatologically by using strong language about the body and its parts to illustrate the seriousness of avoiding sin and its consequences (Matthew 5:22, 29-30, 18:9). This imagery resonates with the imagery in Isaiah 66:24, reinforcing the notion of Gehenna as a place of total annihilation for the wicked. This is consistent with Paul who speaks of the ultimate fate of the wicked in terms of destruction (Philippians 1:28; 3:18-19; Romans 9:22-24; 2 Thessalonians 1:8-9).

Jesus quotes Isaiah 66:24, where it clearly states that corpses are being consumed by fire and maggots, not living beings. The imagery of the worm and fire in Isaiah is not about eternal conscious torment but emphasizes the shame and complete destruction of the wicked.

The original Hebrew phrase "does not die" doesn't mean "will never die" in other biblical contexts (e.g., Genesis 42:20, Exodus 30:20, Jeremiah 38:24). It often means "will not die at a particular time or in a particular context." In Isaiah 66:24, it means that the worms will not die before completely devouring the corpses.

Similarly, a fire that "is not quenched" does not imply a fire that burns forever. "Quench" primarily means "extinguish." The fire in Isaiah cannot be extinguished until it fully consumes its fuel. This imagery emphasizes the complete and irreversible nature of the consumption.

Joseph Dear, Introduction to Evangelical Condiationalism – Mark 9:48, (2018).

Revelation

Revelation 14:10-11

"...he also will drink of the wine of the wrath of God, which is prepared unmixed in the cup of his anger. He will be tormented with fire and sulfur in the presence of the holy angels and in the presence of the Lamb. The smoke of their torment goes up forever and ever. They have no rest day and night, those who worship the beast and his image, and whoever receives the mark of his name."

It is important to note that the book of Revelation is filled with symbolic language and apocalyptic imagery. Much like interpreting a parable, the goal is not to analyze the pictoral elements literally but to look at what meaning the picture is trying to convey.

The torment the wicked experience is not an endless agony, but rather an extreme encounter with God's justice at the final judgment. It is so terrifying that they are unable to rest day or night while awaiting that time. The finality and decisiveness of God's judgment is conveyed through fire and brimstone language. Similar to the fall of Babylon depicted in Revelation 18, their torment and destruction are a closed chapter in history, forever marked by the enduring symbol of rising smoke.

The image of fire and brimstone is found in references to Sodom and Gomorrah (Genesis 19:23, 28; Deuteronomy 29:23; Job 18:15-17; Isaiah 30:27-33; 34:9-11; Ezekiel 38:22). It paints a picture of decisive annihilation and complete destruction, leaving nothing but ashes in its wake. This picture appears to be in stark contrast to excruciating, unending suffering.

Recall how Abraham saw smoke rising from Sodom and Gomorrah, signifying their final annihilation (Genesis 19:28). Smoke rising forever symbolizes the permanent destruction of the wicked. This is the same fate of Edom in Isaiah 34:8-10. The city was destroyed by fire, and the smoke rising forever represents the everlasting nature of its destruction. The smoke, therefore, stands as a permanent reminder of God's final victory over evil.

Joseph Dear, A Primer on Revelation 14:9-11, (2017).
