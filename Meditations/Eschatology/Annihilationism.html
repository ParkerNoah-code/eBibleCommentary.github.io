Conditionalism

Conditionalism, also known as Annihilationism, proposes that after the resurrection and judgment of all people, the unsaved will face a second, irreversible death, ceasing conscious existence forever. Only those who are saved through faith in Christ will receive the gift of immortality and eternal life. One's view of the intermediate state is irrelevant to this issue because it is purely a view about the eternal state.

The traditional belief in eternal torment, whether right or wrong, acts as a doctrinal grid that affects our interpretation of biblical texts. This grid forces texts about "death" and "destruction" to be interpreted metaphorically even though Conditionalism would be the obvious interpretation of these passages otherwise. The doctrine of eternal torment rests on four core texts: Matthew 18:34-35, Mark 9:43-48, Revelation 14:11, and Revelation 20:10.

If Annihilationism is true, each core text used to support eternal torment should have a plausible interpretation that is compatible with Conditionalism. Other texts used to support eternal torment are less compelling and can be interpreted in a harmonious way with Conditionalism. Until these texts are proven to not teach eternal concious torment, that view is bound to be read into every passage. If it is shown not to be taught in Scripture, annihilationism follows naturally and a focus on positive arguments for the position can be examined.

Method

I believe in the inerrancy of the Bible. It is both authoritative and accurate. It is the standard of truth over and against all human traditions and authorities. It is my goal to understand the biblical texts about hell in context and to leave behind presuppositions or philosophical arguments that don't come from the text. I don't have a set position for or against Annihilationism, I'm still studying the issue, but I do see it as a plausible view worth considering seriously after re-examining some of the biblical texts, texts which did appear to me to teach the traditional view but in fact do not.

Caricatures

Annihilationists deny eternal punishment: This is a strawman to be knocked down with a verse like Matthew 25:46. Both sides affirm eternal punishment. The disagreement is over the nature of that punishment. Destruction or annihilation of a person would be an irreversible and eternal punishment that forever seperated that person from God.

Conditionalists just want to believe in annihilation because the idea of people spending eternity in hell is awful, or they are just theological liberals that won't follow the plain meaning of the text: This is just poisoning the well when people throw out these kinds of accusations. The goal is not to psychoanalyze the other side but to examine arguments about the biblical view of hell.

Biblical references to fire and destruction are understood as metaphors for annihilation: Trying to claim that this view interprets these descriptions non-literally or metaphocially is not only false, it is used rhetorically to discount consideration of the view. Destruction and annihilation are synonyms, and things that are set on fire are consumed and destroyed. Rather, construing these descriptions of hell to mean eternal concious tourment is clearly the metaphorical interpretation of these passages.

Conditionalists argue that their view is more consistent with God's character of love and justice: This is a red herring. It may or may not be more consistent with God's love and justice; however, the focus should remain exegetical first and foremost.

Assumptions

The soul is not inherently immortal: Everything that is not God was created by God and depends on Him at every moment for its sustained existence. The Bible never states that the soul will necessarily live on forever. God "can destroy both soul and body in hell" (Mat. 10:28).

That everyone will spend eternity somewhere: The idea that everyone will go to either heaven or hell for eternity is an assumption that must be examined biblically. 1 Corinthians 15 is sometimes applied to all people being raised with immortality and incorruptibility, and it is almost the only text used to argue this position. In context, this chapter is about those who are in Christ being raised and says nothing of unbelievers. Annihilationists reject the traditional interpretations of the core eternal torment texts. This would leave no support for the belief that the unsaved continue existence after the final judgement.
