Carroll Quigley, Tragedy and Hope: A History of the World In Our Time, (San Pedro, California: GSG, 1966).

Objective of the Book

"Tragedy and Hope" is a comprehensive history book covering the world from approximately 1890 to 1960. Its primary focus lies in the realm of international affairs during this period, with particular attention given to the two World Wars, their precursors, and the aftermath that followed. Quigley delves deep into the covert machinations of international financiers and influential organizations such as the Round Table, the Royal Institute of International Affairs, and the Council on Foreign Relations. This clandestine dimension of the book has captured the has attracted attention from conspiracy theorists, spanning the political spectrum, who contend that a shadowy "power elite" wields influence over domestic and international policies, undermining democratic principles.1

Quigley himself was a liberal internationalist who shared some views with the alleged conspirators, though he criticizes their secrecy.2 He highlights the existence of an elite in Western Europe and North America, composed of financiers, industrialists, high-ranking government officials, and the social upper class, who exerted significant influence over global affairs. Sometimes this elite is portrayed as operating with a mix of altruism, self-interest, and naivety. Their plans often backfired, leaving an impression of bumbling rather than sinister manipulation.

Preface

Quigley was writing about events that were very recent to his time. This usually is a problem because both source materials and perspectives are limited the more recent the events are. He tries to counteract those typical problems with a comprehensive perspective on things. Not only connecting historical patterns to the present and future but also examining events in their full context, including military, technological, social, and intellectual aspects, in addition to the usual political and economic analysis. He also draws from various academic disciplines, including history, political science, psychology, anthropology, and economics, to present a more well-rounded and satisfying explanation of the state of affairs.

The history of the book concludes in 1964, marking the end of an era and a pause before a new era with different challenges. This shift is evident in events such as leadership changes in major countries and the waning of the Cold War. The period saw the emergence of competitive coexistence, the disintegration of super-blocs, the rise of neutralism, and a shift in global focus towards living standards, social issues, and mental health, replacing the previous emphasis on armaments, nuclear tensions, and industrialization.

Culture in Civilization

Civilization Life Cycles

Although people throughout history have wondered about the future, in the 20th century, these concerns seemed more pronounced, with a focus on whether human civilization can survive. Quigley gives a pattern of development that civilizations tend to follow:1

Birth and Expansion: A civilization starts and undergoes a period of rapid growth in population, geographic reach, production, and knowledge. This expansion often leads to the creation of core and peripheral regions within the civilization.

Crisis: After the expansion phase, a civilization may face a crisis that prompts it to reorganize. It may experience a decline in vigor and morale.

Stagnation: Following the crisis, a civilization stabilizes and can become stagnant. This period may include a Golden Age, but internal and external challenges emerge.

Conflict: The Age of Conflict is characterized by a declining rate of expansion, growing tensions, class conflicts, imperialist wars, and increased irrationality and pessimism. These issues usually arise in the core area of the civilization first.

Universal Empire: In most civilizations, the Age of Conflict eventually leads to the emergence of a Universal Empire, where one political unit conquers the entire civilization, either by conquering the core area or the entire civilization. The Universal Empire is often a peripheral state.

Life Cycle Examples

Western Civilization has played a role similar to that of invading forces in the past, like the Germanic tribes in Classical Civilization, the Dorians in Cretan Civilization, the Greeks in Mesopotamian or Egyptian Civilization, the Romans in Canaanite Civilization, or the Aryans in Indic Civilization. The Westerners who encountered the Aztecs in 1519, the Incas in 1534, the Mughal Empire in the 18th century, the Qing Dynasty after 1790, the Ottoman Empire after 1774, and the Tokugawa Empire after 1853 were essentially acting as disruptors, much like the Visigoths and other barbarian tribes to the Roman Empire after 377. In each case, when two civilizations collide, one in the Age of Expansion and the other in the Age of Decay, the outcome is predictable: Expansion overpowers Decay.

Over the past four centuries, much of Asia's periphery, which has been occupied by older, declining civilizations (Islamic, Hindu, Chinese, Japanese), has faced pressure from Western Civilization expanding from the oceans and from Orthodox Civilization advancing from the heart of Eurasia. Western Civilization's maritime influence started with Vasco da Gama's arrival in India in 1498, reached its climax aboard the battleship Missouri in Tokyo Bay in 1945, and continued with events like the Anglo-French attack on Suez in 1956. Meanwhile, Russian pressure from the continental heartland extended to the inner frontiers of countries like China, Iran, and Turkey from the 17th century to the present. The history of the twentieth century has been significantly shaped by the interactions of these three factors: the continental power of Russia, the struggling cultures of the Asian Buffer Fringe, and the oceanic strength of Western Civilization.

Western Civilization Life Cycle

Western Civilization has experienced three distinct periods of expansion, followed by three Ages of Conflict, marked by core area conquests and the emergence of new social structures. Each shift from conflict to expansion is characterized by changes in capital investment and accumulation. Western Civilization started as a blend of various cultural elements due to the collapse of Classical Civilization from 350 to 700. Over time, it organized itself and began accumulating capital, leading to changes in production methods, social organization, and economic emphasis.2

The first period of expansion, from 970 to 1270, was followed by an Age of Conflict from 1270 to 1420. Another Age of Expansion, focused on commercial capitalism, occurred from 1440 to about 1680. This eventually transformed into mercantilism, leading to economic decline. The second Age of Conflict, including class struggles and imperialist wars, continued until 1815. However, a new Age of Expansion began with industrial capitalism around 1820, which showed remarkable growth. By the late 19th century, industrial capitalism started becoming monopoly capitalism, leading to signs of a third Age of Conflict. By 1930, Western Civilization was in this new Age of Conflict, with imperial ambitions, class struggles, wars, and irrational behavior.

The outcome remains uncertain, as Western Civilization may either continue down a path of conflict or reorganize for a fourth Age of Expansion. In the former case, a Universal Empire may emerge, potentially led by the United States, followed by a period of decay and eventual collapse. In the latter case, Western Civilization may thrive and prosper.
