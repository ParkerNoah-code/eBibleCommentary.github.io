Lecture Notes on Plato's Philosophy

Lecture 6

Plato Today: His Resurgence in Modernity

In this concluding lecture, we examine the enduring relevance of Plato’s philosophy, particularly in the context of the last 400 years of intellectual history. The Enlightenment era, often characterized as a break from earlier traditions, marked the rise of skepticism towards metaphysical and religious frameworks. However, Plato’s ideas have continued to resurface, offering a counterpoint to materialism and relativism in modern thought.

The Enlightenment and Materialism

With thinkers like Thomas Hobbes, the Enlightenment began with a strong materialist perspective. Hobbes rejected notions of the soul, morality, and divine order, instead advocating for a mechanistic view of the universe and society. Human nature, according to Hobbes, required a powerful state to suppress inherent conflict, a view reminiscent of earlier Sophist skepticism. Despite the dominance of materialism, resistance emerged in the form of the Cambridge Platonists, who upheld the Platonic belief in an inner light and a transcendent moral order.

Three Resurrections of Plato

Plato’s philosophy finds renewed relevance in three key domains: mathematics, meaning, and myth.

Mathematics: The "unreasonable effectiveness" of mathematics in describing physical reality highlights the continued influence of Platonic ideas. Figures like Sir Roger Penrose argue for the existence of a distinct mathematical reality, suggesting a realm of timeless truths that transcends the material world. This view aligns with Plato’s assertion that mathematics provides a blueprint for understanding higher realities.

Meaning: Modern debates about meaning often emphasize individual or subjective constructions, a view at odds with the Platonic framework. Plato asserts that meaning is fixed, universal, and aligned with ultimate reality. For him, true meaning is discovered, not created, and must conform to the inherent structure of the cosmos.

Myth: Plato’s use of myth as a vehicle for philosophical truth underscores its enduring power. Myths, when aligned with Logos (reason), serve as conduits to higher realities. This approach influenced Western literature and allegory, exemplified in works like "The Faerie Queene" and "The Pilgrim’s Progress," where imaginative storytelling reveals deeper truths about existence.

Plato’s Countercultural Challenge

Plato’s ideas challenge modern tendencies toward relativism and radical individualism. He insists on the existence of objective truths and a hierarchical structure to reality. For Plato, freedom must be tempered by reason and aligned with the Good, a stance that critiques both anarchic freedom and authoritarian control.

Practical Applications

Plato’s methods emphasize collaborative inquiry and critical self-reflection. By engaging in dialogue and scrutinizing assumptions, individuals can move closer to understanding truth and meaning. This approach counters modern educational trends that prioritize rote learning over critical thinking, encouraging a deeper engagement with fundamental questions of existence.

Conclusion

Plato’s legacy endures because it addresses perennial human concerns about reality, morality, and meaning. His philosophy offers a path out of the "cave" of ignorance toward a deeper understanding of truth. As we grapple with the challenges of modernity, Plato’s insights remain a vital resource for navigating the complexities of knowledge and existence.
