Lecture Notes on Social Status

Lecture 5

Introduction to Stories and Status

Stories hold a unique place in human culture as they often reflect the dynamics of status, enabling audiences to empathize, learn, and connect. Brian Boyd, in his book "On the Origin of Stories," highlights how humans pursue status with fervor, often unconsciously evaluating and aiming to improve their own standing through narratives. The universal appeal of these stories lies in their depiction of the hero’s journey, a plotline explored deeply by Joseph Campbell and others, emphasizing the protagonist's growth from low to high status.

The Hero’s Journey

Joseph Campbell’s concept of the hero’s journey involves an ordinary protagonist transitioning into a special world through challenges and mentorship, ultimately emerging transformed. This narrative appeals universally because most people resonate with the idea of having untapped potential. Stories tap into the collective yearning to overcome adversity and attain greater status.

Sympathy for Protagonists and the Virtuous Victim Effect

Audiences sympathize with protagonists who either perform moral actions or endure mistreatment. This inclination is described as the virtuous victim effect, where suffering evokes perceptions of moral superiority. For instance, in the show "Breaking Bad," Walter White's initial struggles elicit sympathy, making his moral decline engaging. Such narratives align with the broader human tendency to root for underdogs, provided no immediate stakes are involved.

Creative Expression and Status

Creative storytelling itself serves as a status signal. Sigmund Freud noted that writers transform personal fantasies into relatable stories, achieving admiration and social recognition. Empirical studies confirm that published authors and artists often enjoy greater social and romantic success, not necessarily due to deliberate motives, but as a byproduct of evolutionary advantages tied to creativity.

Language as a Status Tool

Language evolved not only to convey information but also as a means to signal intelligence, social bonding, and cooperation. Robin Dunbar’s research equates small talk to primate grooming, emphasizing its role in relationship building. Public speaking, however, uniquely triggers anxiety due to ancestral associations with high-stakes social judgment.

Status Games

Will Storr categorizes status games into dominance, virtue, and success. Dominance relies on coercion and intimidation, virtue on moral signaling, and success on demonstrable achievements. While dominance games are often destructive, virtue games can foster social cohesion when moderated. Success games, tied to accomplishments, are deemed the most sustainable and impactful in modern societies.

Virtue and Victim Signaling

Virtue signaling, or moral grandstanding, is the use of moral discourse to enhance one’s image. This phenomenon transcends political ideologies, with extremists being the most prone to it. Victim signaling, on the other hand, involves broadcasting one’s hardships, often correlated with manipulative traits such as those in the Dark Triad. Societies must balance the need to support genuine victims while deterring exploitative behavior.

Success and Countersignaling

Success in status games often involves signaling competence and confidence. High-status individuals may engage in countersignaling, where they downplay traditional status markers to demonstrate their position. Examples include casual dress among executives or self-deprecating humor, which enhances likability when used by high-status individuals.

Conflict in Status Ambiguity

Conflict often arises among individuals of similar status due to uncertainty about hierarchy. Studies show that homicides and disputes are more frequent between equals than those with clear status disparities. This parallels animal behavior, where fights are more common between competitors of similar strength, underscoring the need for clear social roles to reduce tension.

Conclusion

Status dynamics, whether expressed through stories, creative output, or social interactions, profoundly influence human behavior. Understanding these patterns helps illuminate the motivations behind social hierarchies and conflicts, offering pathways to foster cooperation, empathy, and balance in both personal and societal contexts.
