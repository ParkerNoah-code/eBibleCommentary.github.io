Lecture Notes on Postmodern Philosophy

Lecture 7

Critical Feminist and Race Theory: Postmodern Perspectives

The year 1968, a pivotal moment in American and European history, marked cultural, political, and philosophical upheaval. The assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. not only disrupted a vital social and political movement but also symbolized a broader attack on philosophical ideals advocating equal liberty and the transcendence of racism. King, with a PhD in philosophy, represented both an activist and a philosophical approach to addressing societal injustices.

Philosophical Transformation in Law

Emerging from the backdrop of King's assassination, two young scholars began integrating postmodern ideas into legal studies, particularly through critical feminist and race theories. Drawing from thinkers like Foucault and Derrida, they challenged traditional Enlightenment values, critiquing the philosophical and institutional foundations of law. Their work initiated a transformation, highlighting the adversarial dynamics between groups and redefining the purpose and methodology of legal studies.

The Evolution of Feminist Philosophy

Feminist theory has evolved through three stages. In the premodern stage, women were systematically oppressed, with accusations of witchcraft and other measures enforcing their subjugation. The modern stage, influenced by Enlightenment ideals, saw first-wave feminism advocating for equality. Thinkers like Mary Wollstonecraft and Olympe de Gouges extended liberal individualist principles of liberty, equality, and property to women.

Second-wave feminism, epitomized by Simone de Beauvoir’s "The Second Sex," introduced existentialist and Marxist influences, shifting away from liberal individualism toward a collectivist, socialist framework. By the late 1960s, third-wave feminism began integrating postmodern themes, emphasizing social construction, adversarial dynamics, and the critique of normative standards.

Feminist Legal Critiques: Catherine MacKinnon

Catherine MacKinnon, a key figure in feminist legal theory, critiques liberal concepts of autonomy and free speech. She argues that pornography, protected under free speech laws, perpetuates systemic oppression by conditioning both men and women. MacKinnon challenges the traditional distinction between speech and action, proposing that pornography acts as a performative force shaping behaviors and social norms. Her advocacy for censorship redefines the debate, rejecting premodern and modern arguments in favor of postmodern critiques of power and social conditioning.

Critical Race Theory: Foundations and Shifts

Critical race theory emerged as a postmodern critique of traditional civil rights movements, which were rooted in Enlightenment ideals of incremental progress and universal equality. Thinkers like Derrick Bell and Kimberlé Crenshaw rejected these principles, questioning the foundations of liberal legal reasoning, equality theory, and constitutional neutrality. Their work emphasized the permanence of racial hierarchies and the systemic nature of oppression.

Derrick Bell’s Racial Realism

Derrick Bell's "Racial Realism" articulates a philosophical and activist strategy acknowledging the enduring subjugation of black individuals. He critiques the liberal legal framework as a facade perpetuating racial inequality. Bell argues for a realistic approach, rejecting idealistic goals of racial equality in favor of strategies like resistance and disruption to assert dignity within an oppressive system. His work represents a shift from Enlightenment ideals to a postmodern understanding of power, subjectivity, and systemic inequality.

The Postmodern Turn

Both critical feminist and race theories embody the postmodern rejection of Enlightenment principles such as universal reason, autonomy, and objective truth. They emphasize social constructs, power dynamics, and collective identity. These theories challenge the liberal individualist framework, advocating for systemic critiques and transformative activism aimed at addressing deep-seated social injustices.
