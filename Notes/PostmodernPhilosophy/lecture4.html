Lecture Notes on Postmodern Philosophy

Lecture 4

Women Ethicists and the Evolution of Moral Philosophy

The mid-20th century saw a transformative moment in philosophy, particularly in ethics, with the emergence of significant contributions by women philosophers. Figures like Ayn Rand, Philippa Foot, Mary Midgley, Elizabeth Anscombe, and Iris Murdoch reshaped the discourse by integrating abstract philosophy with real-world concerns. Their works emerged against the backdrop of World War II and its profound moral and existential implications, which influenced their approaches to foundational questions in ethics.

Naturalizing Ethics

A central theme in the works of Rand and Foot is the naturalization of ethics. They rejected the dichotomy between facts and values, arguing that ethics must be rooted in the biological and psychological realities of human beings. This approach stood in stark contrast to the dominant views in early 20th-century philosophy, which often relegated ethics to subjective, non-cognitive, or even meaningless domains.

Both philosophers built on Aristotelian foundations, emphasizing the integration of scientific insights from biology and psychology into ethical theory. They posited that values emerge from the needs and capacities of living organisms, making ethical principles objective and grounded in the realities of human existence.

Ayn Rand's Objectivist Ethics

Ayn Rand’s philosophy, Objectivism, introduced a relational understanding of values. Rand argued that ethics is a science, analogous to biology, where value judgments are rooted in the life-sustaining needs of organisms. For her, the ultimate value is life itself, and actions that support life are inherently good.

Rand emphasized the distinctiveness of human beings as rational agents. Unlike other animals, humans possess volitional consciousness and must consciously choose actions that align with their survival and flourishing. This perspective places responsibility on individuals to use reason as the basis for their values and decisions.

Philippa Foot's Natural Goodness

Philippa Foot's work extended similar principles within a framework of virtue ethics. In her 2001 book, "Natural Goodness," Foot argued that the evaluation of human actions depends on the essential features of specifically human life. She emphasized the importance of flourishing, a concept that integrates biological needs, capacities, and actions into a coherent standard of goodness.

Foot highlighted the continuity between human and non-human life forms in terms of evaluating actions. For example, just as a lioness is judged as a good mother when she teaches her cubs to hunt, human parents are evaluated based on their ability to nurture and educate their children. These evaluations, Foot argued, are objective, grounded in the nature of the species and the requirements for survival and flourishing.

The Revival of Aristotelian Ethics

Both Rand and Foot drew heavily on Aristotelian principles, particularly the idea that ethics involves identifying and fulfilling the essential functions of human beings. They updated Aristotle’s insights with modern scientific understanding, advocating for a reintegration of ethics with the natural and social sciences.

This revival of Aristotelian ethics marked a departure from the skepticism and subjectivism prevalent in the early 20th century. By linking moral philosophy to biology and psychology, Rand and Foot provided a robust framework for understanding human flourishing and virtue.

The Broader Implications

The works of Rand, Foot, and their contemporaries signaled a significant shift in ethical theory. They challenged the separation of facts and values, the rejection of objectivity in ethics, and the idea that moral principles are arbitrary or subjective. Their contributions opened new avenues for debates about the nature of ethics as a scientific and cognitive discipline, reshaping the landscape of moral philosophy for subsequent generations.
