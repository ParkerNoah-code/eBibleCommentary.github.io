Lecture Notes on Postmodern Philosophy

Lecture 3

Absurdity and Meaninglessness in the Context of War

World War II and the Holocaust brought unprecedented levels of brutality and existential despair. These events profoundly influenced existentialist thinkers Jean-Paul Sartre and Albert Camus, who sought to reconcile the absurdity of human suffering with the search for meaning. Their philosophies emerged against the backdrop of destruction, raising questions about God, morality, and the human condition.

Jean-Paul Sartre's Existentialism

Jean-Paul Sartre's philosophy centers on the idea that existence precedes essence, rejecting the notion of predetermined purposes for human beings. In "Existentialism Is a Humanism," Sartre argues that individuals are born as blank slates, responsible for creating their own identities and values through their actions. This freedom, however, comes with a heavy burden of responsibility.

Sartre emphasizes that human beings are condemned to be free, meaning they cannot escape the responsibility of choice. In the absence of divine guidance or objective morality, individuals must navigate life with anguish, abandonment, and despair. Sartre contrasts this with bad faith, where individuals shirk responsibility by blaming external forces for their choices.

Though existentialism is often criticized as nihilistic, Sartre defends it as a humanistic philosophy that encourages individuals to confront their freedom and responsibility. By acknowledging their role as legislators for humanity, individuals can create meaning in an otherwise absurd universe.

Albert Camus and the Absurd

Albert Camus explores similar themes in his work "The Myth of Sisyphus." Drawing from the Greek myth of Sisyphus, Camus illustrates the futility of life through the endless cycle of pushing a boulder up a hill only for it to roll back down. This struggle symbolizes the human condition: constant striving in the face of inevitable futility.

For Camus, the absurd arises from the conflict between humanity's desire for meaning and the universe's indifference. Rather than succumbing to despair, Camus advocates for embracing the absurd and finding joy in the struggle. By owning one's fate and committing to it, individuals can transform the absurd into a source of meaning.

Camus asserts that "one must imagine Sisyphus happy," highlighting the power of individual agency in creating meaning despite life's inherent pointlessness. He views the question of suicide as central to philosophy, framing it as a confrontation with the absurd and an opportunity to affirm life.

Existentialism's Individualism and Collectivism

Both Sartre and Camus emphasize individual freedom and responsibility, yet their philosophies also touch on collective responsibility. Sartre posits that personal choices implicitly legislate for all of humanity, challenging individuals to consider the broader implications of their actions. Similarly, Camus highlights solidarity as a means of confronting absurdity, suggesting that shared struggles can foster connection and meaning.

Despite their focus on individualism, both thinkers aligned politically with collectivist ideals, reflecting their belief in societal change and justice. This tension between existentialism's individualist philosophy and their political commitments remains a subject of debate and interpretation.

Confronting Absurdity

The existentialist response to the absurd is not despair but active engagement with life's challenges. Sartre's and Camus's works underscore the importance of personal responsibility, creative freedom, and resilience. Through these concepts, existentialism provides a framework for navigating a world devoid of inherent meaning, urging individuals to construct their own paths in the face of uncertainty.
