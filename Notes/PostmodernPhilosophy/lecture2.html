Lecture Notes on Postmodern Philosophy

Lecture 2

The Impact of World War I on Philosophy and the Self

The year 1916, amidst the devastation of World War I, marked a turning point in philosophical reflections on the human condition. The war's unprecedented slaughter shattered the illusion of progress and civilization, laying bare the darker elements of human nature. For Sigmund Freud and Martin Heidegger, the Great War offered profound insights into the human psyche and existential questions.

Freud's Civilization and Its Discontents

Sigmund Freud viewed the war as evidence of the aggressive instincts inherent in human nature. In "Civilization and Its Discontents," he argued that humans are driven by destructive and sexual instincts, regulated by the ego and societal norms. Freud's model of the psyche, comprising the id, ego, and superego, explained the internal conflicts between primal desires and the constraints of civilization.

Freud saw civilization as a necessary but repressive force that channels human instincts into socially acceptable forms. However, this repression leads to neuroses and discontent. Religion, in Freud's analysis, functions as a psychological crutch, rooted in childhood dependency on parental figures. He argued that while mature individuals might turn to science or art for meaning, religion remains essential for the majority who cannot overcome their dependency.

Heidegger's Existential Inquiry

Martin Heidegger, deeply affected by Germany's defeat in World War I, viewed the event as a philosophical and existential crisis. His reflections culminated in works like "Being and Time" and his essay "What is Metaphysics?" Heidegger rejected Enlightenment rationality, emphasizing the emotional and existential dimensions of human existence.

Heidegger's concept of Dasein, or "being-there," reframed human existence as fundamentally relational and situated within the world. He explored themes of anxiety, boredom, and the nothingness that underlies existence. For Heidegger, moments of profound anxiety reveal the contingency of being and the ultimate interconnectedness of being and nothingness.

Contrasting Freud and Heidegger

While Freud focused on the psychological mechanisms underpinning human behavior and civilization, Heidegger delved into the metaphysical and existential dimensions of human existence. Both thinkers, however, shared a skepticism toward traditional Enlightenment ideals of reason and progress. Freud highlighted the enduring power of irrational instincts, while Heidegger emphasized the primacy of existential emotions over rational thought.

Philosophy's Turn from Rationality

The works of Freud and Heidegger signaled a broader philosophical shift in the early 20th century. Rationality and progress, once cornerstones of Enlightenment thought, were increasingly challenged by explorations of the irrational, the emotional, and the existential. This marked a turning point in Western philosophy, as the optimistic narratives of the 18th and 19th centuries gave way to a more nuanced and often darker understanding of the human condition.
